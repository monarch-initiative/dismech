Autosomal Dominant Cerebellar Ataxia Type I

Mendelian MONDO:0019792 Pathograph 5 Show in embeddings browser Hereditary Ataxia Neurodegenerative Disease

Autosomal dominant cerebellar ataxia type I (ADCA-I) is Harding's clinical classification for hereditary ataxias in which a progressive cerebellar syndrome is accompanied by prominent extracerebellar features — pyramidal and extrapyramidal signs, ophthalmoplegia, peripheral neuropathy, cognitive decline, and brainstem involvement — distinguishing it from the pure-cerebellar ADCA-III pattern. ADCA-I is a genetically heterogeneous group whose most representative molecular subtypes are repeat-expansion spinocerebellar ataxias, including the polyglutamine disorders SCA1 (ATXN1), SCA2 (ATXN2), and SCA17 (TBP), the non-coding/repeat disorders SCA8 and SCA36, and SCA4. The shared pathological theme is a toxic gain-of-function from an expanded repeat tract (most often CAG-encoded polyglutamine) that drives misfolded-protein aggregation, transcriptional and proteostatic dysregulation, and progressive degeneration of cerebellar Purkinje cells together with brainstem, pyramidal-tract, and other extracerebellar systems. Onset is typically in adulthood with autosomal dominant transmission, frequent anticipation, and an inverse correlation between expanded-repeat length and age at onset. No disease-modifying therapy is established; management is symptomatic and supportive.

Subtypes

6
Spinocerebellar ataxia type 1 (ATXN1) MONDO:0008119
Polyglutamine cerebellar ataxia caused by a CAG repeat expansion in ATXN1 encoding ataxin-1. The phenotype combines progressive cerebellar ataxia with prominent extracerebellar features — pyramidal signs, peripheral neuropathy, and brainstem involvement — that are characteristic of the ADCA-I class. Anticipation is pronounced, especially with paternal transmission.
Spinocerebellar ataxia type 2 (ATXN2) MONDO:0008458
Polyglutamine cerebellar ataxia caused by a CAG repeat expansion in ATXN2 encoding ataxin-2. Slow saccades, peripheral neuropathy, and sometimes parkinsonism accompany the cerebellar syndrome. Intermediate-length ATXN2 expansions are also a recognized risk factor for amyotrophic lateral sclerosis.
Spinocerebellar ataxia type 4 (ZFHX3) MONDO:0010847
Adult-onset cerebellar ataxia with prominent sensory axonal neuropathy and pyramidal signs, mapped to a GGC repeat expansion in ZFHX3. The combination of cerebellar and prominent extracerebellar (sensory neuropathy, pyramidal) involvement places SCA4 within the ADCA-I class.
Spinocerebellar ataxia type 8 (ATXN8/ATXN8OS) MONDO:0012116
Slowly progressive cerebellar ataxia caused by a bidirectionally transcribed CTG/CAG repeat expansion at the ATXN8OS/ATXN8 locus, combining RNA gain-of-function toxicity with production of a polyglutamine ATXN8 product. Reduced penetrance and a wide normal/ pathogenic overlap make genotype interpretation complex.
Spinocerebellar ataxia type 17 (TBP) MONDO:0011781
Polyglutamine disorder caused by a CAG/CAA repeat expansion in TBP (TATA-box binding protein). The syndrome combines cerebellar ataxia with cognitive decline, psychiatric symptoms, and extrapyramidal movement disorders (chorea, dystonia, parkinsonism), and is also termed Huntington disease-like 4. A fully curated standalone entry exists at kb/disorders/Spinocerebellar_Ataxia_Type_17.yaml; consolidation of that content under this parent is flagged for human review (see issue #1675).
Spinocerebellar ataxia type 36 (NOP56) MONDO:0013594
Late-onset cerebellar ataxia caused by an intronic GGCCTG hexanucleotide repeat expansion in NOP56, characteristically combining cerebellar degeneration with motor neuron involvement (tongue and skeletal-muscle fasciculations, amyotrophy) — an extracerebellar feature consistent with the ADCA-I class. Also known as Asidan.

Pathophysiology

5
Repeat Expansion and Polyglutamine Toxic Gain-of-Function
The unifying molecular lesion across ADCA-I subtypes is an unstable expanded repeat that confers a toxic gain-of-function. In the polyglutamine subtypes (SCA1/ATXN1, SCA2/ATXN2, SCA17/TBP) a CAG expansion encodes an elongated polyglutamine tract; in SCA8 a bidirectionally transcribed CTG/CAG expansion combines RNA toxicity with a polyglutamine product; and in SCA36 a non-coding GGCCTG hexanucleotide expansion drives RNA-mediated toxicity. Repeat instability during meiosis underlies anticipation and the inverse correlation between repeat length and age at onset.

Misfolded Protein Aggregation and Proteostatic Stress
Expanded polyglutamine ataxin-1, ataxin-2, and TBP adopt aberrant conformations and form nuclear and cytoplasmic inclusions. Sequestration of chaperones, ubiquitin-proteasome and autophagy components, and interacting partners progressively impairs neuronal proteostasis, contributing to Purkinje-cell and brainstem-neuron dysfunction. This proteostatic stress is a convergent downstream consequence of the polyglutamine subtypes and is described mechanistically here without an independent ADCA-I-specific quantitative claim.

Transcriptional Dysregulation
Several ADCA-I proteins act in the nucleus: TBP is a core general transcription factor and expanded ataxin-1 reorganizes native transcriptional regulator complexes (e.g., with CIC and RBM17), skewing gene-expression programs required for Purkinje-cell homeostasis. Transcriptional dysregulation is a shared nuclear mechanism among the polyglutamine ADCA-I subtypes; subtype-specific molecular detail is left for follow-up curation of the individual subtype entries.

PMID:30532692 SUPPORT Human Clinical
"neurodegenerative disease caused by a CAG repeat expansion in the TATA-box binding protein gene (TBP)"
In the representative ADCA-I subtype SCA17 the causative gene is TBP, the TATA-box binding protein — the general RNA polymerase II transcription initiation factor. A polyglutamine-encoding CAG expansion in TBP places the disease lesion directly within the core transcription machinery, mechanistically grounding transcriptional dysregulation as a shared nuclear ADCA-I mechanism.
Purkinje Cell Degeneration and Cerebellar Cortical Atrophy
The convergent pathological endpoint across ADCA-I subtypes is progressive degeneration of cerebellar Purkinje cells with loss of the cerebellar cortical architecture, producing the progressive gait and limb ataxia, dysarthria, and oculomotor abnormalities that define the syndrome.

Extracerebellar Neurodegeneration
The defining distinction of ADCA-I from the pure-cerebellar ADCA-III class is involvement of extracerebellar systems: brainstem and pontine nuclei, the pyramidal tracts, basal ganglia (extrapyramidal signs), peripheral sensory and motor nerves, and — in some subtypes — cognitive networks. The specific extracerebellar burden varies by subtype (e.g., motor-neuron involvement in SCA36; cognitive/psychiatric and extrapyramidal features in SCA17; peripheral neuropathy in SCA2/SCA4).

Core Conclusion

ADCA-I is best modeled as a parent-level clinical/mechanistic class. The existing YAML scaffold is conceptually aligned with the literature: it centers the class on autosomal dominant repeat-expansion SCAs with progressive cerebellar degeneration plus extracerebellar involvement. It should remain a parent scaffold until subtype-level entries can carry detailed repeat thresholds, phenotype frequencies, diagnostic nuances, and treatment/surveillance detail.

The current PR's minimal shared pathophysiology - repeat expansion, misfolded protein/proteostatic stress, transcriptional dysregulation, Purkinje-cell degeneration, and extracerebellar neurodegeneration - is supported by the literature. SCA4 should be treated as a recently solved ZFHX3 GGC-repeat / poly-glycine disease, not as an unresolved 16q locus, because multiple 2024 papers and the 2024 GeneReviews entry now support the assignment.

Curation-Relevant Findings

Nosology and inheritance

Harding's 1982 clinical classification remains the source of the ADCA-I/II/III clinical grouping. The Harding paper proposed "a simple classification of the autosomal dominant cerebellar ataxias" and described extracerebellar associated features including "dementia, supranuclear ophthalmoplegia, extrapyramidal dysfunction" (PMID:7066668). A companion genetic paper supports autosomal dominant segregation and counseling logic (PMID:7334501).

For the PR scaffold, the parent should not imply one founder mutation or one uniform phenotype. It should state autosomal dominant inheritance, repeat instability/anticipation where applicable, and reduced penetrance in specific subtypes such as SCA8 and lower-range SCA17 alleles.

Shared repeat-expansion mechanism

The broader polyglutamine/repeat-expansion literature supports a convergent mechanism across SCA1, SCA2, SCA17, and related SCAs. Stoyas and La Spada list SCA1, SCA2, and SCA17 among the CAG-polyglutamine disease family and identify "protein aggregation" and "transcription dysregulation" among recurring pathologic processes (PMID:29325609). Orr and Zoghbi similarly frame unstable repeat expansion as a cause of inherited ataxias and emphasize altered protein conformation in neuronal survival (PMID:17417937).

More explicit SCA1 work supports transcriptional dysregulation as a strong follow-up evidence target: PMID:20628574 reports shared gene-expression changes in SCA1 model cerebella, PMID:32964235 links regional Purkinje-neuron vulnerability to ion-channel gene dysregulation, and PMID:32306062 summarizes SCA mechanisms around "ion channel dysfunction and transcriptional dysregulation."

Phenotypes

The parent-level phenotype block should stay conservative. Progressive cerebellar ataxia and dysarthria are safe parent-level features. Additional parent-level candidates for later curation include cerebellar atrophy, abnormal saccadic eye movements, pyramidal signs/spasticity, dysphagia, peripheral neuropathy, extrapyramidal signs, cognitive impairment, psychiatric symptoms, and sensorineural hearing loss. These should preferably be subtype-scoped rather than asserted uniformly at the parent level.

Diagnosis

The literature consistently supports a clinical-neurologic suspicion followed by molecular repeat-expansion testing:

  • SCA1 GeneReviews: diagnosis by characteristic clinical findings and abnormal ATXN1 CAG expansion (PMID:20301363).
  • SCA2 GeneReviews: molecular genetic testing for abnormal ATXN2 CAG expansion (PMID:20301452).
  • SCA4 GeneReviews: heterozygous abnormal ZFHX3 GGC expansion (PMID:39666847).
  • SCA8 GeneReviews: heterozygous abnormal ATXN8OS/ATXN8 repeat expansion (PMID:20301445).
  • SCA17/SCA36: repeat-primed PCR, Southern blot, long-read sequencing, or targeted repeat analysis may be needed depending on the locus and local test availability (PMID:30532692, PMID:22492559, PMID:37810464).

Parent YAML can mention molecular repeat-expansion testing in a future diagnosis section, but subtype entries should carry locus-specific thresholds and methodology.

Management and treatments

No disease-modifying therapy is established for the ADCA-I parent class. The most defensible treatment frame is supportive, multidisciplinary management: physical therapy, occupational therapy, speech-language therapy, fall prevention/adaptive devices, swallowing assessment for dysphagia, nutrition support, psychiatric/neuropsychologic support when needed, avoidance of alcohol or medications that worsen cerebellar function, and genetic counseling.

SCA1, SCA2, SCA4, and SCA8 GeneReviews each support supportive care and surveillance rather than curative treatment (PMID:20301363, PMID:20301452, PMID:39666847, PMID:20301445). SCA36 review literature discusses emerging mechanistic therapeutic directions but does not establish an accepted disease-modifying treatment (PMID:35309140).

Prevalence

Do not add a single global parent prevalence for ADCA-I. The term is a historical clinical class, and prevalence varies by subtype and founder population. SCA36 is enriched in specific Japanese, Galician/Spanish, and British cohorts (PMID:22492559, PMID:22753339, PMID:37810464); SCA17 fully penetrant alleles were rare in the UK ataxia cohort (PMID:30532692); and SCA8 frequency varies with testing strategy, repeat thresholds, and penetrance interpretation (PMID:20301445, PMID:31471687). Prevalence should be subtype-scoped.
