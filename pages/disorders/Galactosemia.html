Galactosemia

Genetic MONDO:0018116 Pathograph 16 Inborn error of carbohydrate metabolism Newborn screening disorder

Galactosemia is an inborn error of galactose metabolism caused by deficiency of one of three enzymes in the Leloir pathway: galactose-1-phosphate uridyltransferase (GALT, classic galactosemia), galactokinase (GALK), or UDP-galactose-4-epimerase (GALE). Classic galactosemia (GALT deficiency) is the most severe form, presenting in neonates with feeding difficulties, failure to thrive, hepatomegaly, jaundice, and increased susceptibility to E. coli sepsis. Early dietary restriction of galactose prevents acute toxicity but long-term complications including cognitive impairment, speech disorders, and premature ovarian insufficiency may still occur.

Pathophysiology

9
GALT Deficiency in the Leloir Pathway
In classic galactosemia, deficient galactose-1-phosphate uridylyltransferase activity disrupts the core uridylyltransferase step of galactose metabolism.
Show evidence (1 reference)
PMID:33525536 SUPPORT Human Clinical
"Type I (classic) galactosemia, galactose 1-phosphate uridylyltransferase (GALT)-deficiency is a hereditary disorder of galactose metabolism."
This review identifies classic galactosemia as a disorder defined by GALT deficiency in galactose metabolism.
Galactose-1-phosphate accumulation
Galactose-1-phosphate accumulates to high intracellular levels in classic galactosemia and is an important contributor to downstream metabolic toxicity, but not the sole pathogenic factor.
Show evidence (2 references)
PMID:31774565 SUPPORT In Vitro
"One of the main alterations caused by GALT deficiency is the accumulation of galactose 1-phosphate (Gal-1P) in cells."
This directly supports Gal-1-P accumulation as a core biochemical abnormality in classic galactosemia.
PMID:31808946 SUPPORT Other
"Results revealed that Gal-1-P is not the sole pathophysiological agent responsible for the phenotype observed in galactosemia."
This systematic review clarifies that Gal-1-P accumulation is central but insufficient on its own to explain the full phenotype.
UDP-hexose depletion
GALT deficiency and Gal-1-P accumulation reduce intracellular UDP-hexose pools, especially UDP-galactose and related sugar donors.
Show evidence (1 reference)
PMID:12626383 SUPPORT In Vitro
"intracellular concentrations of Gal-1-P found in classic galactosemia inhibit UDP-hexose pyrophosphorylases and reduce the intracellular concentrations of UDP-hexoses"
This study directly supports depletion of intracellular UDP-hexose pools in galactosemic cells.
Impaired Glycosylation
Abnormal glycosylation is a downstream consequence of disturbed UDP-hexose homeostasis and is thought to contribute to persistent neurologic and ovarian complications.
Show evidence (2 references)
PMID:7671968 SUPPORT Human Clinical
"There is circumstantial evidence that defective galactosylation of complex glycoconjugates exists in tissues from galactosemic patients."
This review supports defective galactosylation in patient tissues.
PMID:31808946 SUPPORT Other
"Other currently described contributing factors include accumulation of galactose metabolites, uridine diphosphate (UDP)-hexose alterations and subsequent impaired glycosylation"
This systematic review explicitly places impaired glycosylation downstream of UDP-hexose alterations.
Galactitol accumulation
Excess galactose can be reduced to galactitol, which accumulates in the lens and causes osmotic injury. This is the dominant mechanism in galactokinase deficiency and also contributes to cataract risk in galactosemia more broadly.
Show evidence (1 reference)
PMID:3043741 SUPPORT Human Clinical
"Any single deficient enzyme can result in cataract through the accumulation of galactitol in the lens."
This review directly identifies galactitol accumulation as the mechanism of cataract formation.
Endoplasmic Reticulum Stress
Endoplasmic reticulum stress is an additional downstream mechanism implicated in galactosemia beyond Gal-1-P accumulation alone.

This systematic review identifies ER stress as a distinct mechanistic contributor in galactosemia.

Treatments

4
Galactose-restricted diet MAXO:0000088
Lifelong dietary restriction of galactose is the cornerstone of treatment. Lactose-containing foods (milk, dairy) are eliminated and replaced with soy-based or elemental formulas in infancy.
Show evidence (1 reference)
PMID:20978943 SUPPORT Human Clinical
"more than 80% and perhaps more than 90% are affected despite neonatal diagnosis and careful lifelong dietary restriction of galactose"
Review confirming that dietary restriction of galactose is the standard treatment, though long-term complications persist.
Calcium and vitamin D supplementation
Supplementation is necessary due to dairy restriction to prevent osteoporosis.
Show evidence (1 reference)
PMID:16782422 PARTIAL Human Clinical
"Supplementation of calcium, vitamins K(1) and D(3) given in this dose (750 mg, 1.0 mg and 10.0 mug respectively) is likely to have a role in the treatment of BMC abnormalities in galactosemia."
This randomized clinical trial supports calcium and vitamin D supplementation as part of bone-health management in galactosemia, although the studied regimen also included vitamin K1.
Hormone replacement therapy
Estrogen and progesterone replacement for females with premature ovarian insufficiency.
Show evidence (1 reference)
PMID:35118398 PARTIAL Human Clinical
"This means that in addition to the consequences of the disease, these women also face infertility and the potential need for hormone replacement therapy."
This POI management review supports hormone replacement therapy as a recognized part of care for girls and women with classic galactosemia-associated ovarian insufficiency.
Speech therapy MAXO:0000930
Early intervention for speech and language delays.
Show evidence (1 reference)
PMID:34665663 SUPPORT Human Clinical
"The BBC appears to be a viable intervention to support the speech and expressive language development of children with GC."
This intervention study provides direct human evidence that targeted speech-language therapy can support speech and language development in children with classic galactosemia.

Biochemical Markers

3
Erythrocyte galactose-1-phosphate (INCREASED)
Context: Elevated erythrocyte galactose-1-phosphate is a core biochemical marker of classic galactosemia; in classic disease it is typically above 10 mg/dL.
Show evidence (1 reference)
PMID:20301691 SUPPORT Human Clinical
"In classic galactosemia, erythrocyte galactose-1-phosphate is usually >10 mg/dL and erythrocyte GALT enzyme activity is absent or barely detectable."
GeneReviews identifies elevated erythrocyte Gal-1-P as a defining biochemical abnormality in classic galactosemia.
Erythrocyte GALT enzyme activity (DECREASED_OR_ABSENT)
Context: Erythrocyte GALT activity is absent or barely detectable in classic galactosemia and remains the key enzyme marker used in diagnosis.
Show evidence (2 references)
PMID:20301691 SUPPORT Human Clinical
"In classic galactosemia, erythrocyte galactose-1-phosphate is usually >10 mg/dL and erythrocyte GALT enzyme activity is absent or barely detectable."
This directly supports markedly reduced or absent erythrocyte GALT activity as a core biochemical finding.
PMID:16838075 SUPPORT Human Clinical
"The gold standard for diagnosis of classical galactosaemia is measurement of GALT activity in erythrocytes."
This review confirms erythrocyte GALT activity as the principal biochemical assay for classical galactosemia.
Urinary galactitol (INCREASED)
Context: Urinary galactitol reflects diversion of excess galactose into the aldose reductase pathway and can support biochemical diagnosis and monitoring.
Show evidence (1 reference)
PMID:16838075 SUPPORT Human Clinical
"Gas-chromatographic determination of urinary sugars and sugar alcohols demonstrates elevated concentrations of galactose and galactitol."
This directly supports urinary galactitol elevation as a biochemical marker in classical galactosemia.
