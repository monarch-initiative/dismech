Glycogen Storage Disease Type I

Mendelian MONDO:0002413 Glycogen Storage Disease Inborn Error of Carbohydrate Metabolism

Glycogen storage disease type I (GSD I), also known as von Gierke disease, is an autosomal recessive disorder of glucose metabolism caused by deficiency of the glucose-6-phosphatase system. GSD Ia results from mutations in G6PC1 encoding the catalytic subunit glucose-6-phosphatase-alpha, while GSD Ib results from mutations in SLC37A4 encoding the glucose-6-phosphate transporter (G6PT). Both subtypes lead to impaired hydrolysis of glucose-6-phosphate to glucose, blocking the final step of both glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis, resulting in fasting hypoglycemia, hepatomegaly, nephromegaly, lactic acidosis, hyperlipidemia, and hyperuricemia. GSD Ib additionally features neutropenia and neutrophil dysfunction leading to recurrent infections and inflammatory bowel disease.

Subtypes

2
GSD Ia (glucose-6-phosphatase deficiency)
Caused by biallelic pathogenic variants in G6PC1, encoding the catalytic subunit glucose-6-phosphatase-alpha expressed in liver, kidney, and intestine. GSD Ia accounts for approximately 80% of GSD I cases and presents with fasting hypoglycemia, hepatomegaly, growth retardation, lactic acidosis, hyperlipidemia, and hyperuricemia. Unlike GSD Ib, neutropenia is not a feature.
GSD Ib (glucose-6-phosphate transporter deficiency)
Caused by biallelic pathogenic variants in SLC37A4, encoding the ubiquitously expressed glucose-6-phosphate transporter (G6PT). GSD Ib accounts for approximately 20% of GSD I cases. In addition to the metabolic phenotype shared with GSD Ia, patients with GSD Ib develop neutropenia, neutrophil dysfunction, and inflammatory bowel disease due to the role of G6PT in neutrophil energy homeostasis.

Pathophysiology

4
Impaired glucose-6-phosphate hydrolysis and fasting hypoglycemia
Deficiency of the glucose-6-phosphatase system blocks the final step of both glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis, preventing the liver and kidneys from releasing free glucose into the bloodstream during fasting. Glucose-6-phosphate accumulates in the cytoplasm of hepatocytes and renal tubular cells, driving excessive glycogen and fat synthesis. The inability to maintain blood glucose between meals causes severe fasting hypoglycemia, which is the hallmark metabolic derangement of GSD I.

Secondary metabolic derangements
The block in glucose-6-phosphate hydrolysis leads to accumulation of metabolic intermediates, resulting in several secondary biochemical abnormalities. Excess glucose-6-phosphate is shunted into glycolysis, producing excess pyruvate and lactate (lactic acidosis). Increased acetyl-CoA flux drives de novo lipogenesis, causing hypertriglyceridemia and hypercholesterolemia. Accelerated purine degradation from excess glucose-6-phosphate metabolism, combined with decreased renal urate clearance due to competitive inhibition by lactate, leads to hyperuricemia.

Neutropenia and neutrophil dysfunction in GSD Ib
In GSD Ib, deficiency of the ubiquitously expressed glucose-6-phosphate transporter (G6PT) impairs neutrophil energy homeostasis. Neutrophils express the G6PT/G6Pase-beta complex, which produces endogenous glucose for neutrophil function. Loss of G6PT activity leads to enhanced endoplasmic reticulum stress, oxidative stress, and apoptosis, underlying the neutropenia and neutrophil dysfunction characteristic of GSD Ib. This manifests as recurrent bacterial infections, oral and intestinal ulcers, and inflammatory bowel disease.

Hepatocellular adenoma development
Hepatocellular adenomas are a major long-term complication of GSD I, occurring in 22-75% of affected adults. The adenomas develop typically in post-pubertal patients and carry an approximately 10% risk of malignant transformation to hepatocellular carcinoma. The pathogenesis is multifactorial and may involve chronic metabolic stress, altered hepatocyte growth signaling, and hormonal influences. Better dietary management and metabolic control appear to reduce adenoma incidence.

Treatments

11
Uncooked cornstarch therapy MAXO:0000088
Dietary management with frequent meals of uncooked cornstarch (a slow-release glucose polymer) is the cornerstone of GSD I treatment. Cornstarch provides a sustained source of glucose to prevent fasting hypoglycemia. Patients require multiple doses per day including overnight. Combined with avoidance of fructose, galactose, and sucrose. Optimal metabolic control reduces long-term complications.

PMID:12373568 SUPPORT Human Clinical
"The achievement of optimal biochemical control of glycogen storage disease type 1a continues to be a challenge, but is attainable by meticulous adherence to an individualized dietary regimen based on the results of periodic metabolic evaluation and home blood glucose monitoring."
Long-term study demonstrates that cornstarch-based dietary therapy can achieve metabolic control, though it remains challenging.

    free glucose into the bloodstream during fasting. Glucose-6-phosphate accumulates
    in the cytoplasm of hepatocytes and renal tubular cells, driving excessive glycogen
    and fat synthesis. The inability to maintain blood glucose between meals causes
    severe fasting hypoglycemia, which is the hallmark metabolic derangement of GSD I.

- name: Secondary metabolic derangements
  description: >
    The block in glucose-6-phosphate hydrolysis leads to accumulation of metabolic
    intermediates, resulting in several secondary biochemical abnormalities. Excess
    glucose-6-phosphate is shunted into glycolysis, producing excess pyruvate and
    lactate (lactic acidosis). Increased acetyl-CoA flux drives de novo lipogenesis,
    causing hypertriglyceridemia and hypercholesterolemia. Accelerated purine
    degradation from excess glucose-6-phosphate metabolism, combined with decreased
    renal urate clearance due to competitive inhibition by lactate, leads to
    hyperuricemia.

- name: Neutropenia and neutrophil dysfunction in GSD Ib
  description: >
    In GSD Ib, deficiency of the ubiquitously expressed glucose-6-phosphate
    transporter (G6PT) impairs neutrophil energy homeostasis. Neutrophils express
