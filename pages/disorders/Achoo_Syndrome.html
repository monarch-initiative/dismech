Pathophysiology

5
Optic-Trigeminal Cross-Activation
Abrupt luminance changes activate visual pathways that cross-activate trigeminal and autonomic circuits, leading to nasal prickle and sneezing in susceptible individuals.

PMID:31287245 SUPPORT
"The optic‐trigeminal summation hypothesis postulates that, during light stimulation, the activated optic nerve pathways cross‐activate the trigeminal nerve pathways"
Cross-activation between visual and trigeminal pathways is a leading hypothesis.
PMID:39893297 SUPPORT
"Several theories, including optic-trigeminal summation, parasympathetic hypersensitivity, and parasympathetic generalization, have been proposed, but none have been conclusively validated."
2025 mini-review confirms optic-trigeminal summation remains a leading but unvalidated hypothesis.

"Neuropharmacological alterations associated with photoperiod mechanisms leading to optic trigeminal summation are suggested as possible reasons for spring onset of headshaking."
Equine photic headshaking provides a natural animal model supporting optic-trigeminal summation. Light-triggered headshaking ceased with blindfolding in 5/5 horses.

Trigeminal Melanopsin Photosensitivity
Melanopsin-expressing trigeminal ganglion neurons can directly detect light, providing a potential retina-independent pathway for photic sneezing. These neurons represent approximately 3% of small trigeminal ganglion neurons, are localized to the ophthalmic branch, and respond to blue light with sustained firing similar to intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells.

Establishes a light detection pathway entirely independent of the optic nerve that could bypass the retina-to-trigeminal cross-activation route.
Brainstem Sneeze Circuit
The sneeze reflex is mediated by neuromedin B (NMB) signaling from nasal sensory neurons to NMB receptor-expressing neurons in the sneeze-evoking region of the brainstem, which project to the caudal ventral respiratory group to execute the sneeze motor pattern.

Environmental Factors

1
Bright Light Exposure
Intensity, spectral composition, duration, and timing are potentially important stimulus parameters but systematic studies are lacking.
Sudden exposure to bright light, particularly sunlight, is the triggering stimulus. The reflex habituates with sustained exposure.
Show evidence (3 references)
PMID:31287245 SUPPORT
"Photic sneeze syndrome (PSS) is a condition that causes sneezing when the eye is exposed to sudden bright light."
Light is the essential triggering environmental factor.
PMID:39893297 SUPPORT
"No study has yet examined the parametric relationship between light parameters and the PSR."
A 2025 systematic mini-review confirms that specific light parameters (intensity, wavelength, duration) triggering the reflex remain undefined despite the phenomenon being well-documented.
PMID:8536668 SUPPORT Model Organism
"Cessation of headshaking was observed with blindfolding (5/5 horses), night darkness outdoors (4/4 horses) and use of grey lenses (2/3 horses)."
Equine photic headshaking confirms the causal role of light, with cessation upon light removal in a natural animal model of trigeminal-mediated light sensitivity.

notes: |
  ACHOO syndrome is a benign, common trait affecting approximately 18-35% of the
  population depending on study methodology. Prevalence varies significantly across
  populations (25.6% Chinese, 3.1% Japanese) which may reflect survey methodology
  differences. The condition has occupational/safety relevance for drivers exiting
  tunnels and pilots during abrupt light transitions. The acronym stands for
  Autosomal Dominant Compelling Helioophthalmic Outburst. First described by Collie
  et al. in 1978 (PMID:728575), though observed since Aristotle. The photic sneeze
  is not a classical brainstem reflex but involves cortical processing areas.
  Multiple mechanistic hypotheses remain under investigation, and systematic
  parametric studies of stimulus conditions are still needed. A 2025 systematic
  mini-review (PMID:39893297) confirmed that none of the three leading theories
  (optic-trigeminal summation, parasympathetic hypersensitivity, parasympathetic
  generalization) have been conclusively validated. The discovery of melanopsin
  expression in trigeminal ganglion neurons (PMID:27559310) raises the possibility
  of a direct, retina-independent light detection pathway contributing to photic
  sneezing. Equine photic headshaking provides a natural animal model supporting
  trigeminal nerve involvement, with electrophysiological evidence of
  hyperexcitability not yet demonstrated in humans.
