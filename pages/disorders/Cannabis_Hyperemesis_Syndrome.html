Cannabis Hyperemesis Syndrome

Complex MONDO:0100094 Pathograph 9 Gastrointestinal Disease Substance-Related Disorder

Cannabis Hyperemesis Syndrome (CHS) is a paradoxical disorder characterized by cyclic nausea and vomiting in chronic cannabis users. Despite cannabis's traditionally antiemetic properties, prolonged use leads to severe emesis episodes that are characteristically relieved by hot showers or baths. The syndrome results from chronic overstimulation and dysregulation of cannabinoid receptors, particularly CB1 receptors in the central nervous system and gastrointestinal tract.

Pathophysiology

6
CB1 Receptor Desensitization
Chronic cannabis use leads to prolonged activation of cannabinoid CB1 receptors, resulting in receptor desensitization and/or downregulation. This disrupts the endocannabinoid system's normal antiemetic function and paradoxically leads to pro-emetic effects. The brain's emetic control centers become dysregulated, particularly in the area postrema and dorsal vagal complex.

HPA Axis Disruption
The endocannabinoid system normally exerts negative feedback on the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis. Chronic cannabis use disrupts this feedback mechanism, leading to increased vagal nerve discharge and enhanced emetic responses. Stress states activate the sympathetic nervous system and increase ACTH release, which mobilizes THC stored in adipose tissue.

Increased Vagal Nerve Discharge
Loss of endocannabinoid negative feedback is proposed to increase vagal nerve signaling to emetic control centers, amplifying nausea and vomiting during CHS episodes.
THC Mobilization from Adipose Tissue
During stress states and food deprivation, sympathetic nervous system activation increases lipolysis through ACTH action on adipocyte receptors. This releases THC stored in fat tissue, creating a positive feedback loop that perpetuates emetic episodes during stress or when patients stop eating due to nausea.

Gastrointestinal Dysmotility
CB1 receptors in the gastrointestinal tract normally regulate gut motility. Chronic cannabis exposure leads to inhibition of gastric acid secretion, delayed gastric emptying, and reduced intestinal motility. This contributes to abdominal pain and nausea, creating a cycle where the symptoms worsen with continued cannabis use.

TRPV1 Receptor Interactions
The transient receptor potential vanilloid-1 (TRPV1) receptors interact with the endocannabinoid system and may be involved in the characteristic relief patients experience with hot showers. Heat exposure activates TRPV1 receptors on sensory nerve endings, potentially modulating pain perception and providing symptomatic relief through vasodilation and neural pathway modulation.
