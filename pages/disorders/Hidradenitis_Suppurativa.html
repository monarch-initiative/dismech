Pathophysiology

9
Follicular Hyperkeratosis
Aberrant keratinization of the hair follicle infundibulum leads to follicular plugging. Hyperproliferative keratinocytes produce excess keratin that occludes the follicular ostium. This is the initiating event in HS pathogenesis.

This landmark paper identifies HS as a disease of hair follicles, consistent with follicular occlusion as the primary pathological event.
Follicular Rupture
Continued keratinization causes follicular dilation and eventual mechanical rupture, releasing keratin, commensal bacteria, and damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) into the surrounding dermis. This triggers the innate immune response.
Show evidence (1 reference)
PMID:34409324 SUPPORT Human Clinical

Establishes follicular rupture as a key pathogenic event in HS, releasing keratin and DAMPs into the dermis.
Innate Immune Hyperactivation
Following follicular rupture, DAMPs and PAMPs activate TLR2 and inflammasome pathways (NLRP3/CASP1). This drives IL-1beta and IL-36 release from keratinocytes and myeloid cells, recruiting neutrophils and macrophages to form the early inflammatory infiltrate and abscess.

Epigenetic Reprogramming of Keratinocytes
HS basal progenitor keratinocytes undergo epigenetic rewiring with extensive chromatin remodeling (1,434 gained / 3,722 lost ATAC peaks) that primes inflammatory gene programs. This produces pathogenic keratinocyte clones marked by S100A7/8/9 and KRT6 that trigger IL-1, IL-10, and complement cascades. This represents an epithelial-intrinsic disease driver.

Tertiary Lymphoid Structure Formation
In chronic HS lesions, organized tertiary lymphoid structures (TLSs) form near epithelialized tunnels. These TLSs contain proliferative T cells (including Tfh, Treg, and IL-17A+/IFNG+ pathogenic T cells) and clonally expanded plasma cells producing anti-keratinocyte autoantibodies. HS fibroblasts expressing CXCL13 or CCL19 orchestrate lymphocyte aggregation via TNF-alpha feedback loops.

Sinus Tract Formation
Chronic inflammation and recurrent abscess cycles lead to tissue destruction and formation of epithelialized sinus tracts (tunnels) that grow deeply into the dermis. These tracts serve as persistent inflammatory niches and sites of TLS formation, perpetuating the disease cycle.

Conditions with similar clinical presentations that must be differentiated from Hidradenitis Suppurativa:

Furuncle/Carbuncle MONDO:0100595
Overlapping Features Staphylococcal infection of hair follicles producing painful abscesses, but typically isolated events without recurrence in apocrine-bearing areas.
Distinguishing Features
  • Single or few lesions without sinus tract formation
  • Positive bacterial culture (S. aureus)
  • No comedones or chronic scarring pattern
  • Does not recur in stereotypical intertriginous distribution
Show evidence (1 reference)
PMID:40364202 SUPPORT Human Clinical
"In a Hurley II genital case in a male patient, only 34.5% diagnosed HS, while 25.65% suggested furunculosis and 16.18% venereal granuloma. For a Hurley I genital case in a female patient, 29.92% diagnosed HS, with furunculosis (23.36%) and steatocystoma multiplex (14.35%) as common misdiagnoses."
Large multidisciplinary survey directly quantifying furunculosis as one of the most frequent misdiagnoses for HS, particularly at early Hurley stages.
Crohn Disease (cutaneous) MONDO:0005011
Overlapping Features Perianal fistulae and inflammatory nodules in Crohn disease can mimic HS, particularly in the anogenital region. The two conditions can also coexist.
Distinguishing Features
  • Gastrointestinal symptoms (diarrhea, abdominal pain)
  • Non-caseating granulomas on histopathology
  • Intestinal involvement on endoscopy
  • Knife-cut ulcers rather than follicular-based lesions
Show evidence (2 references)
