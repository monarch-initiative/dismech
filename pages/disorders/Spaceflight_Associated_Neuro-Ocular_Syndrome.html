Spaceflight Associated Neuro-Ocular Syndrome

Complex Neuro-Ophthalmologic Disorder Spaceflight-Induced Condition

Spaceflight Associated Neuro-Ocular Syndrome (SANS) is a constellation of neuro-ophthalmic findings observed in astronauts during and after long-duration spaceflight. The syndrome includes optic disc edema, globe flattening, choroidal and retinal folds, hyperopic refractive shifts, and cotton wool spots. SANS is hypothesized to result from cephalad fluid shifts in microgravity leading to elevated intracranial pressure, cerebral venous congestion, and compartmentalization of cerebrospinal fluid within the optic nerve sheath. It is a major physiologic barrier to future planetary spaceflight missions.

Pathophysiology

7
Cephalad Fluid Shift in Microgravity
In microgravity, the absence of hydrostatic pressure gradients causes a redistribution of body fluids toward the head. This cephalad fluid shift increases venous pressure in the head and neck, elevates intracranial pressure, and contributes to optic disc edema and posterior globe flattening.

Elevated Intracranial Pressure
Chronic mildly elevated intracranial pressure during long-duration spaceflight is hypothesized to transmit along the optic nerve sheath, compressing the optic nerve and causing papilledema, globe flattening, and choroidal folds. Unlike terrestrial idiopathic intracranial hypertension, the ICP elevation in SANS is typically mild and not associated with severe headaches.

Optic Nerve Sheath Distension
Increased cerebrospinal fluid pressure within the optic nerve sheath leads to sheath distension, which can be visualized on orbital MRI and ocular ultrasound. Terrestrially, ONS distension is associated with increased ICP.

CSF Compartmentalization
Compartmentalization of CSF within the orbital subarachnoid space may create a pressure differential that contributes to optic disc edema independently of overall ICP elevation. The cul-de-sac anatomy of the optic nerve sheath may act as a one-way valve, trapping CSF and elevating local pressure.

Choroidal Engorgement and Vascular Congestion
Microgravity-induced venous congestion leads to choroidal thickening and engorgement. This contributes to hyperopic refractive shifts and choroidal folds observed in affected astronauts. The lack of normal gravitational venous drainage impairs outflow from the orbital and ocular vasculature.

Treatments

2
Lower Body Negative Pressure MAXO:0000002
Application of negative pressure to the lower body to counteract cephalad fluid shift by drawing blood and fluids toward the legs. LBNP at 20 mmHg has been demonstrated to safely reduce intracranial pressure without impairing cerebral perfusion, and is a leading countermeasure candidate for SANS during spaceflight.
Show evidence (2 references)
PMID:30286250 SUPPORT Human Clinical
"An LBNP of 20 mmHg may be the optimal level to lower ICP without impairing CPP to counteract spaceflight-associated neuro-ocular syndrome in astronauts."
Clinical study demonstrating LBNP effectively lowers ICP in a dose-dependent fashion while maintaining cerebral perfusion pressure.
PMID:33335101 SUPPORT Human Clinical
"Lower body negative pressure (LBNP) can simulate gravitational stress during spaceflight that is likely to counteract SANS and venous thrombosis, but the ideal dose and method of delivery have yet to be determined."
Review supporting LBNP as a promising countermeasure while noting the need to optimize dosing for in-flight use.
Mobile Lower Body Negative Pressure Suit MAXO:0000002
A wearable negative pressure suit that allows astronauts to apply caudal-directed fluid redistribution during normal crew activities, serving as a practical in-flight countermeasure.
Show evidence (1 reference)
PMID:31747995 SUPPORT Human Clinical
"The flexible GravitySuit at 20 mmHg LBNP comfortably induced mechanical loading and desired fluid displacement while maintaining the mobility of hips and knee joints."
The GravitySuit mobile LBNP device induced footward fluid shift and mechanical loading at 20 mmHg without interfering with daily activity, providing a feasible in-flight countermeasure.

Environmental Factors

2
Microgravity Exposure
Prolonged exposure to microgravity during spaceflight is the primary environmental factor driving SANS. The condition is predominantly seen after missions of 6 months or longer aboard the International Space Station. Over the last 10 years, approximately 1 in 3 astronauts flying long-duration ISS missions have presented with one or more SANS findings.
Show evidence (2 references)
PMID:31162175 SUPPORT Human Clinical
"Over the last 10 years, ~1 in 3 astronauts flying long-duration ISS missions have presented with ≥1 of these ocular findings."
Establishes the epidemiologic association between long-duration microgravity exposure and SANS.
PMID:37072472 SUPPORT Human Clinical
"Long-duration spaceflight is associated with neurologic and ophthalmic clinical and imaging findings in astronauts termed spaceflight associated neuro-ocular syndrome (SANS)."
Confirms the association of SANS specifically with long-duration spaceflight.
Elevated Ambient Carbon Dioxide
The ISS cabin atmosphere contains CO2 levels higher than terrestrial ambient (typically 2-5 mmHg vs <1 mmHg on Earth). Elevated CO2 causes cerebral vasodilation, which may exacerbate intracranial pressure elevation and contribute to SANS pathology.
Show evidence (1 reference)
PMID:32047839 SUPPORT Human Clinical
"These potential risk factors include high salt diets, rigorous resistive exercise, exposure to elevated ambient CO2 levels and possible defects in the vitamin B12-dependent 1-carbon transfer pathways."
Elevated ambient CO2 on the ISS is identified as a potential risk factor that may augment intracranial pressure during spaceflight.
