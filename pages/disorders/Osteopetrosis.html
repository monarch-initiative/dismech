Osteopetrosis

Mendelian MONDO:0017198 Pathograph 2 Skeletal Dysplasia Osteosclerosis

Osteopetrosis (marble bone disease) is a group of heritable skeletal disorders characterized by increased bone density resulting from defective osteoclast-mediated bone resorption. The clinical spectrum ranges from the severe autosomal recessive infantile form (ARO), which is life-threatening in infancy due to bone marrow failure, to the milder autosomal dominant adult form (ADO2), which may be incidentally discovered on radiographs. Mutations in genes essential for osteoclast differentiation or acidification of the resorption lacuna (TCIRG1, CLCN7, OSTM1, SNX10, TNFSF11, TNFRSF11A) underlie most cases. Hematopoietic stem cell transplantation is curative for osteoclast-rich ARO forms, whereas osteoclast-poor forms caused by defective RANKL signaling do not respond to transplantation.

Subtypes

3
Autosomal Recessive Osteopetrosis (Infantile/Malignant)
Severe infantile form presenting in the first months of life with bone marrow failure, hepatosplenomegaly from extramedullary hematopoiesis, cranial nerve compression (optic atrophy, deafness), and failure to thrive. Without hematopoietic stem cell transplantation, most patients die in early childhood.
Autosomal Dominant Osteopetrosis Type 2 (Albers-Schonberg Disease)
The most common form of osteopetrosis, often diagnosed incidentally. Characterized by generalized osteosclerosis, pathologic fractures, and cranial nerve palsies. Many patients are asymptomatic; others develop recurrent fractures, osteomyelitis, or bone pain.
Intermediate Autosomal Recessive Osteopetrosis
An intermediate form with childhood onset, characterized by increased bone density, fractures, and mild anemia, but without the severe bone marrow failure seen in infantile ARO. Often caused by biallelic CLCN7 mutations.

Pathophysiology

3
Defective osteoclast acidification
The majority of osteopetrosis cases result from mutations in genes required for acidification of the osteoclast resorption lacuna. TCIRG1 encodes the a3 subunit of the vacuolar proton pump (V-ATPase), and its loss prevents acid secretion into the resorption lacuna. CLCN7 encodes a chloride-proton antiporter that provides the chloride conductance needed to maintain electroneutrality during proton pumping. OSTM1 stabilizes CLCN7. Without adequate acidification, hydroxyapatite dissolution fails and bone matrix cannot be degraded, leading to progressive accumulation of unresorbed primary spongiosa.

Comprehensive review confirming that osteoclast-rich ARO results from impaired osteoclast function rather than impaired differentiation.
Defective osteoclast differentiation (osteoclast-poor forms)
A subset of ARO cases result from mutations in TNFSF11 (encoding RANKL) or TNFRSF11A (encoding RANK), which are essential for osteoclast differentiation from monocyte-macrophage precursors. These osteoclast-poor forms lack mature osteoclasts entirely. In TNFSF11 (RANKL) deficiency, the defect lies in the bone microenvironment (osteoblasts fail to produce RANKL), so HSCT does not correct the phenotype. In contrast, TNFRSF11A (RANK) mutations affect the hematopoietic osteoclast precursors themselves, so HSCT can theoretically provide functional RANK-expressing donor monocytes.

Progressive accumulation of unresorbed bone encroaches on the marrow cavity, displacing hematopoietic tissue. This leads to pancytopenia and compensatory extramedullary hematopoiesis in the liver and spleen. The hematologic complications are the primary cause of mortality in infantile ARO.

Treatments

2
Hematopoietic stem cell transplantation MAXO:0000747
HSCT is the only curative treatment for osteoclast-rich forms of ARO. Donor-derived monocyte-macrophage precursors differentiate into functional osteoclasts that restore bone resorption. Best outcomes are achieved with early transplantation before irreversible complications develop. HSCT is not effective for osteoclast-poor forms (TNFSF11 mutations) or for ADO2.

For non-infantile forms where HSCT is not indicated, management is supportive. Includes monitoring for cranial nerve impingement, anemia, dental disease, and mineral metabolism abnormalities. Calcium and vitamin D supplementation may be needed for hypocalcemia.
