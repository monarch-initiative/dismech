Atrial Septal Defect

Congenital Cardiovascular Disorder MONDO:0006664 Pathograph 8 Heart Disorder

Atrial septal defect is a congenital cardiovascular disorder in which a deficiency or interatrial communication permits abnormal flow between the left and right atria. The dominant physiology is usually left-to-right shunting with right-sided volume overload; untreated significant defects can lead to exercise intolerance, supraventricular arrhythmias, right ventricular dysfunction, and pulmonary arterial hypertension.

4
Abnormal Atrial Septation
ASD originates from abnormal formation or deficiency of the interatrial septum or from anatomically related interatrial communications. True septal defects are confined to the oval fossa region, while primum, sinus venosus, and coronary sinus communications have distinct morphologic origins but share atrial-level mixing physiology.

Left-to-Right Shunting and Right Heart Volume Overload
In most unrepaired ASDs, greater left-sided filling pressure and right ventricular compliance drive left-to-right atrial shunting. The resulting pulmonary overcirculation enlarges right-sided chambers and can produce long-standing volume-overload complications.
