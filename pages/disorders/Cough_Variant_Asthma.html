Subtypes

2
Atopic Cough Variant Asthma
CVA associated with allergic sensitization and elevated IgE levels, triggered by aeroallergens. Similar proportion of atopy is seen in CVA as in classic asthma.
Show evidence (1 reference)
PMID:35127763 SUPPORT
"Despite lower total serum IgE levels in CVA, there was similar proportion of atopy in both groups."
Confirms atopic sensitization is common in CVA, supporting the existence of an atopic subtype.
Non-Atopic Cough Variant Asthma
CVA occurring without allergic sensitization, often triggered by respiratory infections, cold air, or irritants.

Pathophysiology

4
Eosinophilic Airway Inflammation
Eosinophilic infiltration of the bronchial mucosa with release of cytotoxic mediators causing epithelial damage and airway hyperresponsiveness. CVA shares type 2 inflammatory biology with classic asthma but typically shows lower eosinophilic biomarker levels despite comparable or greater cough burden, suggesting cough generation is not a simple linear function of eosinophil load.

Show evidence (4 references)
PMID:35127763 SUPPORT Human Clinical
"Cough variant asthma is distinctive from classic asthma in regard to clinical features, lung function, and airway inflammation."
Large multicenter APAC cohort demonstrates that CVA has measurable but lower type 2 inflammatory markers compared to classic asthma.
PMID:35127763 SUPPORT Human Clinical
"No correlation was found between cough assessment outcomes and sputum eosinophil count, blood eosinophil count, FENO, spirometry variables, or PD20-FEV1."
Demonstrates that cough severity in CVA is dissociated from eosinophilic inflammation intensity, suggesting additional cough-driving mechanisms beyond eosinophilia.
PMID:38566832 SUPPORT Human Clinical
"In the three groups of CVA patients, after receiving budesonide inhalation combined with conventional symptomatic treatment, the expression levels of serum IL-5, IL-6, IL-8, TNF-α, TGF-β1, and IgE and number of eosinophils significantly decreased (P <0.05)."
Confirms the presence of eosinophilic and cytokine-mediated inflammation in CVA that responds to inhaled corticosteroid treatment.

Airway Hyperresponsiveness
Enhanced bronchoconstrictor response to nonspecific stimuli such as methacholine, representing the hallmark physiologic abnormality that distinguishes CVA from non-asthmatic eosinophilic bronchitis. CVA typically shows milder AHR than classic asthma but it remains a defining diagnostic criterion.

Cough Reflex Hypersensitivity
Heightened sensitivity of airway cough receptors including vagal C-fibers and rapidly adapting receptors, leading to exaggerated cough responses to normally innocuous stimuli. Cough hypersensitivity symptom profiles are similar between asthmatic cough and refractory chronic cough, suggesting a shared neuronal mechanism across chronic cough phenotypes.

Small Airway Dysfunction
Small airway dysfunction is highly prevalent in CVA and frequently persists after short-term antiasthmatic treatment despite improvement in symptoms and inflammatory markers. This may contribute to disease chronicity and risk of progression to classic asthma.

Treatments

3
Inhaled Corticosteroids MAXO:0000314
First-line treatment that reduces eosinophilic airway inflammation, inflammatory cytokines, and cough symptom scores. Budesonide inhalation achieves complete remission in approximately 56% and partial remission in 36% of CVA patients at 8 weeks.
Show evidence (2 references)
PMID:38566832 SUPPORT Human Clinical
"Budesonide inhalation combined with conventional symptomatic treatment can significantly reduce the levels of serum inflammatory factors in patients with CVA to reduce inflammation and the allergic response, thereby reducing the cough symptom score, improving pulmonary function, and improving..."
Demonstrates efficacy of inhaled budesonide in reducing inflammation and improving symptoms in CVA, with biomarker-guided early response prediction.
PMID:34164212 SUPPORT Human Clinical
"Inhaled corticosteroids (ICSs) are the first-line treatment, and leukotriene receptor antagonists are also effective, in patients with both CVA and NAEB."
Confirms ICS as first-line treatment for CVA.
Bronchodilator Therapy MAXO:0000316
Short-acting beta-2 agonists provide symptomatic relief, and cough responsiveness to bronchodilators is a distinguishing feature of CVA versus non-asthmatic eosinophilic bronchitis.
Show evidence (1 reference)
PMID:34164212 SUPPORT Human Clinical
"Coughing is responsive to bronchodilators such as beta-agonists in CVA, but such feature has not been clarified in NAEB."
Confirms bronchodilator responsiveness of cough as a diagnostic feature of CVA.
Leukotriene Receptor Antagonists MAXO:0000315
Montelukast and similar agents are effective adjunctive therapy for CVA, particularly in patients with concurrent allergic rhinitis.
Show evidence (1 reference)
PMID:34164212 SUPPORT Human Clinical

Confirms leukotriene receptor antagonists as an effective treatment for CVA.

Environmental Factors

3
Allergen Exposure
Exposure to common aeroallergens such as house dust mite, pollens, moulds, and animal dander can trigger or exacerbate CVA in atopic individuals. Sensitization to multiple allergens is associated with progression to classic asthma with wheezing.
Show evidence (2 references)
PMID:17941915 SUPPORT Human Clinical
"To prevent the progression of cough variant asthma to classic asthma, avoidance of relevant allergens may be essential."
Direct clinical study of 74 CVA patients showing aeroallergen sensitization (HDM, pollens, moulds, dander) in CVA and recommending allergen avoidance.
PMID:17941915 SUPPORT Human Clinical
"Wheezing developed in six (15%) patients with cough variant asthma, who were sensitized to larger numbers of allergens (P=0.02) and had higher rates of sensitization to HDM (P=0.01) and dog dander (P=0.02) than the 34 patients in whom wheezing did not develop."
Multi-allergen sensitization in CVA patients is directly linked to progression risk, establishing allergens as clinically relevant triggers.
Cold Air Exposure
Inhalation of cold dry air is a well-recognized trigger of cough in CVA patients.
Upper Respiratory Infections
Viral upper respiratory tract infections frequently precede the onset of CVA or trigger exacerbations.
