NS1-Mediated Endothelial Glycocalyx Degradation
Dengue virus nonstructural protein 1 (NS1) is secreted and acts as an endothelial toxin, triggering glycocalyx degradation through activation of host proteases including MMP-9, cathepsin L, sialidases, and heparanase. NS1 engages TLR4 on monocytes and endothelial cells to induce inflammatory signaling, disrupts tight junctions by reducing ZO-1 and beta-catenin, and generates inflammatory lipid mediators via PLA2 activation.

NS1-Mediated Complement Activation and Modulation
NS1 activates complement via classical and lectin pathways while simultaneously modulating complement regulation by interacting with C4b-binding protein and vitronectin. This dual action contributes to vascular leak through immune complex formation and anaphylatoxin generation while limiting membrane attack complex formation.

Inflammasome Activation by NS1
NS1 acts as a damage-associated molecular pattern (DAMP) that activates the NLRP3 inflammasome in macrophages and monocytes, leading to caspase-1 activation and IL-1beta release. This occurs without pyroptotic cell death and appears to play a protective role in dengue infection.

Mast cells degranulate in response to dengue infection, releasing chymase, tryptase, histamine, leukotrienes, and platelet-activating factor. These mediators contribute to vascular permeability, vasodilation, and shock. Mast cell chymase is a predictive biomarker of severe disease.
