The epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR), a key tyrosine kinase of the ErbB 
family, regulates cell fate and tissue homeostasis in the skeletal system 
through complex downstream signaling networks, including the MAPK/ERK, PI3K/Akt, 
and NF-κB pathways. The balance between its physiological and pathological 
functions is critical for maintaining osteoprogenitor pools and articular 
cartilage integrity; however, yet how to selectively target EGFR in skeletal 
diseases remains poorly understood. To address this research gap, This review 
summarizes the dual roles of EGFR in osteoporosis (OP) and osteoarthritis (OA). 
We summarize its involvement in bone remodeling and cartilage degradation, 
compile evidence from network pharmacology linking EGFR to pharmacological 
modulators, and provide structural insights into its activation and inhibition 
via in silico molecular docking. Furthermore, this review highlights several 
unresolved questions: the cell-type-specific and spatiotemporal mapping of EGFR 
activation, the precise mechanisms of its allosteric modulation, and the 
development of tissue-targeted, non-oncological EGFR delivery systems. 
Addressing these gaps will be important for developing precision-based 
therapeutic strategies for skeletal degeneration.
