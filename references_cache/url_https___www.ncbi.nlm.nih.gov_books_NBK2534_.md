            <div class="main-content lit-style" itemscope="itemscope" itemtype="http://schema.org/CreativeWork"><div class="meta-content fm-sec"><h1 id="_NBK2534_"><span class="title" itemprop="name"><i>FLNB-</i>Related Disorders</span></h1><p class="contrib-group"><span itemprop="author">Stephen Robertson</span>, FRACP, DPhil and <span itemprop="author">Joanna Meira</span>, MD, MSc.</p><a data-jig="ncbitoggler" href="#__NBK2534_ai__" style="border:0;text-decoration:none">Author Information and Affiliations</a><div style="display:none" class="ui-widget" id="__NBK2534_ai__"><div class="contrib half_rhythm"><span itemprop="author">Stephen Robertson</span>, FRACP, DPhil<div class="affiliation small">Professor of Paediatric Genetics<br />Department of Paediatrics and Child Health<br />Dunedin School of Medicine<br />University of Otago<br />Dunedin, New Zealand<div><span class="email-label">Email: </span><a href="mailto:dev@null" data-email="zn.ca.ogato@nostrebor.nehpets" class="oemail">zn.ca.ogato@nostrebor.nehpets</a></div></div></div><div class="contrib half_rhythm"><span itemprop="author">Joanna Meira</span>, MD, MSc<div class="affiliation small">Professor, State University of Bahia (UNEB)<br />Salvador, Bahia, Brazil<div><span class="email-label">Email: </span><a href="mailto:dev@null" data-email="moc.liamg@mcgannaoj" class="oemail">moc.liamg@mcgannaoj</a></div></div></div></div><p class="small">Initial Posting: <span itemprop="datePublished">October 9, 2008</span>; Last Update: <span itemprop="dateModified">September 11, 2025</span>.</p><p><em>Estimated reading time: 34 minutes</em></p></div><div class="jig-ncbiinpagenav body-content whole_rhythm" data-jigconfig="allHeadingLevels: ['h2'],smoothScroll: false" itemprop="text"><div id="flnb-dis.Summary" itemprop="description"><h2 id="_flnb-dis_Summary_">Summary</h2><div><h4 class="inline">Clinical characteristics.</h4><p>The <i>FLNB-</i>related disorders can be divided into two groups of conditions caused by loss of function or gain of function of filamin-B. Biallelic <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/loss-of-function/">loss-of-function</a> pathogenic variants in <i>FLNB</i> cause spondylocarpotarsal synostosis syndrome (<i>FLNB</i>-SCT). Monoallelic <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gain-of-function/">gain-of-function</a> pathogenic variants in <i>FLNB</i> cause a spectrum of phenotypic severity ranging from apparently <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/isolated/">isolated</a> clubfoot to Larsen syndrome (<i>FLNB</i>-LS), atelosteogenesis type 3 (<i>FLNB-</i>AO3), and atelosteogenesis type 1 (<i>FLNB</i>-AO1), which is perinatal lethal. For the purposes of this GeneReview, the previously described entities Piepkorn dysplasia and boomerang dysplasia are subsumed under the <i>FLNB</i>-AO1 spectrum.</p><p><i>FLNB</i>-SCT is characterized by postnatal disproportionate short stature; scoliosis and lordosis due to vertebral fusions; carpal and tarsal synostosis; and, variably, clubfeet, hearing loss, and dental enamel hypoplasia.</p><p><i>FLNB</i>-LS is characterized by combinations of <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/congenital/">congenital</a> dislocations of the hip, knee, and elbow; clubfeet (equinovarus or equinovalgus foot deformities); scoliosis and cervical kyphosis (which can be associated with a cervical myelopathy); short, broad, spatulate distal phalanges; distinctive craniofacial features (prominent forehead, depressed nasal bridge, malar flattening, and widely spaced eyes); vertebral anomalies; and supernumerary carpal and tarsal ossification centers. Individuals with <i>FLNB</i>-LS may also present with midline cleft palate and hearing loss.</p><p><i>FLNB</i>-AO1 and <i>FLNB</i>-AO3 are characterized by severe short-limbed dwarfism; dislocated hips, knees, and elbows; and clubfeet. <i>FLNB</i>-AO1 is lethal in the perinatal period. At its most severe, the spectrum of phenotypes assigned <i>FLNB</i>-AO1 can present with perinatal-lethal micromelic dwarfism characterized by flipper-like limbs (polysyndactyly with complete syndactyly of all fingers and toes, hypoplastic or absent first digits, and duplicated intermediate and distal phalanges); macrobrachycephaly; prominent forehead; hypertelorism; and proptosis. Occasional features include cleft palate, omphalocele, and cardiac and genitourinary anomalies. In individuals with <i>FLNB</i>-AO3, survival beyond the neonatal period is possible with intensive and invasive respiratory support.</p></div><div><h4 class="inline">Diagnosis/testing.</h4><p>The diagnosis of <i>FLNB</i>-SCT is established in a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> by identification of <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/biallelic/">biallelic</a> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/loss-of-function/">loss-of-function</a> pathogenic variants in <i>FLNB</i> by <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/molecular-genetic-testing/">molecular genetic testing</a>. The diagnosis of other <i>FLNB-</i>related disorders (LS, AO1, AO3) is established in a proband by identification of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gain-of-function/">gain-of-function</a> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> in <i>FLNB</i> by molecular genetic testing.</p></div><div><h4 class="inline">Management.</h4><p><i>Treatment of manifestations:</i> Cervical spine instability in asymptomatic infants can be successfully managed with posterior arthrodesis. Function can be stabilized (if not improved) in infants with myelopathic signs by a combination of anterior decompression and circumferential arthrodesis. Hip dislocation in individuals with <i>FLNB</i>-LS usually requires operative reduction. Scoliosis and clubfeet are managed in a routine manner. Anesthetic agents that allow more rapid induction and recovery are preferred in those with laryngotracheomalacia. When possible, cleft palate and hearing loss are best managed by multidisciplinary teams.</p><p><i>Surveillance:</i> Annual orthopedic evaluation for progressive scoliosis; feeding and growth assessment for those with cleft palate by a multidisciplinary team; annual audiologic and dental evaluations.</p><p><i>Pregnancy management:</i> Delivery of an affected infant has the potential to be complicated by extended breech presentation due to dislocation of the hips and knees.</p></div><div><h4 class="inline">Genetic counseling.</h4><p><i>FLNB</i>-SCT is inherited in an <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/autosomal-recessive/">autosomal recessive</a> manner. If both parents are known to be <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a> for an <i>FLNB</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a>, each sib of an affected individual has at conception a 25% chance of inheriting <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/biallelic/">biallelic</a> pathogenic variants and being affected, a 50% chance of inheriting one pathogenic variant and being heterozygous, and a 25% chance of inheriting neither of the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/familial/">familial</a> <i>FLNB</i> pathogenic variants. Heterozygous sibs of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> with <i>FLNB</i>-SCT can exhibit mild reductions in stature but no other medically significant phenotypic manifestations. Once the <i>FLNB</i> pathogenic variant(s) have been identified in an affected family member, <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygote/">heterozygote</a> testing for at-risk relatives and prenatal/<a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/preimplantation-genetic-testing/">preimplantation genetic testing</a> are possible.</p><p><i>FLNB</i>-LS, <i>FLNB</i>-AO1, <i>FLNB-</i>AO3, and <i>FLNB-</i>related apparently <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/isolated/">isolated</a> clubfoot are inherited in an <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/autosomal-dominant/">autosomal dominant</a> manner. Comparatively mild (e.g., <i>FLNB</i>-LS) and severe (e.g., <i>FLNB-</i>AO3) forms of the autosomal dominant <i>FLNB</i>-related disorders can occur in the same family. Some individuals diagnosed with an autosomal dominant <i>FLNB</i>-related disorder have the disorder as the result of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> inherited from a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a> or mosaic parent. Some individuals have the disorder as the result of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/de-novo/"><i>de novo</i></a> pathogenic variant (the vast majority of lethal <i>FLNB</i> conditions are the result of <i>de novo</i> pathogenic variants). Each child of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> who is heterozygous for an <i>FLNB</i> pathogenic variant has a 50% chance of inheriting the pathogenic variant. Each child of a proband with <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/somatic-mosaicism/">somatic mosaicism</a> for an <i>FLNB</i> pathogenic variant has up to a 50% chance of inheriting the pathogenic variant. Offspring who inherit an <i>FLNB</i> pathogenic variant from a proband with somatic mosaicism may be more severely affected than the proband. Once the <i>FLNB</i> pathogenic variant has been identified in an affected family member, prenatal/<a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/preimplantation-genetic-testing/">preimplantation genetic testing</a> are possible.</p></div></div><div id="flnb-dis.GeneReview_Scope"><h2 id="_flnb-dis_GeneReview_Scope_"><i>GeneReview</i> Scope</h2><div id="flnb-dis.Tc" class="table"><p class="large-table-link" style="display:none"><span class="right"><a href="/books/NBK2534/table/flnb-dis.Tc/?report=objectonly" target="object">View in own window</a></span></p><div class="large_tbl" id="__flnb-dis.Tc_lrgtbl__"><table class="no_bottom_margin"><thead><tr><th id="hd_h_flnb-dis.Tc_1_1_1_1" scope="col" rowspan="1" colspan="1" style="text-align:left;vertical-align:middle;"><i>FLNB-</i>Related Disorders: Phenotypic Spectrum</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td headers="hd_h_flnb-dis.Tc_1_1_1_1" rowspan="1" colspan="1" style="text-align:left;vertical-align:middle;"><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Spondylocarpotarsal synostosis syndrome (<i>FLNB</i>-SCT)</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Larsen syndrome (<i>FLNB</i>-LS)</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Atelosteogenesis type 1 (<i>FLNB</i>-AO1)</div><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Including boomerang dysplasia, Piepkorn dysplasia, &#x00026; spondylohumerofemoral (giant cell) dysplasia</div></li></ul></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Atelosteogenesis type 3 (<i>FLNB</i>-AO3)</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Apparently <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/isolated/">isolated</a> clubfoot</div></li></ul>

Biallelic <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/loss-of-function/">loss-of-function</a> pathogenic variants in <i>FLNB</i> cause spondylocarpotarsal synostosis syndrome (<i>FLNB</i>-SCT). Monoallelic <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gain-of-function/">gain-of-function</a> pathogenic variants in <i>FLNB</i> cause a spectrum of phenotypic severity ranging from apparently <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/isolated/">isolated</a> clubfoot to Larsen syndrome (<i>FLNB</i>-LS), atelosteogenesis type 3 (<i>FLNB-</i>AO3), and atelosteogenesis type 1 (<i>FLNB</i>-AO1), which is perinatal lethal. For the purposes of this <i>GeneReview</i>, the previously described entities Piepkorn dysplasia and boomerang dysplasia are subsumed under the <i>FLNB</i>-AO1 spectrum.</p><div id="flnb-dis.Suggestive_Findings"><h3>Suggestive Findings</h3><div id="flnb-dis.FLNBRelated_Spondylocarpotarsal"><h4><i>FLNB</i>-Related Spondylocarpotarsal Synostosis Syndrome (<i>FLNB</i>-SCT)</h4><p><i>FLNB-</i>SCT <b>should be suspected</b> in individuals with the following clinical and radiographic features [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.langer.1994.1">Langer et al 1994</a>].</p><p>
<b>Clinical features</b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Postnatal disproportionate short stature</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Scoliosis and lordosis</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Clubfeet</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Other manifestations: midline cleft palate, conductive and/or sensorineural hearing loss, joint stiffness, dental enamel hypoplasia</div></li></ul><p>
<b>Radiographic features</b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div class="half_rhythm">Fusion of adjacent vertebrae and posterior elements, which can involve noncontiguous areas of the cervical, thoracic, and lumbar spine</div><div class="half_rhythm">Note: (1) Asymmetric fusion of the posterior elements can result in "a unilateral unsegmented vertebral bar" [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.langer.1994.1">Langer et al 1994</a>]. (2) More complex bilateral and midline-fused structures have also been reported. (3) Although frequently referred to as "segmentation defects," the process of segmentation is normal in individuals with <i>FLNB</i>-SCT and the fusion of adjacent vertebral elements is related to a defect in a separate pathologic process that occurs later in development. (4) Basilar impression with or without foramen magnum stenosis has been described.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div class="half_rhythm">Carpal and tarsal synostosis. Carpal synostosis is usually between the capitate and hamate, or the lunate and triquetrum [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.langer.1994.1">Langer et al 1994</a>].</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div class="half_rhythm">Delayed ossification of epiphyses (especially of carpal bones) and bilateral epiphyseal dysplasia of the femur have been reported in two individuals [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.honeywell.2002.318">Honeywell et al 2002</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.mitter.2008.779">Mitter et al 2008</a>].</div></li></ul></div><div id="flnb-dis.FLNBRelated_Larsen_Syndrome_FLN"><h4><i>FLNB</i>-Related Larsen Syndrome (<i>FLNB</i>-LS)</h4><p><i>FLNB</i>-LS <b>should be suspected</b> in individuals with the following clinical and radiographic features [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.larsen.1950.574">Larsen et al 1950</a>].</p><p>
<b>Clinical features</b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Congenital dislocations of the hip, knee, elbow, and occasionally the shoulder</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Clubfoot (equinovarus or equinovalgus foot deformities). This may be the only clinically apparent sign in some individuals [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.yang.2016.1181">Yang et al 2016</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.quiggle.2022.421">Quiggle et al 2022</a>].</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Scoliosis and cervical kyphosis, which can be associated with cervical myelopathy</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Short, broad, spatulate distal phalanges, particularly of the thumb</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Distinctive craniofacial features (prominent forehead, depressed nasal bridge, malar flattening, and widely spaced eyes)</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Other manifestations: midline cleft palate, hearing loss (often resulting from malformations of the ossicles)</div></li></ul><p>
<b>Radiographic features in early childhood</b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div class="half_rhythm">Vertebral anomalies: hypoplastic vertebrae, hemivertebrae, spondylolisthesis, bifid posterior processes</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div class="half_rhythm">Supernumerary (accessory) carpal and tarsal bone ossification centers</div><div class="half_rhythm">Note: Possibly a universal finding [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.bicknell.2007.89">Bicknell et al 2007</a>].</div></li></ul></div><div id="flnb-dis.FLNBRelated_Atelosteogenesis_Ty"><h4><i>FLNB</i>-Related Atelosteogenesis Type 3 (<i>FLNB</i>-AO3)</h4><p><i>FLNB</i>-AO3 should be suspected in individuals with the following clinical and radiographic features.</p><p>
<b>Clinical features</b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Dislocated hips, knees, and elbows</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Clubfoot</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Milder than <i>FLNB</i>-AO1; survival beyond the neonatal period is possible with intensive and invasive respiratory support [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.schultz.1999.28">Schultz et al 1999</a>].</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Laryngotracheobronchomalacia</div></li></ul><p>
<b>Radiographic features</b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Mild vertebral hypoplasia</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Distal tapering of the humeri and femora</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Short and broad tubular bones of the hands and feet</div></li></ul><p>Note: There are no qualitative diagnostic differences between <i>FLNB-</i>AO3 and <i>FLNB-</i>LS. The prime distinction lies in the severity of the disorder in individuals with <i>FLNB-</i>AO3, such that survival beyond the first year of life is rare.</p></div><div id="flnb-dis.FLNBRelated_Atelosteogenesis_Ty_1"><h4><i>FLNB</i>-Related Atelosteogenesis Type 1 (<i>FLNB</i>-AO1)</h4><p><i>FLNB</i>-AO1 <b>should be suspected</b> in individuals with the following clinical and radiographic features.</p><p>
<b>Clinical features</b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Perinatal-lethal short-limbed dwarfism</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Severe dislocations of the hips, knees, and elbows; clubfoot</div></li></ul><p>
<b>Radiographic features</b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Marked platyspondyly</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Hypoplastic pelvis</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Thoracic hypoplasia</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Incomplete or absent, shortened, or distally tapered humeri and femora; absent, shortened, or bowed radii; shortened and bowed ulnae and tibiae; absent fibulae</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Unossified or partially ossified metacarpals and middle and proximal phalanges</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Occasionally, extraskeletal manifestations including encephalocele and/or omphalocele [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.bicknell.2005.e43">Bicknell et al 2005</a>]</div></li></ul><p>Note: Individuals with a diagnosis of <b>boomerang dysplasia</b> or <b>Piepkorn dysplasia</b> (both entities being perinatal-lethal bone dysplasias with close similarities to <i>FLNB</i>-AO1) are now best subsumed under a diagnosis of <i>FLNB</i>-AO1. Bowing of the femora was previously considered a differentiating feature between boomerang dysplasia and <i>FLNB</i>-AO1, but following the definition of their molecular pathogenesis, it is unlikely that this clinical sign adequately differentiates these two conditions. Similarly, Piepkorn dysplasia exhibits significant phenotypic overlap with <i>FLNB</i>-AO1, with oligosyndactyly possibly representing the only differentiating feature. For the purposes of this review, boomerang dysplasia and Piepkorn dysplasia are subsumed under the <i>FLNB</i>-AO1 spectrum.</p></div><div id="flnb-dis.FLNBRelated_Apparently_Isolated"><h4><i>FLNB</i>-Related Apparently Isolated Clubfoot</h4><p>Preliminary evidence suggests that a minimal manifestation of the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/autosomal-dominant/">autosomal dominant</a> <i>FLNB-</i>related disorders is apparently <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/isolated/">isolated</a> clubfoot [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.yang.2016.1181">Yang et al 2016</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.quiggle.2022.421">Quiggle et al 2022</a>]. Some individuals in these series had other mild manifestations of Larsen syndrome, but in pedigrees where the identified variants segregated, manifestations were mild, if not monosymptomatic. Studies of larger cohorts are necessary to fully define the phenotypic manifestations of these presentations.</p></div></div><div id="flnb-dis.Establishing_the_Diagnosis"><h3>Establishing the Diagnosis</h3><p><b><i>FLNB-</i>SCT.</b> The diagnosis of <i>FLNB-</i>SCT <b>is established</b> in a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> by identification of <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/biallelic/">biallelic</a> pathogenic (or <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/likely-pathogenic/">likely pathogenic</a>) <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/loss-of-function/">loss-of-function</a> variants in <i>FLNB</i> by <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/molecular-genetic-testing/">molecular genetic testing</a> (see <a href="/books/NBK2534/table/flnb-dis.T.molecular_genetic_testing_use/?report=objectonly" target="object" rid-ob="figobflnbdisTmoleculargenetictestinguse">Table 1</a>).</p><p><b>Autosomal dominant <i>FLNB</i>-related disorders.</b> The diagnosis of other <i>FLNB-</i>related disorders (LS, AO1, and AO3, and <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/isolated/">isolated</a> clubfoot) <b>is established</b> in a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> by identification of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a> pathogenic (or <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/likely-pathogenic/">likely pathogenic</a>) <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gain-of-function/">gain-of-function</a> variant in <i>FLNB</i> by <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/molecular-genetic-testing/">molecular genetic testing</a> (see <a href="/books/NBK2534/table/flnb-dis.T.molecular_genetic_testing_use/?report=objectonly" target="object" rid-ob="figobflnbdisTmoleculargenetictestinguse">Table 1</a>).</p><p>Note: (1) Per American College of Medical Genetics and Genomics&#x000a0;/ Association for Molecular Pathology variant interpretation guidelines, the terms "<a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a>" and "<a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/likely-pathogenic/">likely pathogenic</a> variant" are synonymous in a clinical setting, meaning that both are considered diagnostic and can be used for clinical decision making [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.richards.2015.405">Richards et al 2015</a>]. Reference to "pathogenic variants" in this <i>GeneReview</i> is understood to include likely pathogenic variants. (2) The identification of variant(s) of <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/uncertain-significance/">uncertain significance</a> cannot be used to confirm or rule out the diagnosis.</p><p>Molecular genetic testing approaches can include a combination of <b><a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gene/">gene</a>-targeted testing</b> (single gene testing, <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/multigene-panel/">multigene panel</a>) and <b>comprehensive</b>
<b><a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/genomic/">genomic</a> testing</b> (<a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/exome-sequencing/">exome sequencing</a>, <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/genome-sequencing/">genome sequencing</a>). Gene-targeted testing requires that the clinician determine which gene(s) are likely involved (see <a href="#flnb-dis.Option_1">Option 1</a>), whereas comprehensive genomic testing does not (see <a href="#flnb-dis.Option_2">Option 2</a>).</p><div id="flnb-dis.Option_1"><h4>Option 1</h4><p>When the phenotypic and laboratory findings suggest the diagnosis of <i>FLNB-</i>related disorders, <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/molecular-genetic-testing/">molecular genetic testing</a> approaches can include <b>single-<a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gene/">gene</a> testing</b> or use of a <b><a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/multigene-panel/">multigene panel</a></b>.</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div class="half_rhythm"><b>Single-<a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gene/">gene</a> testing.</b> Sequence analysis of <i>FLNB</i> is performed first to detect <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/missense/">missense</a>, <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/nonsense-variant/">nonsense</a>, and splice-site variants and small intragenic deletions/insertions. Note: (1) Depending on the sequencing method used, single-<a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/exon/">exon</a>, multiexon, or whole-gene deletions/duplications may not be detected. If no <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> is found (or only one pathogenic variant is identified in a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> with features characteristic of <i>FLNB-</i>SCT), the next step is to perform gene-targeted <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/deletion-duplication-analysis/">deletion/duplication analysis</a> to detect exon and whole-gene deletions or duplications. (2) Multiexon <i>FLNB</i> deletions have been identified in two individuals with <i>FLNB-</i>SCT [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.fukushima.2021.528">Fukushima et al 2021</a>] but have not been reported to cause <i>FLNB-</i>LS, <i>FLNB-</i>AO1, or <i>FLNB-</i>AO3. (see <a href="/books/NBK2534/table/flnb-dis.T.molecular_genetic_testing_use/?report=objectonly" target="object" rid-ob="figobflnbdisTmoleculargenetictestinguse">Table 1</a>).</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div class="half_rhythm"><b>A <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/multigene-panel/">multigene panel</a></b> that includes <i>FLNB</i> and other genes of interest (see <a href="#flnb-dis.Differential_Diagnosis">Differential Diagnosis</a>) is most likely to identify the genetic cause of the condition while limiting identification of pathogenic variants and variants of <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/uncertain-significance/">uncertain significance</a> in genes that do not explain the underlying <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a>. Note: (1) The genes included in the panel and the diagnostic <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/sensitivity/">sensitivity</a> of the testing used for each <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gene/">gene</a> vary by laboratory and are likely to change over time. (3) In some laboratories, panel options may include a custom laboratory-designed panel and/or custom phenotype-focused <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/exome/">exome</a> analysis that includes genes specified by the clinician. (4) Methods used in a panel may include <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/sequence-analysis/">sequence analysis</a>, <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/deletion-duplication-analysis/">deletion/duplication analysis</a>, and/or other non-sequencing-based tests.</div><div class="half_rhythm">For an introduction to multigene panels click <a href="/books/n/gene/app5/#app5.Multigene_Panels">here</a>. More detailed information for clinicians ordering genetic tests can be found <a href="/books/n/gene/app5/#app5.Multigene_Panels_FAQs">here</a>.</div></li></ul></div><div id="flnb-dis.Option_2"><h4>Option 2</h4><p>When the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a> is indistinguishable from many other skeletal dysplasias, <b>comprehensive</b>
<b><a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/genomic/">genomic</a> testing</b> does not require the clinician to determine which <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gene/">gene</a> is likely involved. <b>Exome sequencing</b> is most commonly used; <b><a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/genome-sequencing/">genome sequencing</a></b> is also possible.</p><p>For an introduction to comprehensive <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/genomic/">genomic</a> testing click <a href="/books/n/gene/app5/#app5.Comprehensive_Genomic_Testing">here</a>. Biallelic <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/loss-of-function/">loss-of-function</a> pathogenic variants in <i>FLNB</i> cause spondylocarpotarsal synostosis syndrome (<i>FLNB</i>-SCT). Monoallelic <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gain-of-function/">gain-of-function</a> pathogenic variants in <i>FLNB</i> cause a spectrum of phenotypic severity ranging from apparently <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/isolated/">isolated</a> clubfoot to Larsen syndrome (<i>FLNB</i>-LS), atelosteogenesis type 3 (<i>FLNB-</i>AO3), and atelosteogenesis type 1 (<i>FLNB</i>-AO1), which is perinatal lethal. For the purposes of this <i>GeneReview</i>, the previously described entities Piepkorn dysplasia, boomerang dysplasia, and spondylohumerofemoral (giant cell) dysplasia are subsumed under the <i>FLNB</i>-AO1 spectrum. To date, more than 100 individuals with pathogenic variants in <i>FLNB</i> have been identified [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.daniel.2012.665">Daniel et al 2012</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.girisha.2016.27">Girisha et al 2016</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.yang.2016.1181">Yang et al 2016</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.salian.2018.159">Salian et al 2018</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.stenson.2020.1197">Stenson et al 2020</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.quiggle.2022.421">Quiggle et al 2022</a>]. The following description of the phenotypic features associated with <i>FLNB-</i>related conditions is based on these reports.</p><div id="flnb-dis.FLNBRelated_Spondylocarpotarsal_1"><h4><i>FLNB-</i>Related Spondylocarpotarsal Synostosis Syndrome (<i>FLNB</i>-SCT)</h4><p>Individuals with <i>FLNB-</i>SCT have normal or near-normal birth length; however, progressive vertebral fusion results in poor growth of the trunk and short stature becomes evident postnatally. Stature is typically 3-6 standard deviations (SD) below the mean [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.salian.2018.159">Salian et al 2018</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.ramosmej_a.2022.167">Ramos-Mej&#x000ed;a et al 2022</a>].</p><p>Scoliosis is common but variable in severity and time of onset because of the extent and pattern of vertebral fusion. Some authors have observed deformity at birth, although the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a> may only become evident later in childhood [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.salian.2018.159">Salian et al 2018</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.ramosmej_a.2022.167">Ramos-Mej&#x000ed;a et al 2022</a>]. The irregular nature of the vertebral anomalies can also give rise to other complications such as cervical spine instability [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.seaver.2000.340">Seaver &#x00026; Boyd 2000</a>] and basilar impression.</p><p>Clubfeet, pes planus, and cleft palate have been described in a small number of individuals with <i>FLNB</i>-SCT. Some authors have reported mild craniofacial dysmorphism as part of this condition, but the majority of individuals do not exhibit these features [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.ramosmej_a.2022.167">Ramos-Mej&#x000ed;a et al 2022</a>].</p><p><i>FLNB</i>-SCT has also been associated with retinal anomalies [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.steiner.2000.131">Steiner et al 2000</a>] and sensorineural deafness [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.langer.1994.1">Langer et al 1994</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.co_lho.1998.12">Co&#x000ea;lho et al 1998</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.yasin.2021.31">Yasin et al 2021</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.ramosmej_a.2022.167">Ramos-Mej&#x000ed;a et al 2022</a>]. The cataracts and retinal abnormalities were not severe enough to impair vision [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.steiner.2000.131">Steiner et al 2000</a>] and have not been observed in subsequently described individuals, so they may not represent primary manifestations of the condition [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.ramosmej_a.2022.167">Ramos-Mej&#x000ed;a et al 2022</a>].</p><p>Dental enamel hypoplasia has been reported in at least two unrelated individuals [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.brunettipierri.2008.1230">Brunetti-Pierri et al 2008</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.mitter.2008.779">Mitter et al 2008</a>].</p><p>Intelligence is normal.</p><p>In keeping with the observation that common variation at the <i>FLNB</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/locus/">locus</a> contributes modestly to the genetic basis of stature in healthy humans [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.yengo.2022.704">Yengo et al 2022</a>], individuals with a <b><a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a></b> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/loss-of-function/">loss-of-function</a> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> in <i>FLNB</i> can exhibit mild reductions in stature but no other medically significant phenotypic manifestations.</p></div><div id="flnb-dis.FLNBRelated_Larsen_Syndrome_FLN_1"><h4><i>FLNB</i>-Related Larsen Syndrome (<i>FLNB</i>-LS)</h4><p><i>FLNB-</i>LS is compatible with survival into adulthood [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.bicknell.2007.89">Bicknell et al 2007</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.girisha.2016.27">Girisha et al 2016</a>]. Intelligence is normal.</p><p>Intrafamilial variation in <i>FLNB</i>-LS can be remarkable. In a large family segregating one of the recurring pathogenic variants leading to <i>FLNB</i>-LS, some individuals had cleft palate and multiple large joint dislocations, whereas others who had no major anomalies had short stature and very mild clinical and radiographic features, such as short distal phalanges and supernumerary carpal and tarsal bones [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.bicknell.2007.89">Bicknell et al 2007</a>]. These mild phenotypes may be analogous to reports that <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/isolated/">isolated</a> clubfoot may be a minimal manifestation of this spectrum of conditions [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.yang.2016.1181">Yang et al 2016</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.quiggle.2022.421">Quiggle et al 2022</a>]. Clinical variability can also result from the presence of <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/somatic-mosaicism/">somatic mosaicism</a> for a causative <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> in a mildly affected parent and the presence of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/germline/">germline</a> pathogenic variant in more severely affected offspring [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.bernkopf.2017.1360">Bernkopf et al 2017</a>].</p><p>In their study of 20 unrelated families with a total of 52 affected individuals, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.bicknell.2007.89">Bicknell et al [2007]</a> determined that all probands had dislocations or subluxations of the large joints (80% hip, 80% knee, and 65% elbow). Delivery of an affected infant has the potential to be complicated by extended breech presentation due to dislocation of the hips and knees. The most mildly affected <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> had subluxation of the shoulders as her only large joint manifestation. Clubfoot was present in 75% of affected individuals.</p><p>Stature is mildly affected. In 14 of 20 probands height was below the tenth centile; height was rarely below the first centile and in one individual was above the 97th centile [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.bicknell.2007.89">Bicknell et al 2007</a>].</p><p>Spinal abnormalities were observed on radiographs in 16 (84%) of 19 probands. Cervical kyphosis was noted in 50%, usually from subluxation or fusion of the bodies of C2, C3, and C4, which was commonly associated with posterior vertebral arch dysraphism (i.e., dysplasia of the vertebral laminae and hypoplasia of the lateral processes of all cervical vertebrae). Individuals with <i>FLNB</i>-LS and cervical spine dysplasia are at significant risk for cervical cord myelopathy and secondary tetraparesis [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.bicknell.2007.89">Bicknell et al 2007</a>]. The incidence of myelopathy is at least 15%. Evidence suggests that preemptive posterior stabilization of the cervical spine in individuals with <i>FLNB</i>-LS with cervical spine dysplasia may prevent this complication and that combined anterior and posterior stabilization can lead to clinical improvement in individuals with evidence of myelopathy [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.sakaura.2007.e39">Sakaura et al 2007</a>].</p><p>Craniofacial anomalies are found in all individuals with <i>FLNB</i>-LS<i>.</i> These include a prominent forehead, depressed nasal bridge, malar flattening, and widely spaced eyes. Cleft palate occurs in 15% of affected individuals.</p><p>Deafness is common [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.herrmann.1981.45">Herrmann et al 1981</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.stanley.1988.395">Stanley et al 1988</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.maack.1991.51">Maack &#x00026; Muntz 1991</a>]. Conductive deafness, often with malformation of the ossicles of the middle ear, was observed in four (21%) of 19 probands [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.bicknell.2007.89">Bicknell et al 2007</a>].</p><p>Although laryngotracheomalacia has been reported in association with <i>FLNB</i>-LS, few individuals with <i>FLNB</i>-LS and a documented <i>FLNB</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> are severely affected.</p><p>Short, broad, spatulate distal phalanges, particularly of the thumb, are a common but not invariable manifestation (67%) of <i>FLNB</i>-LS [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.bicknell.2007.89">Bicknell et al 2007</a>].</p></div><div id="flnb-dis.FLNBRelated_Atelosteogenesis_Ty_2"><h4><i>FLNB</i>-Related Atelosteogenesis Type 3 (<i>FLNB</i>-AO3)</h4><p>The most conspicuous finding of <i>FLNB</i>-AO3 is joint dislocations. A specific clinical diagnosis of <i>FLNB</i>-AO3 is seldom possible by prenatal ultrasound examination alone. Delivery of an affected infant has the potential to be complicated by extended breech presentation due to dislocation of the hips and knees.</p><p>Infants with <i>FLNB</i>-AO3 can survive the neonatal period but may require intensive and invasive support to do so. The infant reported by <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.schultz.1999.28">Schultz et al [1999]</a> had significant respiratory insufficiency as a result of laryngotracheomalacia and thoracic hypoplasia. Her mother, who was intellectually normal, had similar but milder respiratory problems in the neonatal period. The manifestations of <i>FLNB</i>-AO3 overlap with those of <i>FLNB</i>-LS: large joint dislocations, clubfeet, short stature, and spinal anomalies. The observation of a distally tapering humerus on radiographs is indicative of <i>FLNB</i>-AO3, and suggests a stronger likelihood of significant laryngotracheobronchomalacia, the major differentiating feature between these two diagnoses.</p><p>Infants with <i>FLNB</i>-AO3 have been born to parents with milder phenotypes (similar to <i>FLNB</i>-LS). In these instances, the parents probably present a mild <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a> associated with <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/somatic-mosaicism/">somatic mosaicism</a>, whereas their offspring with a non-mosaic <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/germline/">germline</a> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> present a severe phenotype.</p><p>Neurodevelopment is mildly affected in some long-term survivors with <i>FLNB</i>-AO3 [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.schultz.1999.28">Schultz et al 1999</a>], although the authors assumed this to be a secondary consequence of orthopedic and respiratory complications of the primary disorder.</p></div><div id="flnb-dis.FLNBRelated_Atelosteogenesis_Ty_3"><h4><i>FLNB</i>-Related Atelosteogenesis Type 1 (<i>FLNB</i>-AO1) (including boomerang dysplasia and Piepkorn dysplasia)</h4><p>On prenatal ultrasound examination, the findings of <i>FLNB</i>-AO1 consist of thoracic hypoplasia and limb shortening with delayed or absent ossification of vertebral and appendicular elements. Joint dislocations may be evident. Definitive clinical diagnosis by ultrasound examination alone is possible [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.tsutsumi.2012.216">Tsutsumi et al 2012</a>]. Polyhydramnios can complicate the pregnancy. Neonates with <i>FLNB</i>-AO1 die soon after birth from cardiorespiratory insufficiency. Occasionally, extraskeletal manifestations including encephalocele and omphalocele are encountered [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.bicknell.2005.e43">Bicknell et al 2005</a>].</p><p>A severe form of the <i>FLNB-</i>AO1 spectrum characterized by perinatal-lethal micromelic dwarfism has been described in fewer than five individuals in the literature and was formerly referred to as Piepkorn dysplasia. It is characterized by flipper-like limbs, a characteristic form of polysyndactyly with complete syndactyly of all fingers and toes. The thumbs and halluces are either hypoplastic or absent. The intermediate and distal phalanges of all fingers are duplicated, resulting in distal octadactyly. Occasional features include cleft palate, omphalocele, cardiac anomalies, and genitourinary defects including sex reversal. The radiographic features of this condition are largely supportive of it being a severe form of <i>FLNB-</i>AO1.</p></div><div id="flnb-dis.FLNBRelated_Apparently_Isolated_1"><h4><i>FLNB</i>-Related Apparently Isolated Clubfoot</h4><p>Clubfoot is the most common <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/congenital/">congenital</a> malformation, affecting 1-2 in 1,000 live births, with <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/multifactorial/">multifactorial</a> causes including genetic and environmental factors. Pathogenic variants in <i>FLNB</i> (one of them recurrent [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.quiggle.2022.421">Quiggle et al 2022</a>]) have been associated with apparently <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/isolated/">isolated</a> clubfoot in two recent studies involving a small number of individuals from a large cohort with this <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a> [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.yang.2016.1181">Yang et al 2016</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.quiggle.2022.421">Quiggle et al 2022</a>]. These observations may represent minimal manifestations of a spectrum that extends to <i>FLNB-</i>LS, and a deeper study of the phenotype of these individuals is required to address this question.</p></div></div><div id="flnb-dis.GenotypePhenotype_Correlations"><h3>Genotype-Phenotype Correlations</h3><p><b><i>FLNB</i>-SCT.</b> Homozygosity or compound heterozygosity for pathogenic frameshift or <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/nonsense-variant/">nonsense</a> variants in <i>FLNB</i> causes <i>FLNB</i>-SCT [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.krakow.2004.405">Krakow et al 2004</a>]. Pathogenic variants associated with <i>FLNB</i>-SCT are associated with loss of protein expression and hence constitute true <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/null/">null</a> alleles [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.farringtonrock.2006.705">Farrington-Rock et al 2006</a>]. Consequently, no <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/genotype/">genotype</a>-<a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a> association has been described.</p><p><b><i>FLNB</i>-LS, <i>FLNB</i>-AO1, and <i>FLNB</i>-AO3.</b> The <i>FLNB</i> pathogenic variants associated with LS, AO1, and AO3 are either <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/missense/">missense</a> variants or small <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/in-frame/">in-frame</a> deletions and are predicted to encode full-length filamin-B protein.</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>LS-associated <i>FLNB</i> pathogenic variants are spread predominantly across exons 2-5 and 27-33 [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.bicknell.2007.89">Bicknell et al 2007</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.daniel.2012.665">Daniel et al 2012</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.girisha.2016.27">Girisha et al 2016</a>].</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>AO3-causing <i>FLNB</i> pathogenic variants occur in exons 2-5, 13, and 27-33 [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.farringtonrock.2006.705">Farrington-Rock et al 2006</a>].</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>The large majority of <i>FLNB</i> pathogenic variants reported in individuals with <i>FLNB-</i>AO1 are in exons 2-5 [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.bicknell.2005.e43">Bicknell et al 2005</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.daniel.2012.665">Daniel et al 2012</a>]. Three individuals with Piepkorn dysplasia have had pathogenic variants in exons 28 and 29.</div></li></ul><p>In some instances, the same <i>FLNB</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> is associated with different phenotypes (e.g., <a href="/books/NBK2534/table/flnb-dis.T.flnb_pathogenic_variants_refe/?report=objectonly" target="object" rid-ob="figobflnbdisTflnbpathogenicvariantsrefe">c.502G&#x0003e;A [p.Gly168Ser]</a> is associated with both AO1 and AO3).</p><p><b>Recurrent</b>
<i>FLNB</i> pathogenic variants:</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div><a href="/books/NBK2534/table/flnb-dis.T.flnb_pathogenic_variants_refe/?report=objectonly" target="object" rid-ob="figobflnbdisTflnbpathogenicvariantsrefe">c.5071G&#x0003e;A (p.Gly1691Ser)</a> is the most common recurrent substitution, associated with phenotypes ranging from mild <i>FLNB</i>-LS (<a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/isolated/">isolated</a> bilateral dislocation of the knees with digital and craniofacial anomalies) to <i>FLNB</i>-AO3 [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.bicknell.2005.e43">Bicknell et al 2005</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.farringtonrock.2006.705">Farrington-Rock et al 2006</a>].</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div><a href="/books/NBK2534/table/flnb-dis.T.flnb_pathogenic_variants_refe/?report=objectonly" target="object" rid-ob="figobflnbdisTflnbpathogenicvariantsrefe">c.679G&#x0003e;A (p.Glu227Lys)</a> is associated with <i>FLNB</i>-LS.</div></li></ul><div id="flnb-dis.Somatic_Mosaicism"><h4>Somatic Mosaicism</h4><p>Clinical evidence suggests that <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/somatic-mosaicism/">somatic mosaicism</a> can complicate the presentation of these conditions [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.petrella.1993.187">Petrella et al 1993</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.bicknell.2007.89">Bicknell et al 2007</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.bernkopf.2017.1360">Bernkopf et al 2017</a>]. Somatic mosaicism for an <i>FLNB</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> can result in an asymmetric or milder clinical presentation suggestive of LS in a mosaic parent, whereas the same pathogenic variant in the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/germline/">germline</a> state can be associated with AO1, leading to perinatal lethality [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.meira.2018.570">Meira et al 2018</a>].</p></div></div><div id="flnb-dis.Penetrance"><h3>Penetrance</h3><p>Germline <i>FLNB</i> pathogenic variants associated with <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/syndromic/">syndromic</a> <i>FLNB-</i>related disorders are fully penetrant but show <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/variable-expressivity/">variable expressivity</a>, leading to the range of phenotypes described in this <i>GeneReview</i>. Although most individuals with <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/idiopathic/">idiopathic</a> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/congenital/">congenital</a> clubfoot presented with the malformation in isolation, and some kindreds seemed to indicate reduced <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/penetrance/">penetrance</a> [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.yang.2016.1181">Yang et al 2016</a>], in some individuals, subtle phenotypic signs &#x02013; such as elbow and thumb hypermobility, as well as wide, flat thumbs &#x02013; have been identified that suggest mild manifestations within the clinical spectrum of LS [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.quiggle.2022.421">Quiggle et al 2022</a>].</p></div><div id="flnb-dis.Nomenclature"><h3>Nomenclature</h3><p><b>Larsen syndrome.</b> Some authors have described what they termed "<a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/autosomal-recessive/">autosomal recessive</a> Larsen syndrome" [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.claytonsmith.1988.499">Clayton-Smith &#x00026; Donnai 1988</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.bonaventure.1992.465">Bonaventure et al 1992</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.laville.1994.63">Laville et al 1994</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.yamaguchi.1996.75">Yamaguchi et al 1996</a>]; however, these disorders are phenotypically heterogeneous and are not typical of the <i>FLNB-</i>related <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a> [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.topley.1994.263">Topley et al 1994</a>]. The conditions (which likely include a variety of chondrodysplasias with multiple joint dislocations) include: the "Reunion Island form of Larsen syndrome" [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.bonaventure.1992.465">Bonaventure et al 1992</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.laville.1994.63">Laville et al 1994</a>], which is clinically and radiographically distinct from <i>FLNB</i>-LS and is caused by pathogenic variants in <i>B4GALT7</i> [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.cartault.2015.49">Cartault et al 2015</a>]; <a href="/books/n/gene/cd-chst3/"><i>CHST3</i>-related chondrodysplasia with congenital joint dislocations</a>; and two different forms of Desbuquois dysplasia caused by pathogenic variants in <i>CANT1</i> and <i>XYLT1</i>.</p><p><b>Atelosteogenesis types 1 and 3</b> were so named because the major manifestation was disordered and incomplete ossification of the skeleton [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.maroteaux.1982.15">Maroteaux et al 1982</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.sillence.1982.7">Sillence et al 1982</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.stern.1990.183">Stern et al 1990</a>].</p><p>Note: Atelosteogenesis type 2, one of the sulfate transporter-related osteochondrodysplasias caused by pathogenic variants in <i>SLC26A2</i> (<i>DTDST</i>), is genetically distinct from <i>FLNB</i>-AO1 and <i>FLNB</i>-AO3 (see <a href="/books/n/gene/ao2/"><i>SLC26A2</i>-Related Atelosteogenesis</a>).</p><p><b>Piepkorn dysplasia,</b> formerly considered to be the same disorder as boomerang dysplasia, has been readdressed by <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.rehder.2018.1559">Rehder et al [2018]</a>, who described a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a> that was more severe but with significant overlap with <i>FLNB</i>-AO1. This severe phenotype included flipper-like limbs, a characteristic form of synpolydactyly, and completely absent ossification of many skeletal elements at mid-gestation.</p><p><b>Boomerang dysplasia</b> was so named due to the distinctive bowing of the femora in the original descriptions. The definition of its pathogenesis has confirmed that this condition is best subsumed under the <i>FLNB-</i>AO1 spectrum.</p></div><div id="flnb-dis.Prevalence"><h3>Prevalence</h3><p>No prevalence figures are available for any of the <i>FLNB-</i>related conditions. To date, more than 100 individuals with a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a>(s) in <i>FLNB</i> have been identified [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.daniel.2012.665">Daniel et al 2012</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.girisha.2016.27">Girisha et al 2016</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.salian.2018.159">Salian et al 2018</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.stenson.2020.1197">Stenson et al 2020</a>].</p></div></div><div id="flnb-dis.Genetically_Related_Allelic_Dis"><h2 id="_flnb-dis_Genetically_Related_Allelic_Dis_">Genetically Related (Allelic) Disorders</h2><p>No mendelian phenotypes other than those discussed in this <i>GeneReview</i> are known to be associated with <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/germline/">germline</a> pathogenic variants in <i>FLNB</i>.</p><p>In one study, variants in <i>FLNB</i> were implicated as potential contributory factors in the context of a proposed <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/oligogenic/">oligogenic</a> etiology for some instances of adolescent <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/idiopathic/">idiopathic</a> scoliosis [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.jiang.2020.405">Jiang et al 2020</a>]. Corroborative studies are required to explore this proposition.</p></div><div id="flnb-dis.Differential_Diagnosis"><h2 id="_flnb-dis_Differential_Diagnosis_">Differential Note: There may not be clinical trials for this disorder.</p></div></div><div id="flnb-dis.Genetic_Counseling"><h2 id="_flnb-dis_Genetic_Counseling_">Genetic Counseling</h2><p>
<i>Genetic counseling is the process of providing individuals and families with
information on the nature, mode(s) of inheritance, and implications of genetic disorders to help them
make informed medical and personal decisions. The following section deals with genetic
risk assessment and the use of family history and genetic testing to clarify genetic
status for family members; it is not meant to address all personal, cultural, or
ethical issues that may arise or to substitute for consultation with a genetics
professional</i>. &#x02014;ED.</p><div id="flnb-dis.Mode_of_Inheritance"><h3>Mode of Inheritance</h3><p><i>FLNB-</i>related spondylocarpotarsal synostosis syndrome (<i>FLNB</i>-SCT) is caused by <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/biallelic/">biallelic</a> <i>FLNB</i> pathogenic variants and is inherited in an <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/autosomal-recessive/">autosomal recessive</a> manner.</p><p><i>FLNB-</i>related Larsen syndrome (<i>FLNB-</i>LS), <i>FLNB-</i>related atelosteogenesis type 1 (<i>FLNB-</i>AO1), <i>FLNB-</i>related atelosteogenesis type 3 (<i>FLNB-</i>AO3), and <i>FLNB-</i>related apparently <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/isolated/">isolated</a> clubfoot are inherited in an <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/autosomal-dominant/">autosomal dominant</a> manner. Note: Comparatively mild (e.g., <i>FLNB</i>-LS) and severe (e.g., <i>FLNB-</i>AO3) forms of the autosomal dominant <i>FLNB</i>-related disorders can occur in the same family.</p></div><div id="flnb-dis.Autosomal_Recessive_Inheritance"><h3>Autosomal Recessive Inheritance &#x02013; Risk to Family Members</h3><p>
<b>Parents of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a></b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>The parents of a child with <i>FLNB</i>-SCT are presumed to be <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a> for an <i>FLNB</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a>.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Molecular genetic testing is recommended for the parents of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> to confirm that both parents are <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a> for an <i>FLNB</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> and to allow reliable <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/recurrence-risk/">recurrence risk</a> assessment.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>If a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> is detected in only one parent and parental identity testing has confirmed biological maternity and paternity, it is possible that one of the pathogenic variants identified in the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> occurred as a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/de-novo/"><i>de novo</i></a> event in the proband or as a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/postzygotic/">postzygotic</a> <i>de novo</i> event in a mosaic parent [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.j_nsson.2017.519">J&#x000f3;nsson et al 2017</a>]. If the proband appears to have <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/homozygous/">homozygous</a> pathogenic variants (i.e., the same two pathogenic variants), additional possibilities to consider include:</div><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>A single- or multi-<a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/exon/">exon</a> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/deletion/">deletion</a> in the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> that was not detected by <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/sequence-analysis/">sequence analysis</a> and resulted in the artifactual appearance of homozygosity;</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Uniparental isodisomy for the parental <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/chromosome/">chromosome</a> with the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> that resulted in homozygosity for the pathogenic variant in the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a>.</div></li></ul></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Heterozygous parents of a child with <i>FLNB</i>-SCT can exhibit mild reductions in stature but no other medically significant phenotypic manifestations. One report described a parent of a child with typical <i>FLNB</i>-SCT who had a height 2.2 standard deviations (SD) below the mean and mild unilateral hip dysplasia.</div></li></ul><p>
<b>Sibs of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a></b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>If both parents are known to be <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a> for an <i>FLNB</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a>, each sib of an affected individual has at conception a 25% chance of inheriting <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/biallelic/">biallelic</a> pathogenic variants and being affected, a 50% chance of inheriting one pathogenic variant and being heterozygous, and a 25% chance of inheriting neither of the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/familial/">familial</a> <i>FLNB</i> pathogenic variants.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Heterozygous sibs of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> with <i>FLNB</i>-SCT can exhibit mild reductions in stature but no other medically significant phenotypic manifestations.</div></li></ul><p><b>Offspring of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a>.</b> The offspring of an individual with <i>FLNB</i>-SCT are obligate heterozygotes for a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> in <i>FLNB</i>.</p><p><b>Other family members.</b> Each sib of the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a>&#x02019;s parents is at 50% risk of being <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a> for an <i>FLNB</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a>.</p><p><b>Heterozygote detection.</b> Heterozygote testing for at-risk relatives of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> with <i>FLNB</i>-SCT requires prior identification of the <i>FLNB</i> pathogenic variants in the family.</p></div><div id="flnb-dis.Autosomal_Dominant_Inheritance"><h3>Autosomal Dominant Inheritance &#x02013; Risk to Family Members</h3><p>
<b>Parents of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a></b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div class="half_rhythm">Some individuals diagnosed with an <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/autosomal-dominant/">autosomal dominant</a> <i>FLNB-</i>related disorder have the disorder as the result of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> inherited from a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a> or mosaic parent.</div><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>The <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a> parent of an individual with <i>FLNB</i>-LS may have a milder or more severe <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a>. Significant clinical variability can be seen among heterozygous family members with <i>FLNB</i>-LS.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>The parent of an individual with <i>FLNB</i>-AO1 or <i>FLNB</i>-AO3 may have a milder <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a> associated with <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/somatic-mosaicism/">somatic mosaicism</a> [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.meira.2018.570">Meira et al 2018</a>]. Somatic mosaicism for an <i>FLNB</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> can be associated with <i>FLNB</i>-LS in a mosaic parent, whereas the same pathogenic variant in the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/constitutional/">constitutional</a> state can be associated with a more severe phenotype such as <i>FLNB</i>-AO1 [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.meira.2018.570">Meira et al 2018</a>] or <i>FLNB</i>-AO3.</div></li></ul></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div class="half_rhythm">Some individuals diagnosed with an <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/autosomal-dominant/">autosomal dominant</a> <i>FLNB-</i>related disorder have the disorder as the result of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/de-novo/"><i>de novo</i></a> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a>. The vast majority of lethal <i>FLNB</i> conditions are the result of <i>de novo</i> pathogenic variants.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div class="half_rhythm">If the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> appears to be the only affected family member (i.e., a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/simplex/">simplex</a> case), <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/molecular-genetic-testing/">molecular genetic testing</a> is recommended for the parents of the proband to evaluate their genetic status and inform <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/recurrence-risk/">recurrence risk</a> assessment. Detailed clinical examination (including radiographic examination if indicated) may also be recommended.</div><div class="half_rhythm">Note: A <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> may appear to be the only affected family member because of failure to recognize the disorder in family members and/or a milder phenotypic presentation. Therefore, <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/de-novo/"><i>de novo</i></a> occurrence of an <i>FLNB</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> cannot be confirmed unless <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/molecular-genetic-testing/">molecular genetic testing</a> has demonstrated that neither parent is <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a> for the <i>FLNB</i> pathogenic variant.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div class="half_rhythm">If the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> identified in the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> is not identified in either parent and parental identity testing has confirmed biological maternity and paternity, the following possibilities should be considered:</div><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div class="half_rhythm">The <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> has a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/de-novo/"><i>de novo</i></a> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a>.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div class="half_rhythm">The <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> inherited a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> from a parent with gonadal (or somatic and gonadal) <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/mosaicism/">mosaicism</a>.* Note: Testing of parental leukocyte DNA may not detect all instances of <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/somatic-mosaicism/">somatic mosaicism</a> and will not detect a pathogenic variant that is present in the germ (gonadal) cells only.</div><div class="half_rhythm">*&#x000a0;A parent with somatic and <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gonadal-mosaicism/">gonadal mosaicism</a> for an <i>FLNB</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> may be mildly/minimally affected (see Clinical Description, <a href="#flnb-dis.Somatic_Mosaicism">Somatic Mosaicism</a>).</div></li></ul></li></ul><p><b>Sibs of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a>.</b> The risk to the sibs of the proband depends on the genetic status of the proband's parents:</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>If a parent of the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> is affected and/or is known to have the <i>FLNB</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> identified in the proband, the risk to the sibs is 50%. Note: Significant intrafamilial clinical variability is observed in <i>FLNB</i>-LS; a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a> sib may have milder or more severe manifestations of the disorder than the proband.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Clinical evidence suggests that <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/somatic-mosaicism/">somatic mosaicism</a> can complicate the presentation and recurrence risks associated with <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/autosomal-dominant/">autosomal dominant</a> <i>FLNB-</i>related disorders [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.bernkopf.2017.1360">Bernkopf et al 2017</a>]. Most notably, the presentation of a typical <i>FLNB</i>-LS <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a> in a parent can result from mosaicism for a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> that, when present as a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/constitutional/">constitutional</a> pathogenic variant in a child, leads to the <i>FLNB</i>-AO3 phenotype.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>If the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> identified in the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> cannot be detected in the leukocyte DNA of either parent and/or the parents appear to be clinically unaffected based on detailed clinical examination, the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/recurrence-risk/">recurrence risk</a> to sibs is slightly greater than that of the general population because of the risk of parental gonadal (or somatic and gonadal) <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/mosaicism/">mosaicism</a> [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.meira.2018.570">Meira et al 2018</a>].</div></li></ul><p>
<b>Offspring of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a></b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Each child of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> who is <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a> for an <i>FLNB</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> has a 50% chance of inheriting the pathogenic variant.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Each child of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> with <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/somatic-mosaicism/">somatic mosaicism</a> for an <i>FLNB</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> has up to a 50% chance of inheriting the pathogenic variant. Offspring who inherit an <i>FLNB</i> pathogenic variant from a proband with somatic mosaicism may be more severely affected than the proband (see Clinical Description, <a href="#flnb-dis.Somatic_Mosaicism">Somatic Mosaicism</a>). One approach for the assessment of <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/recurrence-risk/">recurrence risk</a> in offspring of a male proband with an <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/autosomal-dominant/">autosomal dominant</a> <i>FLNB</i>-related disorder who is known to have somatic mosaicism is to measure the degree of <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gonadal-mosaicism/">gonadal mosaicism</a> in sperm [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.bernkopf.2017.1360">Bernkopf et al 2017</a>].</div></li></ul><p><b>Other family members.</b> The risk to other family members depends on the genetic status of the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a>'s parents: if a parent has the <i>FLNB</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a>, the parent's family members may be at risk.</p></div><div id="flnb-dis.Related_Genetic_Counseling_Issu"><h3>Related Genetic Counseling Issues</h3><p>
<b>Family planning</b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>The optimal time for determination of genetic risk and discussion of the availability of prenatal/<a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/preimplantation-genetic-testing/">preimplantation genetic testing</a> is before pregnancy.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>It is appropriate to offer <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/genetic-counseling/">genetic counseling</a> (including discussion of potential risks to offspring and reproductive options) to young adults who are affected or at risk.</div></li></ul></div><div id="flnb-dis.Prenatal_Testing_and_Preimplant"><h3>Prenatal Testing and Preimplantation Genetic Testing</h3><p><b>A priori high-risk pregnancies.</b> Once the <i>FLNB</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a>(s) have been identified in an affected family member, prenatal and <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/preimplantation-genetic-testing/">preimplantation genetic testing</a> are possible.</p><p><b>A priori low-risk pregnancies.</b> Routine prenatal ultrasound examination may identify skeletal findings such as limb changes consistent with multiple joint dislocations that raise the possibility of <i>FLNB</i>-LS in a fetus not known to be at increased risk. Detection of fetuses with <i>FLNB</i>-AO1 by ultrasound examination during the second trimester is possible because of the multiple anomalies present including shortening of the limbs and thoracic hypoplasia [<a class="bk_pop" href="#flnb-dis.REF.tsutsumi.2012.216">Tsutsumi et al 2012</a>]. Consideration of <i>FLNB</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/molecular-genetic-testing/">molecular genetic testing</a> in these situations is appropriate.</p><p>Note: Delivery of an affected infant has the potential to be complicated by extended breech presentation due to dislocation of the hips and knees.</p><p>Differences in perspective may exist among medical professionals and within families regarding the use of prenatal and <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/preimplantation-genetic-testing/">preimplantation genetic testing</a>. While most health care professionals would consider use of prenatal and preimplantation genetic testing to be a personal decision, discussion of these issues may be helpful.</p></div></div><div id="flnb-dis.Resources"><h2 id="_flnb-dis_Resources_">Resources</h2><p>
