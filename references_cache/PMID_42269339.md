Preeclampsia (PE) is a complex pregnancy disorder. It is characterized by 
hypertension and multi-organ dysfunction, significantly contributing to maternal 
and fetal morbidity and mortality. Recent studies underscore the important 
contribution of NOD-like receptors (NLRs), particularly the NLRP3 inflammasome, 
to PE development. This review synthesizes the most recent data on NLRs in PE, 
focusing on their role in disease genesis, pathogenic processes, biomarker 
potential, and therapeutic consequences. Key characteristics of PE include 
dysregulated NLR activity, excessive inflammation, oxidative stress, endothelial 
dysfunction, and impaired trophoblast invasion. The genetic and epigenetic 
control of NLRs-as well as their modulation by non-coding RNAs-further clarifies 
their relevance in PE. New treatment techniques targeting NLR pathways are also 
being explored, including small-molecule inhibitors, antioxidant approaches, and 
microRNA-based interventions. The potential of NLR-related biomarkers for early 
diagnosis and risk stratification is also under investigation. Despite 
encouraging preclinical and translational research, validating NLR-targeted 
treatments for clinical use remains difficult. Future studies should focus on 
developing treatments to improve maternal and fetal outcomes and on 
incorporating NLR-based biomarkers into multi-omics systems.
