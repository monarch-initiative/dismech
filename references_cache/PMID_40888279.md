Membranous nephropathy (MN), an autoimmune cause of adult nephrotic syndrome, is
driven by podocyte-targeting antibodies against PLA2R/THSD7A. Current models
fail to fully capture human disease progression. This review evaluates three
transformative approaches: (1) Heterologous antibody-induced models enabling
acute injury replication; (2) Antigen-driven immunization modeling adaptive
immunity; and (3) GBF-on-Chip platforms mimicking filtration barrier dynamics.
Collectively, they reveal complement-dependent and direct podocytotoxic injury
mechanisms. While antibody-induced models offer rapid injury induction and high
reproducibility, their transient phenotype cannot model chronic progression or
immune tolerance breakdown. Antigen-driven models recapitulate adaptive immunity
but face prolonged timelines and epitope targeting bias diverging from human
IgG4 dominance. GFB-on-Chip systems excel in mechanistic dissection of podocyte
injury but lack immune microenvironment integration and physiologically accurate
glomerular architecture. This review synthesizes strategies for MN model
development through antibody-podocyte interaction studies, critically evaluates
the strengths of existing platforms, and discusses emerging technologies for
probing disease mechanisms and accelerating therapeutic discovery.

Plain Language Summary: Membranous nephropathy (MN) is a chronic kidney disease
where the immune system mistakenly attacks specific proteins (PLA2R and THSD7A)
in the kidney’s filtering units, causing protein leakage into urine. This review
explains how scientists are using innovative tools to study the disease and
develop treatments.Our approach We have summarized advanced modeling strategies:
Animal Models: Injecting antibodies or antigens similar to those found in MN
patients into animals recreates key disease features, such as proteinuria and
kidney damage. Kidney-on-a-Chip: A miniature device built with human cells
mimics the kidney’s filter. When exposed to MN patient blood, the chip
“malfunctions” like real kidneys, showing how antibodies trigger injury.Key
findings New MN models closely mimic the disease process, helping identify why
the immune system goes awry and what damages the kidneys.The kidney-on-a-chip
accurately predicts patient-specific damage, serving as a powerful tool to study
mechanisms and test potential drugs.Why it matters Current MN treatments often
suppress the entire immune system, causing harmful side effects. Our work
demonstrates how these models can guide safer therapies—like targeted drugs that
block only harmful immune responses. In the future, personalized treatments
could be tested on patient-derived kidney chips, improving outcomes for those
with MN.
