itemprop="description"><h2 id="_sost_Summary_">Summary</h2><div><h4 class="inline">Clinical characteristics.</h4><p><i>SOST</i>-related sclerosing bone dysplasias include <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosteosis and <i>SOST-</i>related endosteal hyperostosis, van Buchem type (van Buchem disease), both disorders of progressive bone overgrowth due to increased bone formation.</p><p>The major clinical features of <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosteosis are progressive skeletal overgrowth, most pronounced in the skull and mandible, and variable syndactyly, usually of the second (index) and third (middle) fingers. Affected individuals appear normal at birth except for syndactyly. Facial distortion due to frontal bossing and mandibular overgrowth is seen in nearly all individuals and becomes apparent in early childhood with progression into adulthood. Hyperostosis of the skull results in narrowing of the foramina, causing entrapment of the seventh cranial nerve (leading to facial palsy) with other, less common nerve entrapment syndromes including visual loss (2nd cranial nerve), neuralgia or anosmia (5th cranial nerve), and sensorineural hearing loss (8th cranial nerve). In <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosteosis, hyperostosis of the calvarium reduces intracranial volume, increasing the risk for potentially lethal elevation of intracranial pressure. Survival of individuals with <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosteosis into old age is unusual but not unprecedented.</p><p>The manifestations of van Buchem disease are generally milder than <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosteosis. Stature is typically normal, cranial nerve entrapment of the seventh and eighth cranial nerves are common, and increased intracranial pressure is rare, seen only in severely affected individuals. Individuals with van Buchem disease do not have syndactyly or other digit deformities. Life span appears not to be altered.</p></div><div><h4 class="inline">Diagnosis/testing.</h4><p>The diagnosis of a <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosing bone dysplasia is established in a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> with typical clinical and radiographic findings and <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/biallelic/">biallelic</a> pathogenic variants in <i>SOST</i> (in individuals with <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosteosis) or a biallelic 52-kb <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/deletion/">deletion</a> downstream of <i>SOST</i> (in individuals with van Buchem disease) identified on <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/molecular-genetic-testing/">molecular genetic testing</a>.</p></div><div><h4 class="inline">Management.</h4><p><i>Treatment of manifestations:</i> Hearing aids with middle ear surgery or cochlear implant depending on the nature of the hearing loss; surgical decompression as needed for entrapped facial nerves; surgical reduction of mandibular overgrowth; orbital decompression for proptosis; treatment of dental manifestations per orthodontist and/or craniofacial team; craniectomy and ventriculoperitoneal drain for increased intracranial pressure; surgical correction of syndactyly.</p><p><i>Surveillance:</i> Intermittent assessment of bone mineral density and biochemical markers of bone turnover until age 18 years and then every five years in adulthood; annual audiologic assessment in childhood and as needed in adults; examination for evidence of cranial nerve entrapment and increased intracranial pressure every six months until age 18 years and then annually in adulthood; annual ophthalmology examination to assess for proptosis, intraocular pressure, and evaluation of the optic nerve papilla; annual dental and orthodontic evaluation to assess for tooth malalignment and malocclusion until age 18 years; routine dental screening in adults.</p><p><i>Agents to avoid:</i> Agents known to suppress bone resorption (e.g., bisphosphonates, denosumab, selective estrogen receptor modulators) and agents known to stimulate bone formation (e.g., teriparatide, abaloparatide, romozosumab).</p></div><div><h4 class="inline">Genetic counseling.</h4><p><i>SOST</i>-related sclerosing bone dysplasias are inherited in an <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/autosomal-recessive/">autosomal recessive</a> manner. If both parents are known to be <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a> for a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> associated with a <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosing bone dysplasia, each sib of an affected individual has at conception a 25% chance of being affected, a 50% chance of being heterozygous, and a 25% chance of inheriting neither of the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/familial/">familial</a> pathogenic variants. Once the pathogenic variants associated with a <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosing bone dysplasia have been identified in an affected family member, <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/carrier-testing/">carrier testing</a> for at-risk family members and prenatal/<a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/preimplantation-genetic-testing/">preimplantation genetic testing</a> are possible.</p></div></div><div id="sost.GeneReview_Scope"><h2 id="_sost_GeneReview_Scope_"><i>GeneReview</i> Scope</h2><div id="sost.Td" class="table"><p class="large-table-link" style="display:none"><span class="right"><a href="/books/NBK587318/table/sost.Td/?report=objectonly" target="object">View in own window</a></span></p><div class="large_tbl" id="__sost.Td_lrgtbl__"><table class="no_bottom_margin"><thead><tr><th id="hd_h_sost.Td_1_1_1_1" scope="col" rowspan="1" colspan="1" style="text-align:left;vertical-align:middle;"><i>SOST</i>-Related Sclerosing Bone Dysplasias: Included Phenotypes</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td headers="hd_h_sost.Td_1_1_1_1" rowspan="1" colspan="1" style="text-align:left;vertical-align:middle;"><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div><i>SOST</i>-related sclerosteosis&#x000a0;<sup>1</sup></div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div><i>SOST-</i>related endosteal hyperostosis, van Buchem type (van Buchem disease)</div></li></ul>

The disorders differ in severity and in type of molecular genetic variants.</p><div id="sost.Suggestive_Findings"><h3>Suggestive Findings</h3><p><i>SOST</i>-related sclerosing bone dysplasias <b>should be suspected</b> in individuals with the following findings.</p><p>
<b>Clinical findings</b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Generalized progressive skeletal overgrowth, most pronounced in the skull and mandible, leading to:</div><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Potentially lethal elevation of intracranial pressure in childhood or early adulthood as a result of calvarial overgrowth;</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Entrapment of the seventh cranial nerve leading to recurrent facial palsy that is initially intermittent and eventually constant, resulting in impaired facial movements in adulthood;</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Conductive hearing loss in childhood followed by additional entrapment of the eighth cranial nerve and closure of the oval and round windows, leading to sensorineural hearing loss in adulthood;</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Distortion of the face with asymmetric mandibular hypertrophy, frontal bossing, midface hypoplasia, or proptosis;</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Tall stature with accelerated bone growth beginning in childhood.</div></li></ul></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Variable cutaneous or bony syndactyly of fingers two (index) and three (middle), and occasionally fingers three and other fingers. The syndactyly is usually bilateral but not necessarily symmetric. (Note: Syndactyly is not present in individuals with van Buchem disease.)</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Radial deviation of the terminal phalanges</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Dysplastic or absent nails</div></li></ul><p>
<b>Radiographic findings</b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Widening (hyperostosis) and increased density (sclerosis) of the calvarium, the base of the skull, and the shafts of the tubular bones</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Undermodeling of the shafts of the tubular bones of the metacarpals and phalanges</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Broad and dense clavicles and ribs</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Sclerosis of the scapulae and pelvis without an increase in size</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>High bone mineral density (z score &#x0003e;5 standard deviations above the mean) measured by dual-energy x-ray absorptiometry [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.van_lierop.2017.51">van Lierop et al 2017</a>]</div></li></ul><p><b>Family history</b> is consistent with <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/autosomal-recessive/">autosomal recessive</a> inheritance (e.g., affected sibs and/or parental <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/consanguinity/">consanguinity</a>). Absence of a known family history does not preclude the diagnosis.</p><p>Note: The majority of persons affected with <b><i>SOST</i>-related sclerosteosis</b> are of the <b>Afrikaner (Dutch ancestry) population of South Africa</b>. Within this population the diagnosis should be suspected in any neonate with syndactyly, or in the presence of fluctuating facial palsy. <b>Van Buchem disease</b> is almost exclusively found in individuals from the <b>Netherlands</b>.</p></div><div id="sost.Establishing_the_Diagnosis"><h3>Establishing the Diagnosis</h3><p>The diagnosis of a <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosing bone dysplasia <b>is established</b> in a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> with typical clinical and radiographic findings and <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/biallelic/">biallelic</a> pathogenic (or <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/likely-pathogenic/">likely pathogenic</a>) variants in <i>SOST</i> (in individuals with <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosteosis) or a biallelic 52-kb <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/deletion/">deletion</a> downstream of <i>SOST</i> (in individuals with van Buchem disease) identified on <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/molecular-genetic-testing/">molecular genetic testing</a> (see <a href="/books/NBK587318/table/sost.T.molecular_genetic_testing_used_in/?report=objectonly" target="object" rid-ob="figobsostTmoleculargenetictestingusedin">Table 1</a>).</p><p>Note: (1) Per ACMG/AMP variant interpretation guidelines, the terms "<a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a>" and "<a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/likely-pathogenic/">likely pathogenic</a> variant" are synonymous in a clinical setting, meaning that both are considered diagnostic and can be used for clinical decision making [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.richards.2015.405">Richards et al 2015</a>]. id="sost.Clinical_Features_of_SOSTRelated_Sc"><h4>Clinical Features of <i>SOST</i>-Related Sclerosteosis</h4><p><b>Tall stature.</b> Accelerated linear growth becomes evident in early childhood. Longitudinal growth arrests at puberty, by which time individuals can reach heights exceeding two meters (6.5 feet).</p><p><b>Hearing loss</b> is also highly prevalent, affecting 94% of individuals. It starts as conductive hearing loss in childhood, but often progresses into mixed conductive and sensorineural hearing loss later in life related to compression of the eighth cranial nerve [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.hamersma.2003.192">Hamersma et al 2003</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.van_lierop.2017.51">van Lierop et al 2017</a>].</p><p><b>Recurrent facial palsies</b> are hallmark complications in <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosteosis, affecting 93% of individuals. The first episodes develop in early childhood and in some individuals within the first months of life. The facial palsies are caused by narrowing of the neural foramina due to bone overgrowth of the skull.</p><p>Other, less common nerve entrapment syndromes in <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosteosis are visual loss (2nd cranial nerve), neuralgia or anosmia (5th cranial nerve), and sensorineural hearing loss (8th cranial nerve) [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.van_lierop.2017.51">van Lierop et al 2017</a>].</p><p><b>Facial distortion</b> due to severe cranial hyperostosis results in frontal bossing and mandibular overgrowth in 90% of individuals, with proptosis, hypertelorism, or midfacial hypoplasia seen in some individuals. These facial findings become apparent in early childhood and progress into adulthood [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.hamersma.2003.192">Hamersma et al 2003</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.van_lierop.2017.51">van Lierop et al 2017</a>].</p><p><b>Dental manifestations.</b> Teeth can be malaligned with malocclusion due to mandibular hyperostosis.</p><p><b>Increased intracranial pressure</b> can develop due to narrowing of the intracranial cavity by the thickening of calvaria. It often starts in late adolescence. In a recent study, it was reported in 71% of individuals with <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosteosis and was considered the cause of death in 12 of 33 deceased individuals of Afrikaner background, and six additional individuals died due to perioperative complications [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.van_lierop.2017.51">van Lierop et al 2017</a>].</p><p><b>Digit and nail abnormalities&#x000a0;/ syndactyly,</b> ranging from soft-tissue webbing to bony union of the phalanges, is found at birth in 66% of individuals. It most often affects the second and third fingers, although other fingers or toes can be affected as well. More subtle deformity of the digits can also be seen, such as radial deviation of the phalanges or nail aplasia [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.itin.2001.259">Itin et al 2001</a>].</p><p><b>Prognosis.</b> The complications of <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosteosis progress into adulthood but appear to stabilize in the third decade in the majority of affected individuals [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.van_lierop.2011.2804">van Lierop et al 2011</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.van_lierop.2013.848">van Lierop et al 2013</a>]. Survival into old age is unusual in <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosteosis but not unprecedented [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.barnard.1980.401">Barnard et al 1980</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.van_lierop.2011.2804">van Lierop et al 2011</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.van_lierop.2013.848">van Lierop et al 2013</a>]. Life expectancy is reduced because of sudden deaths due to herniation of the brain stem, or perioperative complications from surgery to correct increased intracranial pressure. Mean age of death is 33 years [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.hamersma.2003.192">Hamersma et al 2003</a>], but with increasing use of early craniectomy, longer-term survival is likely. The natural history of <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosteosis has been reviewed in various reports, most recently in <a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.van_lierop.2017.51">van Lierop et al [2017]</a>.</p></div><div id="sost.Clinical_Features_of_Van_Buchem_Dis"><h4>Clinical Features of Van Buchem Disease</h4><p><b>Stature</b> is typically normal in individuals with van Buchem disease; linear overgrowth is not typical.</p><p><b>Hearing loss</b> is conductive and sensorineural with onset in childhood.</p><p><b>Cranial nerve</b> entrapment syndromes due to bone overgrowth of the skull such as visual loss (2nd cranial nerve), neuralgia or anosmia (5th cranial nerve), facial palsy (7th cranial nerve), and sensorineural hearing loss (8th cranial nerve) can occur but are less common than in <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosteosis.</p><p><b>Cranial hyperostosis</b> typically only manifests as mandibular overgrowth, which becomes apparent in young adulthood. Teeth are not typically affected.</p><p><b>Increased intracranial pressure</b> is rare in individuals with van Buchem disease [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.van_lierop.2010.2796">van Lierop et al 2010</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.van_lierop.2013.848">van Lierop et al 2013</a>]. Sudden death due to herniation of the brain stem has never been reported in individuals with van Buchem disease.</p><p><b>Digit and nails</b> typically appear normal without syndactyly. Mild distortion of tubular bones of hands and feet can be seen on radiographs.</p><p><b>Prognosis.</b> Van Buchem disease also tends to stabilize in adulthood [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.van_lierop.2013.848">van Lierop et al 2013</a>]. Life expectancy in van Buchem disease appears to be normal, and reported individuals have had no significant comorbidities. The oldest individual to be studied was age 81 years with diabetes mellitus type 2, mild heart failure, and non-metastasized prostate cancer, comorbidities frequent in elderly populations [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.van_lierop.2017.51">van Lierop et al 2017</a>].</p></div><div id="sost.Laboratory_Tests"><h4>Laboratory Tests</h4><p><b>Sclerostin.</b> Serum concentration of sclerostin is undetectable in individuals with <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosteosis [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.van_lierop.2011.2804">van Lierop et al 2011</a>], but low serum sclerostin concentration can be detected in individuals with van Buchem disease [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.van_lierop.2013.848">van Lierop et al 2013</a>].</p><p><b>Bone formation markers.</b> Serum concentration of bone formation markers, such as procollagen type 1 amino terminal propeptide (P1NP), alkaline phosphatase, or osteocalcin, are elevated in individuals with <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosteosis and van Buchem disease. Levels decline with age but remain elevated above the upper limit of normal in the majority of individuals [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.wergedal.2003.5778">Wergedal et al 2003</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.van_lierop.2011.2804">van Lierop et al 2011</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.van_lierop.2013.848">van Lierop et al 2013</a>].</p><p><b>Bone resorption markers.</b> Serum concentration of the bone resorption marker type I collagen cross-linked C-terminal telopeptide (CTX) is increased in childhood, but concentration decreases with age toward the lower end of the reference range in adulthood [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.van_lierop.2011.2804">van Lierop et al 2011</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.van_lierop.2013.848">van Lierop et al 2013</a>]. Urinary concentration of type I collagen cross-linked N-terminal telopeptide (NTX) was elevated in six individuals with van Buchem disease [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.wergedal.2003.5778">Wergedal et al 2003</a>].</p><p><b>Normal findings.</b> Serum calcium, phosphorus, and parathyroid hormone concentrations are normal [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.epstein.1979.1105">Epstein et al 1979</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.van_lierop.2011.2804">van Lierop et al 2011</a>].</p></div><div id="sost.Bone_Findings"><h4>Bone Findings</h4><p>Bone mineral density measured by dual-energy x-ray absorptiometry (DXA) is greatly increased, with z scores ranging from 7.7 to 14.4 standard deviations (SD) above the mean at the spine and 7.8 to 11.5 SD above the mean at the hip in individuals with <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosteosis [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.balemans.2005.943">Balemans et al 2005</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.piters.2010.e1526">Piters et al 2010</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.power.2010.1867">Power et al 2010</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.van_lierop.2011.2804">van Lierop et al 2011</a>], and from 5.4 to 12.3 SD above the mean at the spine and 5.2 to 12.1 SD above the mean at the hip in individuals with van Buchem disease [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.van_lierop.2013.848">van Lierop et al 2013</a>].</p><p>Histologic examination of bone reveals increased bone volume and thickness of cortex and trabeculae, increased osteoblastic bone formation with normal or decreased osteoclastic bone resorption, and no abnormal mineralization of bone tissue [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.stein.1983.267">Stein et al 1983</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.hassler.2014.2144">Hassler et al 2014</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.van_lierop.2017.51">van Lierop et al 2017</a>].</p><p>The high bone density in <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosteosis is not associated with increased mineralization [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.hassler.2014.2144">Hassler et al 2014</a>], as is seen in osteopetrosis, but there is an increased biomechanical competence of the bone and resistance to fractures [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.van_lierop.2017.51">van Lierop et al 2017</a>].</p><p>The risk for fractures, osteomyelitis, or bone marrow failure is not increased.</p></div></div><div id="sost.GenotypePhenotype_Correlations"><h3>Genotype-Phenotype Correlations</h3><p>There is no apparent difference in <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a> associated with any of the known <i>SOST</i> pathogenic variants.</p><p>The <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a> of van Buchem disease, which is caused by a <b>52-kb <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/deletion/">deletion</a> downstream of <i>SOST</i></b>, is milder than that of <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosteosis.</p></div><div id="sost.Nomenclature"><h3>Nomenclature</h3><p>In the 2023 revision of the Nosology of Genetic Skeletal Disorders, the <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosing bone dysplasias are included in the osteosclerotic disorders group, and van Buchem disease is referred to as <i>SOST-</i>related endosteal hyperostosis, van Buchem type [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.unger.2023.1164">Unger et al 2023</a>].</p><p>In the past, sclerosteosis and van Buchem disease have been grouped with other dense bone disorders under nonspecific general terms including "marble bones," "osteopetrosis," and "Albers-Sch&#x000f6;nberg disease." Diagnostic precision and <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/syndromic/">syndromic</a> delineation followed, and the term "sclerosteosis" became established. Similarly, van Buchem and his colleagues employed the designation "hyperostosis corticalis generalisata familiaris" for the condition that is now known as van Buchem disease.</p></div><div id="sost.Prevalence"><h3>Prevalence</h3><p><i>SOST</i>-related sclerosteosis is primarily found among the Afrikaner (Dutch ancestry) community of South Africa due to a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/founder-variant/">founder variant</a>; the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/carrier-rate/">carrier rate</a> is estimated at 1:100 and prevalence at 1:60,000 [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.beighton.1979.783">Beighton &#x00026; Hamersma 1979</a>]. However, individuals with <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosteosis outside the Afrikaner population have been reported [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.van_lierop.2017.51">van Lierop et al 2017</a>]. Founder variants have also been reported in individuals from Brazil and Turkey (see <a href="/books/NBK587318/table/sost.T.sost_pathogenic_variants_referenc/?report=objectonly" target="object" rid-ob="figobsostTsostpathogenicvariantsreferenc">Table 7</a>). With 100 individuals reported worldwide, of which 66 were from South Africa, <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosteosis is an extremely rare disease outside South Africa.</p><p>There have been only 31 reported individuals with van Buchem disease, of which 29 were from the Netherlands and two were from Germany [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.van_lierop.2017.51">van Lierop et al 2017</a>]. Three additional affected individuals are known to the authors [N Appelman-Dijkstra, personal observation].</p></div></div><div id="sost.Genetically_Related_Allelic_Disorde"><h2 id="_sost_Genetically_Related_Allelic_Disorde_">Genetically Related (Allelic) Disorders</h2><p><b><i>SOST</i>-related craniodiaphyseal dysplasia (CDD).</b> Autosomal dominant CDD (OMIM <a href="https://omim.org/entry/122860" ref="pagearea=body&amp;targetsite=external&amp;targetcat=link&amp;targettype=uri">122860</a>) caused by <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a> <i>SOST</i> pathogenic variants has been documented in two affected children in Korea and Poland [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.kim.2011.497">Kim et al 2011</a>]. Details of the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a> in the latter child were published previously [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.bieganski.2007.2435">Bieganski et al 2007</a>]. The main phenotypic features are progressive overgrowth of the craniofacial bones with deafness, facial palsy, and visual disturbance as a result of nerve entrapment. Choanal stenosis is a clinically significant complication. Radiologically, the cranial and facial bones are hyperostotic while the diaphyses of the long bones are expanded with thin cortices. In <i>SOST</i>-related CDD, <i>SOST</i> pathogenic variants are located in the secretion signal of the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gene/">gene</a> and prevent sclerostin secretion, possibly by a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/dominant-negative/">dominant-negative</a> mechanism [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.kim.2011.497">Kim et al 2011</a>]. To date, no other individuals with <i>SOST</i>-related CDD have been reported.</p></div><div id="sost.Differential_Diagnosis"><h2 id="_sost_Differential_Diagnosis_">Differential Diagnosis</h2><p><i>SOST</i>-related sclerosing bone dysplasias must be distinguished from other sclerosing bone dysplasias, including:</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>The osteoscleroses, notably osteopetrosis, characterized by increased bone density with no bone overgrowth and little or no disturbance of the contours of the bones;</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>The craniotubular dysplasias, characterized by abnormal modeling of the skeleton and moderate sclerosis of the calvarium and base of the skull.</div></li></ul><p>The predominant feature of the craniotubular hyperostoses is overgrowth of bone, which leads to alterations of contours and increase in radiologic density of the skeleton. The bones are often very resistant to trauma. Treatment of these disorders mainly consists of surgical intervention to ameliorate complications.</p><p>In one adult with severe van Buchem disease, treatment with glucocorticoids was successful in suppressing bone formation and disease progression [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.van_lierop.2010.2796">van Lierop et al 2010</a>], although the individual still needed repeated surgery [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.datema.2015.1215">Datema et al 2015</a>]. In two children with van Buchem disease short courses of prednisolone were given during exacerbations of facial palsies. While biochemical bone turnover markers decreased during therapy, there was no clinical improvement with steroid treatment in these individuals [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.van_egmond.2012.740">van Egmond et al 2012</a>] The long-term benefits of this treatment in <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosteosis or van Buchem disease have not been studied.</p><p>Note: The bones in individuals with <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosteosis are thick and dense; surgical intervention may be difficult and prolonged. Standard neurosurgical instruments may not be sufficient (i.e., drill bits may be too short and power tools may be damaged by the dense bone) [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.du_plessis.1993.388">du Plessis 1993</a>]. Note: There may not be clinical trials for this disorder.</p></div></div><div id="sost.Genetic_Counseling"><h2 id="_sost_Genetic_Counseling_">Genetic Counseling</h2><p>
<i>Genetic counseling is the process of providing individuals and families with
information on the nature, mode(s) of inheritance, and implications of genetic disorders to help them
make informed medical and personal decisions. The following section deals with genetic
risk assessment and the use of family history and genetic testing to clarify genetic
status for family members; it is not meant to address all personal, cultural, or
ethical issues that may arise or to substitute for consultation with a genetics
professional</i>. &#x02014;ED.</p><div id="sost.Mode_of_Inheritance"><h3>Mode of Inheritance</h3><p><i>SOST</i>-related sclerosing bone dysplasias (<i>SOST</i>-related sclerosteosis and <i>SOST-</i>related endosteal hyperostosis, van Buchem type [van Buchem disease]) are inherited in an <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/autosomal-recessive/">autosomal recessive</a> manner.</p></div><div id="sost.Risk_to_Family_Members"><h3>Risk to Family Members</h3><p>
<b>Parents of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a></b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>The parents of an individual with <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosteosis are presumed to be <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a> for a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> in <i>SOST</i>. The parents of an individual with van Buchem disease are presumed to be heterozygous for a 52-kb <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/deletion/">deletion</a> downstream of <i>SOST</i>.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Molecular genetic testing is recommended for the parents of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> to confirm that both parents are <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a> for the genetic alteration identified in the proband and to allow reliable <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/recurrence-risk/">recurrence risk</a> assessment.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>If a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> is detected in only one parent and parental identity testing has confirmed biological maternity and paternity, it is possible that one of the genetic alterations identified in the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> occurred as a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/de-novo/"><i>de novo</i></a> event in the proband or as a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/postzygotic/">postzygotic</a> <i>de novo</i> event in a mosaic parent [<a class="bk_pop" href="#sost.REF.j_nsson.2017.519">J&#x000f3;nsson et al 2017</a>]. If the proband appears to have <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/homozygous/">homozygous</a> pathogenic variants (i.e., the same two pathogenic variants), additional possibilities to consider include:</div><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>A single- or multiexon <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/deletion/">deletion</a> in the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> that was not detected by <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/sequence-analysis/">sequence analysis</a> and that resulted in the artifactual appearance of homozygosity;</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Uniparental isodisomy for the parental <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/chromosome/">chromosome</a> with the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> that resulted in homozygosity for the genetic alteration in the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a>.</div></li></ul></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Heterozygotes have increased bone mass and calvarial widening, but signs and symptoms of a <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosing bone dysplasia have not been described.</div></li></ul><p>
<b>Sibs of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a></b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>If both parents are known to be <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a> for a genetic alteration associated with a <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosing bone dysplasia, each sib of an affected individual has at conception a 25% chance of being affected, a 50% chance of being heterozygous, and a 25% chance of inheriting neither of the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/familial/">familial</a> genetic alterations.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Heterozygotes have increased bone mass and calvarial widening, but signs and symptoms of a <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosing bone dysplasia have not been described.</div></li></ul><p>
<b>Offspring of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a></b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Unless an affected individual's reproductive partner also has an <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosing bone dysplasia or is a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/carrier/">carrier</a>, offspring will be obligate heterozygotes (carriers) for a genetic alteration associated with a <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosing bone dysplasia.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>If the reproductive partner of the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> is <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a> for a genetic alteration associated with a <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosing bone dysplasia, each offspring has a 50% chance of inheriting <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/biallelic/">biallelic</a> genetic alterations and being affected. Reproductive partners are more likely to be carriers of a genetic alteration associated with a <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosing bone dysplasia if they are related to the proband or are members of populations with a high <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/carrier/">carrier</a> frequency (e.g., the Afrikaner community in South Africa). See <a href="#sost.Prevalence">Prevalence</a>.</div></li></ul><p><b>Other family members.</b> Each sib of the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a>'s parents is at a 50% risk of being a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/carrier/">carrier</a> of a genetic alteration associated with a <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosing bone dysplasia.</p></div><div id="sost.Carrier_Detection"><h3>Carrier Detection</h3><p>Carrier testing for at-risk family members requires prior identification of the genetic alterations associated with the <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosing bone dysplasia in the family.</p></div><div id="sost.Related_Genetic_Counseling_Issues"><h3>Related Genetic Counseling Issues</h3><p>
<b>Family planning</b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>The optimal time for determination of genetic risk and discussion of the availability of prenatal/<a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/preimplantation-genetic-testing/">preimplantation genetic testing</a> is before pregnancy.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>It is appropriate to offer <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/genetic-counseling/">genetic counseling</a> (including discussion of potential risks to offspring and reproductive options) to young adults who are affected, are carriers, or are at risk of being carriers.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Carrier testing should be considered for the reproductive partners of known carriers and for the reproductive partners of individuals affected with a <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosing bone dysplasia, particularly if both partners are of the same ancestral background. Founder variants have been identified in several populations (see <a href="/books/NBK587318/table/sost.T.sost_pathogenic_variants_referenc/?report=objectonly" target="object" rid-ob="figobsostTsostpathogenicvariantsreferenc">Table 7</a>).</div></li></ul></div><div id="sost.Prenatal_Testing_and_Preimplantatio"><h3>Prenatal Testing and Preimplantation Genetic Testing</h3><p><b>Molecular genetic testing.</b> Once the genetic alterations associated with a <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosing bone dysplasia have been identified in an affected family member, prenatal and <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/preimplantation-genetic-testing/">preimplantation genetic testing</a> are possible.</p><p><b>Ultrasound examination</b> may be able to detect syndactyly in fetuses at risk for <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosteosis. This finding is variable in <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosteosis, and therefore its presence in an at-risk fetus is indicative of <i>SOST</i>-related sclerosteosis but its absence is not indicative of an unaffected fetus.</p><p>Differences in perspective may exist among medical professionals and within families regarding the use of prenatal and <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/preimplantation-genetic-testing/">preimplantation genetic testing</a>. Sclerostin acts as an inhibitor of bone formation by suppressing the canonic Wnt signaling pathway in cells of the osteoblast lineage. Sclerostin binds to the Wnt signaling coreceptors LRP5 and LRP6, thereby disabling the binding of Wnt particles to these receptors. For this mode of action sclerostin is reliant on its own coreceptor, LRP4.</p><p><b>Mechanism of disease causation.</b> Loss of function</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div><i>SOST</i>-related class="half_rhythm"><div>Individuals with <i>SOST-</i>related endosteal hyperostosis, van Buchem type (van Buchem disease), caused by a large <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/deletion/">deletion</a> of regulatory elements only necessary for postnatal sclerostin transcription downstream of <i>SOST</i>, do not develop
