Bosma arhinia microphthalmia syndrome (BAMS) is an extremely rare and striking
condition characterized by complete absence of the nose with or without ocular
defects. We report here that missense mutations in the epigenetic regulator
SMCHD1 mapping to the extended ATPase domain of the encoded protein cause BAMS
in all 14 cases studied. All mutations were de novo where parental DNA was
available. Biochemical tests and in vivo assays in Xenopus laevis embryos
suggest that these mutations may behave as gain-of-function alleles. This
finding is in contrast to the loss-of-function mutations in SMCHD1 that have
been associated with facioscapulohumeral muscular dystrophy (FSHD) type 2. Our
results establish SMCHD1 as a key player in nasal development and provide
biochemical insight into its enzymatic function that may be exploited for
development of therapeutics for FSHD.
