Huntington's disease (HD) is an autosomal, progressive, dominant inherited 
neurological disorder characterized by motor dysfunction, cognitive decline, and 
psychiatric symptoms. HD is caused by abnormal expansion of trinucleotide CAG in 
exon1 of the Huntington gene and the accumulation of mutant huntingtin (mHTT) 
fragments, which leads to neurotoxicity mainly in the brain's cortex region. 
This review aimed to collect current research on developing effective treatment 
strategies, including small-molecule approaches, gene therapies, and protein 
degradation techniques to reduce the mHTT levels. We further discuss various 
therapeutic strategies, including CRISPR-based approaches and small-molecule 
targeted protein degradation. Additionally, the potential of VTX-003 and ANX005 
in mitigating disease progression is explored. Despite these promising 
therapies, challenges persist, particularly in long-term assessment, delivery 
strategy, and off-target effects. Considering the future landscape and need, the 
review has strengthened the need of therapeutic interventions to enhance 
efficacy and safety, ultimately improving the quality of life of HD patients.

Plain Language Summary: Huntington’s disease (HD) is a rare and lethal genetic 
brain disorder. It is inherited from parents to children and associated with an 
error in a single gene (HTT). A part of this gene has an excessive amount of 
repeating DNA letters CAG. These extra repeats lead to the production of a toxic 
protein known as mutant huntingtin (mHTT), which accumulates in brain cells and 
destroys regions that control movement, thinking, and emotions. Many who have HD 
have difficulty with motor control, difficulty in controlling the gaze, memory 
loss, depression, and mood swings. These signs, which might begin with a mild 
degree and either mimic other diseases or be overlooked altogether, have an 
impact on the diagnosis. Because the disease worsens with time and has no cure, 
it is critical to identify remedies that help at the outset of the disease. 
Scientists are also trying to find “biomarkers” signals in the body that might 
indicate how the disease spreads. Recent studies are shed light on how HD works 
on a cellular level. Methods such as generating brain cells in a lab dish from 
stem cells can assist scientists in studying the disease more closely. With 
their limitations, these methods do open the door to testing new drugs. This 
review comprehends the most recent work on potential treatments for HD. These 
range from standard drugs to newer options like antisense oligonucleotides 
(ASOs), gene-editing techniques such as CRISPR, and treatments that assist in 
the degradation of the toxic mHTT protein. New drugs like VTX-003 and ANX005 are 
also promising in slowing the disease down. Despite these advances, however, 
challenges remain, including the effective delivery of treatments to the brain 
without side effects. This review calls for more investigation and therapeutic 
advances in the area, which will allow us to more effectively treat symptoms, 
slow disease progression, and improve the lives of individuals with Huntington’s 
disease.
