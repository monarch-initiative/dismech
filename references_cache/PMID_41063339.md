OBJECTIVE: To elucidate risk factors and preceding events for the development of 
symptoms of superior canal dehiscence syndrome (SCDS).
STUDY DESIGN: Retrospective cohort study.
SETTING: Tertiary referral center.
PATIENTS: Adult patients with SCDS who underwent surgical repair.
INTERVENTIONS: Not applicable.
MAIN OUTCOME MEASURES: Data on occupational and recreational histories, inciting 
events, and presenting symptoms were collected from medical records.
RESULTS: Records of 405 patients undergoing 454 surgeries were reviewed. The 
average age was 48 years; 231 (57%) patients were female. Forty-nine (12%) 
patients underwent bilateral sequential surgeries. Of those with a reported 
occupation (n=207), occupational exposures included loud noises in 22% and 
ambient pressure changes in 23% of patients. Of 56 patients reporting hobbies, 
66% included physical exercise (weightlifting, running, etc), and 27% either 
played an instrument or sang. Scuba diving, flying airplanes, hunting, and 
farming were each reported once (2%). One hundred seventy-five (43%) patients 
described preceding events leading to the development of symptoms, subdivided 
into an internal cause (eg, sneezing or coughing, etc) in 33% of those patients, 
external trauma (eg, fall, motor vehicle accident, etc) in 32%, an external 
cause not involving trauma (eg, loud noise exposure, ambient pressure changes 
such as during scuba diving) in 27%, spontaneously occurring in 5%, and in the 
immediate aftermath of surgery in 3%. Presenting symptoms were autophony (78%), 
sound-induced vertigo or oscillopsia (73%), somatosounds (70%), pressure-induced 
vertigo or oscillopsia (68%), and pulsatile tinnitus (66%).
CONCLUSIONS: Certain occupational and avocational factors may predispose 
individuals to SCDS, and certain traumatic events may precipitate its 
development.
