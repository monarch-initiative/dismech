Bardet-Biedl Syndrome (BBS) is a rare human hereditary disorder associated with 
several features including obesity, retinopathy, renal defects, polydactyly, 
learning disabilities and hypogenitalism. This article discusses the 
abnormalities accounting for energy imbalance leading to obesity in BBS, with 
emphasis on the recent evidence pointing to aberrations in hypothalamic action 
of leptin. Indeed, BBS proteins have emerged as important mediators of leptin 
receptor trafficking, and loss of BBS genes results in leptin resistance that 
could be due to abnormal leptin receptor handling in a subset of 
leptin-responsive neurons. These recent discoveries hold promise for improved 
clinical management of BBS patients. The relevance of these findings to 
non-syndromic common obesity is also discussed.
