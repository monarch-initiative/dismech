# Hypotension, unspecified: Uncharted Mortality Trends and Disparities in the United States, A CDC WONDER Analysis (1999-2025)
**Authors:** Palwasha Asghar, Muhammad Bilal Masood, Sahla Waqas, Wajeeha Iftikhar Shah, Fatima Fazal, Raghabendra Kumar Mahato
**DOI:** [10.21203/rs.3.rs-9541628/v1](https://doi.org/10.21203/rs.3.rs-9541628/v1)

## Content

Abstract
Introduction:
 Hypotension of unspecified etiology is life-threatening, with an annual prevalence 9% and up to 75% in severe cases. Delayed treatment may cause cardiogenic or septic shock with high mortality. This study examines racial and geographic disparities in adult mortality.
Methods
 CDC WONDER data (1999–2025) were used to calculate age-adjusted mortality rates (AAMR) per 100,000 adults ≥ 25 years. Trends and annual percent change (APC) were analyzed using joinpoint regression.
Results
 Overall, 305,933 deaths occurred (AAMR 5.7). Rates rose from 5.7(1999) to 6.2(2006; APC 1.50). Declined to 4.1 (2018; APC – 3.10), increased to 4.8 (2021; APC 5.35), then fell to 4.2(2025; APC-3.10). Adults ≥ 85 years had the highest mortality (59.4). Mortality was higher in men than women (men: 4.9 per 100,000 [95% CI: 4.8-5.0] vs. women: 3.7 per 100,000 [95% CI: 3.6–3.8] in 2025). The gender gap persisted throughout the study period, with men showing a sharper rise during 2018–2021 (APC 5.89% vs. 4.63% in women), NH Blacks (8.1), urban Southern residents, especially South Caroline (8.3).
Conclusion
 Hypotension-associated mortality disproportionately affects NH Black men in urban Southern areas, particularly those aged ≥ 85 years. Targeted interventions addressing medication safety in long-term care facilities and improved cardiovascular care access in rural Southern communities are warranted.
Limitations:
 ICD-10 code I95.9 may represent a terminal physiological state rather than a primary disease entity.
