PJS-type hamartomatous polyps are most common in the small intestine (in order of prevalence: jejunum, ileum, and duodenum) but can also occur in the stomach, large bowel, and extraintestinal sites including the renal pelvis, bronchus, gall bladder, nasal passages, urinary bladder, and ureters. GI polyps can result in chronic bleeding, anemia, and recurrent obstruction and intussusception requiring repeated laparotomy and bowel resection. Mucocutaneous hyperpigmentation presents in childhood as dark blue to dark brown macules around the mouth, eyes, and nostrils, in the perianal area, and on the buccal mucosa. Hyperpigmented macules on the fingers are common. The macules may fade in puberty and adulthood. Recognition of the distinctive skin manifestations is important especially in individuals who have PJS as the result of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/de-novo/"><i>de novo</i></a> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> as these skin findings often predate GI signs and symptoms. Individuals with PJS are at increased risk for a wide variety of epithelial malignancies (colorectal, gastric, pancreatic, breast, and ovarian cancers). Females are at risk for sex cord tumors with annular tubules (SCTAT), a benign neoplasm of the ovaries, and adenoma malignum of the cervix, a rare aggressive cancer. Males occasionally develop large calcifying Sertoli cell tumors of the testes, which secrete estrogen and can lead to gynecomastia, advanced skeletal age, and ultimately short stature, if untreated.</p></div><div><h4 class="inline">Diagnosis/testing.</h4><p>The diagnosis of PJS is based on clinical findings. Identification of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> in <i>STK11</i> by <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/molecular-genetic-testing/">molecular genetic testing</a> confirms the diagnosis and allows for testing of at-risk relatives.</p></div><div><h4 class="inline">Management.</h4><p><i>Treatment of manifestations:</i> Routine endoscopic surveillance with polypectomy decreases the frequency of emergency laparotomy and bowel loss resulting from intussusception. Small-bowel imaging includes video capsule endoscopy (VCE), CT enterography, and/or magnetic resonance enterography (MRE). Balloon-assisted enteroscopy allows for removal of deep small-bowel polyps. Occasionally intraoperative enteroscopy and enterotomy is needed for removal of large distal small-bowel polyps. Intussusception and malignancies should be treated in the standard manner.</p><p><i>Prevention of primary manifestations:</i> Although not specifically studied in individuals with PJS, prophylactic mastectomy to decrease the risk of breast cancer could be considered based on family history or other clinical factors. Similarly, there is no prospective data on gynecologic prophylactic surgery for the elevated gynecologic cancer risk in females with PJS.</p><p><i>Surveillance:</i> In children and adolescents: colonoscopy and upper endoscopy at age eight years; if negative, follow up at age 18 years. If polyps are detected repeat every one to three years based on size, number, and histopathology of polyps. Small-bowel surveillance by MRE or VCE every one to three years beginning at age eight years. Examination for precocious puberty in females annually beginning at age eight years. Testicular examination and examination for feminizing changes in males annually beginning at age ten years.</p><p>In adults: Colonoscopy, upper endoscopy, and small-bowel examination by MRE or VCE every two to three years beginning at age 18 years; clinical breast examination in women every six months beginning at age 30 years; mammogram and breast MRI in women annually beginning at age 30 years; pelvic examination and pap smear in women annually beginning at age 18 to 20 years. Pancreatic imaging with endoscopic ultrasound or MRI/MRCP annually beginning at age 30 to 35 years.</p><p><i>Evaluation of relatives at risk:</i> If the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> in the family is known, offer <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/molecular-genetic-testing/">molecular genetic testing</a> to at-risk relatives so that morbidity and mortality can be reduced by early diagnosis and prevention of disease through appropriate surveillance and consideration of prophylactic measures in affected family members. If the family variant is not known, offer clinical diagnostic evaluations to all at-risk family members, who will benefit from early treatment and appropriate surveillance.</p></div><div><h4 class="inline">Genetic counseling.</h4><p>PJS is inherited in an <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/autosomal-dominant/">autosomal dominant</a> manner. The majority of individuals diagnosed with PJS have an affected parent; however, many individuals with PJS represent apparently <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/simplex/">simplex</a> cases. The exact proportion of individuals who have PJS as the result of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/de-novo/"><i>de novo</i></a> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> is unknown. If a parent of the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> is affected and/or is known to have the <i>STK11</i> pathogenic variant identified in the proband, the risk to the sibs of inheriting the pathogenic variant is 50%. Once the <i>STK11</i> pathogenic variant has been identified in an affected family member, predictive testing for at-risk family members, <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/prenatal-testing/">prenatal testing</a>, and <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/preimplantation-genetic-testing/">preimplantation genetic testing</a> are possible.</p></div></div><div id="pjs.Diagnosis"><h2 id="_pjs_Diagnosis_">Diagnosis</h2><div id="pjs.Suggestive_Findings"><h3>Suggestive Findings</h3><p>Peutz-Jeghers syndrome (PJS) <b>should be suspected</b> in individuals with the following:</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Two or more PJS-type hamartomatous polyps of the gastrointestinal (GI) tract.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Characteristic mucocutaneous pigmentation and hyperpigmented macules (periorbital, lips, fingers, nose, toes, and anus)</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Gynecomastia in males as a result of estrogen-producing Sertoli cell testicular tumors</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>History of intussusception, especially in a child or young adult</div></li></ul><p><b>PJS-type GI polyps.</b> The sine qua non of PJS diagnosis is the hamartomatous GI polyp, which is histopathologically characterized by distinctive interdigitating smooth muscle bundles in a characteristic arborizing (branching tree) appearance throughout the lamina propria. Small-bowel polyps are typically pedunculated and demonstrate a lobular architecture. The overlying epithelium is typically nondysplastic, although dysplasia can occur. In the stomach, PJS polyps may be indistinguishable from juvenile or hyperplastic polyps. In the large intestine, PJS polyps resemble mucosal prolapse polyps. Their lobular architecture and desmin-positive smooth muscle fiber favor the diagnosis of PJS [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.rosty.2018.1">Rosty 2018</a>]. Pseudo invasion of misplaced crypts is an innate property of the PJS hamartoma, which may reflect the role of <i>STK11</i> in cell polarity [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.tse.2013.1235">Tse et al 2013</a>].</p><p>Note: Individuals with PJS also develop many other polyps; polyps showing adenomatous changes frequently arise in the colon and may cause confusion with <a href="/books/n/gene/fap/">familial adenomatous polyposis</a>. The histology of gastric PJS polyps can be similar to gastric hyperplastic polyps, thus highlighting the importance of a GI pathologist in the review of polyp histology and knowledge of the clinical context [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.rosty.2018.1">Rosty 2018</a>].</p></div><div id="pjs.Establishing_the_Diagnosis"><h3>Establishing the Diagnosis</h3><p>The clinical diagnosis of PJS <b>is 2010</a>]:</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Two or more histologically confirmed PJS-type hamartomatous polyps</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Any number of PJS-type polyps detected in an individual who has a family history of PJS in at least one close relative</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Characteristic mucocutaneous pigmentation in an individual who has a family history of PJS in at least one close relative</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Any number of PJS-type polyps in an individual who also has characteristic mucocutaneous pigmentation</div></li></ul><p>The molecular diagnosis of PJS <b>is established</b> in a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> with <a href="#pjs.Suggestive_Findings">suggestive findings</a> and a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> in <i>STK11</i> significance</a> does not establish or rule out the diagnosis.</p><p>Molecular genetic testing approaches can include <b>single-<a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gene/">gene</a> testing, <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/multigene-panel/">multigene panel</a></b>, and <b>comprehensive</b>
<b><a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/genomic/">genomic</a> testing</b> depending on the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a>.</p><p><b>Single-<a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gene/">gene</a> testing.</b> Sequence analysis of <i>STK11</i> is performed first to detect small intragenic deletions/insertions and <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/missense/">missense</a>, <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/nonsense-variant/">nonsense</a>, and <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/splice-site/">splice site</a> variants. If no variant is detected by the sequencing method used, the next step is to perform gene-targeted <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/deletion-duplication-analysis/">deletion/duplication analysis</a> to detect exon and whole-gene deletions or duplications. The risk for GI and extraintestinal malignancies is significantly increased. Distinct benign and malignant gonadal and gynecologic tumors can also be seen. Variable expressivity is common; for example, some affected individuals in families with PJS may have only polyps or perioral pigmentation.</p><p><b>GI polyposis.</b> PJS-type hamartomatous polyps can occur anywhere in the GI tract but occur most commonly in the small intestine. The density of polyps is greatest in the jejunum, followed by the ileum, then the duodenum. Polyps also occur in the stomach and large bowel. Polyps have also been reported in the renal pelvis, urinary bladder, ureters, lungs, nares, and gallbladder.</p><p>Adenomas also appear with increased prevalence throughout the GI tract.</p><p>The malignant potential of PJS-type hamartomatous polyps is unknown; however, the polyps can cause significant complications including bowel obstruction, rectal prolapse, and/or severe GI bleeding with secondary anemia requiring multiple emergency laparotomies and bowel resections. The age of onset of symptoms from polyps is variable, with some children developing symptoms within the first few years of life. The risk of intussusception was estimated to be 44% by age ten years and 50% by age 20 years [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.van_lier.2011a.940">van Lier et al 2011a</a>]. Intussusception risk increases with polyp size &#x02265;15 mm [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.latchford.2019.442">Latchford et al 2019</a>]. One small single-center retrospective study concluded that endoscopic management of small-bowel polyps in PJS using balloon assisted endoscopy (BAE) &#x02013; or when needed, laparoscopically assisted double-balloon endoscopy &#x02013; decreased the occurrence of urgent laparotomy [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.belsha.2017.500">Belsha et al 2017</a>].</p><p>Significant <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/interfamilial-variability/">interfamilial variability</a> in the age at which polyps first appear is observed, suggesting that the natural history of polyps in a family may be a predictor of severity for offspring. Increasingly, case series have demonstrated a younger onset of GI pathology either through diagnostic testing or evaluation based on a positive family history. These data have prompted recommendations to begin surveillance at a younger age to detect and remove GI polyps, decrease risk of malignancy, and reduce complications of bowel obstruction [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.wagner.2021.473">Wagner et al 2021</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF37">NCCN 2021</a>]. Increasingly, the literature demonstrates the efficacy and safety of BAE and polypectomy in children with PJS.</p><p><b>Mucocutaneous pigmentation.</b> Melanocytic macules (MM) are rarely present at birth; they become pronounced in most children before the fifth year, but then may fade in puberty and adulthood. Children often present with dark blue to dark brown mucocutaneous MM around the mouth, eyes, and nostrils, in the perianal area, and on the buccal mucosa. Hyperpigmented MM on the fingers are also common. In one series, 94% of individuals with PJS had perianal MM, 73% had MM that affected the digits, 65% had MM on the buccal mucosa, and 21% had MM at other sites [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.utsunomiya.1975.71">Utsunomiya et al 1975</a>].</p><p>Histologically, increased melanocytes are observed at the epidermal-dermal junction, with increased melanin in the basal cells. No malignancy risk is associated with MM. In <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/sporadic/">sporadic</a> cases, MM is often the first diagnostic clue to PJS. <i>STK11</i> molecular analysis has been recommended in children with characteristic PJS freckling even in the absence of additional clinical criteria [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.latchford.2019.442">Latchford et al 2019</a>].</p><p><b>Gonadal tumors.</b> Females with PJS are at risk for ovarian sex cord tumors with annular tubules (SCTATs) and mucinous tumors of the ovaries and fallopian tubes. Symptoms include irregular or heavy menstrual periods and, occasionally, precocious puberty due to hyperestrogenism. PJS-related SCTATs are bilateral multifocal small tumors with focal calcification and a typically benign course [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.wagner.2021.473">Wagner et al 2021</a>]. In contrast, <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/sporadic/">sporadic</a> SCTATs are large, unilateral, and associated with a 20% risk of malignancy.</p><p>Males occasionally develop large cell calcifying Sertoli cell tumors of the testes derived from sperm cord cells. These tumors may secrete estrogen and can lead to gynecomastia, advanced skeletal age, and ultimately short stature, if untreated. Multifocal calcifications are typically seen on testicular ultrasound. Aromatase inhibitors help reverse the hormonal effects of Sertoli cell tumors including reduction of gynecomastia and slowing of linear bone growth and bone age [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.crocker.2014.e2673">Crocker et al 2014</a>].</p><p><b>Malignancy.</b> Individuals with PJS are at increased risk for intestinal and extraintestinal malignancies. A meta analysis of 583 individuals with PJS found an overall cancer risk of 83% by age 70 years [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.ishida.2016.1231">Ishida et al 2016</a>]. A cohort study of 336 individuals with PJS reported an overall cancer diagnosis of 55% by age 60 years [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.chen.2017.1010428317705131">Chen et al 2017</a>].</p><div The incidence of cancer increases markedly after age 50 years. Colorectal cancer (28% by age 60) was the most common cancer reported by <a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.chen.2017.1010428317705131">Chen et al [2017]</a> in individuals with PJS.</p><p><b>Breast cancer</b> can occur at early ages in women with PJS. The breast cancer risk in women with PJS may approach that of women who have a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> in <i>BRCA1</i> or <i>BRCA2</i>. Early-onset breast cancer has been reported in multiple families with PJS.</p><p><b>Ovarian cancer.</b> In an Italian series of 61 females with PJS, three had ovarian cancer, one of which was a malignant SCTAT [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.resta.2013.606">Resta et al 2013</a>]. In a Dutch series of 69 females with PJS, two females had malignant Sertoli cell ovarian tumors and one had ovarian small cell cancer [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.van_lier.2011b.141">van Lier et al 2011b</a>].</p><p><b>Cervical cancer.</b> Minimal deviation adenocarcinoma (adenoma malignum) is a rare well-differentiated adenocarcinoma of the uterine cervix. Presenting symptoms include bleeding or a mucoid, watery vaginal discharge. Histologic diagnosis can be difficult on small pathologic samples and a core biopsy or endo-cervical curettage is often necessary.</p><p>The current risk for gynecologic cancer in females with PJS is estimated to range from 18% to 50% by age 50 years [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.wagner.2021.473">Wagner et al 2021</a>].</p><p><b>Testicular cancer.</b> Malignant transformation of Sertoli cell tumors is unusual. In a series including 64 males with PJS, one testicular seminoma was reported [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.van_lier.2011b.141">van Lier et al 2011b</a>].</p><p><b>Pancreatic cancer</b> is among the top causes of cancer death in the US and Europe with a dismal five-year survival after diagnosis. Recent advances in the detection of early pancreatic adenocarcinoma amenable to curative surgical resection and identification of premalignant lesions by endoscopic ultrasound and MRI/MRCP have supported surveillance in high-risk individuals including individuals with PJS [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.korsse.2013.59">Korsse et al 2013</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.goggins.2020.7">Goggins et al 2020</a>].</p></div><div id="pjs.GenotypePhenotype_Correlations"><h3>Genotype-Phenotype Correlations</h3><p>Data on <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/genotype/">genotype</a>-<a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a> correlation related to <i>STK11</i> pathogenic variants are conflicting. Genotype-phenotype correlations have been extensively reviewed [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.daniell.2018.421">Daniell et al 2018</a>].</p><p><b>Variants that predict premature protein truncation</b> are thought to predispose to a more severe <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a>. <a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.amos.2004.327">Amos et al [2004]</a> found that individuals who had pathogenic <i>STK11</i> variants that predicted premature truncation and those who tested negative for pathogenic variants had similar ages of onset for first-reported polyps or polypectomy, and those with <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/missense/">missense</a> variants had later onset for these symptoms. <a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.salloch.2010.97">Salloch et al [2010]</a> similarly found that persons with pathogenic <i>STK11</i> variants that predicted premature truncation had more GI surgeries, a higher polyp count, and an earlier age of first polypectomy.</p><p><b>Pathogenic variants affecting protein kinase <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/domain/">domain</a> XI coded by <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/exon/">exon</a> 7</b> correlated with a 90% (9/10) incidence of GI polyp dysplasia [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.wang.2014.851">Wang et al 2014</a>].</p><p>In a study of 297 individuals with PJS, the type or site of the <i>STK11</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> did not influence cancer risk [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.lim.2004.1788">Lim et al 2004</a>]. A review of 419 affected individuals found that the variant type and site within the functional domains of the expressed protein did not affect cancer risk [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.hearle.2006a.3209">Hearle et al 2006a</a>]. The risk for small-bowel intussusception was not influenced by <i>STK11</i> variant status [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.hearle.2006b.e41">Hearle et al 2006b</a>]. PJS <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a> did not vary between individuals with large deletions and those with other <i>STK11</i> variants [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.daniell.2018.421">Daniell et al 2018</a>].</p></div><div id="pjs.Penetrance"><h3>Penetrance</h3><p>To date all reported individuals with pathogenic variants in <i>STK11</i> have shown clinical manifestations.</p></div><div id="pjs.Nomenclature"><h3>Nomenclature</h3><p>The following terms have also been used for PJS:</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Polyp and spots syndrome</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Inherited hamartomatous polyps in association with mucocutaneous melanocyte macules</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Hutchinson Weber-Peutz syndrome</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Perioral lentiginosis (sometimes used inappropriately as a synonym for PJS)</div></li></ul></div><div id="pjs.Prevalence"><h3>Prevalence</h3><p>Birth prevalence has not been reliably established; estimates range widely, from 1:25,000 to 1:280,000 [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.tchekmedyian.2013.e79639">Tchekmedyian et al 2013</a>].</p><p>PJS can occur in any racial or ethnic group.</p></div></div><div id="pjs.Genetically_Related_Allelic_Disorder"><h2 id="_pjs_Genetically_Related_Allelic_Disorder_">Genetically Related (Allelic) Disorders</h2><p>One individual with a pathogenic <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/nonsense-variant/">nonsense</a> variant of <i>STK11</i> was diagnosed with gonadotropin-independent precocious puberty [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.massa.2007.1083">Massa et al 2007</a>]. Another individual with a 29-<a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/base-pair/">base pair</a> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/deletion/">deletion</a> of <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/exon/">exon</a> 1 of <i>STK11</i> was diagnosed with juvenile polyposis coli (see <a href="/books/n/gene/jps/">Juvenile Polyposis Syndrome</a>) [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.sweet.2005.2465">Sweet et al 2005</a>]. This strategy has two goals:</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>To decrease the sequelae of large polyps including bleeding, anemia, obstruction, and intussusception</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>To reduce the risk for cancer by the malignant transformation of PJS-type polyps</div></li></ul><p>The luminal polyp-related complications arise in childhood whereas cancer in PJS is typically seen in adulthood. Some evidence indicates that routine endoscopy and intraoperative enteroscopy with polypectomy decreases the frequency of emergency laparotomy and bowel loss. From St Mark's PJS registry of 51 affected individuals who underwent surveillance endoscopies, none had emergency surgical interventions and no GI luminal cancers were diagnosed [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.latchford.2011.1547">Latchford et al 2011</a>]. In surveillance endoscopies in affected individuals by age 18 years, 17/28 had large gastroduodenal or colonic polyps (&#x0003e;1 cm). These studies demonstrate that endoscopic surveillance and polypectomy in PJS is safe and effective.</p><p>Until recently, distal small-bowel polyps that are beyond the reach of conventional endoscopy have been difficult to manage. In the past, barium contrast upper-GI series with a small-bowel follow through has been recommended. However, recent advances allow better diagnosis and eradication of small-bowel polyps, often without laparotomy and with a decrease in the radiation burden related to prior dependence on CT scan imaging [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.belsha.2017.500">Belsha et al 2017</a>].</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Video capsule endoscopy (VCE) allows for better visualization of the small-bowel polyps than barium x-rays and is recommended as a first-line surveillance procedure. In children, the capsule can be deployed in the duodenum after upper endoscopy. The results of the small-bowel imaging studies direct the endoscopic removal of polyps. See Note.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Magnetic resonance enterography (MRE) is a reliable procedure for the detection of larger small-bowel polyps with similar <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/sensitivity/">sensitivity</a> to VCE, and avoids the radiation exposure of CT enterography. CT and MR enteroclysis are alternative procedures but are less well tolerated. See Note.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Balloon-assisted enteroscopy (BAE) can remove distal small-bowel polyps with or without laparotomy [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.gao.2010.768">Gao et al 2010</a>]. The efficacy and safety of BAE for small-bowel polyp management in PJS has been established in children with PJS [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.korsse.2012.261">Korsse et al 2012</a>]. BAE and polypectomy decrease the need for intraoperative enteroscopy and enterotomy, which should be reserved for affected individuals with large and distal small-bowel polyps beyond the reach of BAE.</div></li></ul><p>Note: (1) VCE was preferred by individuals and detected more large polyps than MRE [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.urquhart.2014.249">Urquhart et al 2014</a>]. (2) In three individuals, MRE detected polyps &#x0003e;15 mm that were not detected by VCE [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.gupta.2010.108">Gupta et al 2010</a>].</p><p><b>Intussusception</b> should be treated in a standard manner.</p><p><b>Malignancies</b> should be treated in a standard manner. Conservative management of gonadal tumors in males and females is appropriate.</p></div><div id="pjs.Prevention_of_Primary_Manifestations"><h3>Prevention of Primary Manifestations</h3><p>Although not specifically studied in females with PJS, prophylactic mastectomy may be considered to manage the increased risk for breast cancer based on the family history or other clinical factors. Prophylactic hysterectomy and bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy to prevent gynecologic malignancy in women may be considered. In some disorders with a high risk for malignancy (e.g., <a href="/books/n/gene/hnpcc/">Lynch syndrome</a>), evidence supports this strategy [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.schmeler.2006.261">Schmeler et al 2006</a>]. As the benefits of surveillance and prophylactic surgery have not been established, all females with PJS should be offered expert breast and gynecologic care at an established center in the framework of a study or registry [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.wagner.2021.473">Wagner et al 2021</a>].</p></div><div id="pjs.Surveillance"><h3>Surveillance</h3><p>Surveillance guidelines for PJS have evolved. the risk for polyps or malignancy in individuals with PJS have been described.</p><p>Due to the increased risk for cervical, lung, and pancreatic cancer in individuals with PJS, smoking should be avoided.</p></div><div id="pjs.Evaluation_of_Relatives_at_Risk"><h3>Evaluation of Relatives at Risk</h3><p>It is appropriate to evaluate apparently asymptomatic older and younger at-risk relatives of an affected individual in order to identify as early as possible those who would benefit from initiation of treatment and preventive measures.</p><p><b>If the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> in the family is known</b>, it is appropriate to offer <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/molecular-genetic-testing/">molecular genetic testing</a> for the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/familial/">familial</a> <i>STK11</i> pathogenic variant to at-risk relatives.</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Morbidity and mortality can be reduced in those individuals identified to have the family-specific <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> by means of early diagnosis, treatment, and surveillance (see <a href="#pjs.Surveillance">Surveillance</a>).</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Relatives in whom the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/familial/">familial</a> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> is not identified (and who do not meet clinical diagnostic criteria for PJS) should not be considered as requiring PJS surveillance [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.wagner.2021.473">Wagner et al 2021</a>].</div></li></ul><p><b>If the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> in the family is not known</b>, it is appropriate to offer:</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Clinical diagnostic evaluations to identify those family members who will benefit from early treatment;</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Surveillance as outlined in <a href="#pjs.Surveillance">Surveillance</a> to all first-degree relatives whether or not they meet diagnostic criteria.</div></li></ul><p>See <a href="#pjs.Related_Genetic_Counseling_Issues">Genetic Counseling</a> for issues related to testing of at-risk relatives for <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/genetic-counseling/">genetic counseling</a> purposes.</p></div><div id="pjs.Therapies_Under_Investigation"><h3>Therapies Under Investigation</h3><p>Search <a href="https://clinicaltrials.gov/" ref="pagearea=body&amp;targetsite=external&amp;targetcat=link&amp;targettype=uri">ClinicalTrials.gov</a> in the US and <a href="https://www.clinicaltrialsregister.eu/ctr-search/search" ref="pagearea=body&amp;targetsite=external&amp;targetcat=link&amp;targettype=uri">EU Clinical Trials Register</a> in Europe for access to information on clinical studies for a wide range of diseases and conditions. Note: There may not be clinical trials for this rare disorder.</p></div><div id="pjs.Other"><h3>Other</h3><p>Several animal models of PJS have been generated using <i>STK11</i> knockout mice [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.wei.2005.11297">Wei et al 2005</a>]. Gastrointestinal hamartomatous polyposis in <i>STK11</i><sup>+/&#x02013;</sup> mice mimics human PJS polyps with the unique smooth muscle arborization. In these animal models, upregulation of cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2) in polyp tissue was noted [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.rossi.2002.12327">Rossi et al 2002</a>]. Overexpression of COX-2 in human PJS hamartomas and PJS-associated cancers has also been detected [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.mcgarrity.2003.671">McGarrity et al 2003</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.wei.2003.1665">Wei et al 2003</a>]. COX-2 inhibition in mice using celecoxib suppresses polyp growth [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.udd.2004.1030">Udd et al 2004</a>]. Polyp burden in <i>STK11</i> (<i>Lkb1</i>) <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a> (+/&#x02013;) knockout mice was reduced by 86% among mice who had developed polyps and were then treated with celecoxib, a Cox inhibitor.</p><p>Selective COX-2 inhibitors have been used to inhibit premalignant adenoma formation. To date, however, no clinical trials in the US are studying efficacy of COX-2 inhibitors in reducing polyp formation in individuals with PJS. Increased cardiovascular and cerebrovascular adverse events with selective COX-2 inhibitors limit their use.</p><p>Observation of hyperactivation of mTOR in hereditary hamartoma syndrome and a variety of cancers suggests that mTOR inhibitors may be useful in the management of PJS [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.van_veelen.2011.2289">van Veelen et al 2011</a>]. A significant reduction in tumor burden in <i>STK11</i>+/&#x02013; mice treated with rapamycin was reported compared with that in mice without rapamycin treatment [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.wei.2009.149">Wei et al 2009</a>]. Treatment begun before the onset of polyposis resulted in more dramatic reduction than treatment begun after onset. In another study in <i>STK11</i>+/&#x02013; mice oral rapamycin intake was associated with a significant reduction in microvessel growth in polyps as well as in tumor burden [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.robinson.2009.35">Robinson et al 2009</a>].</p><p>In addition, in two small trials in persons with <a href="/books/n/gene/tuberous-sclerosis/">tuberous sclerosis complex</a>, treatment with rapamycin induced regression of the astrocytomas [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.franz.2006.490">Franz et al 2006</a>] and reduced facial angiofibroma [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.hofbauer.2008.473">Hofbauer et al 2008</a>]. Whether rapamycin would decrease polyp growth in PJS has not been documented in human studies. The mTOR inhibitor, everolimus, caused partial regression of a pancreatic cancer in an individual with PJS. Induction of apoptosis in colon polyps was also noted [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.kl_mpen.2011.e150">Kl&#x000fc;mpen et al 2011</a>]. These findings suggest that mTOR inhibitors are an option to investigate for management of polyposis in PJS. A Phase II trial was initiated in Utah and the Netherlands with a goal of recruiting 15 participants to evaluate chemoprevention with everolimus. The trial was stopped due to low enrollment and poor tolerance of everolimus after only two participants could be recruited [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.de_brabander.2018.399">de Brabander et al 2018</a>].</p><p><a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.poffenberger.2018.406">Poffenberger et al [2018]</a> demonstrated that selective <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/deletion/">deletion</a> of STK11 in T-cells reproduced the PJS <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a> in mice. PJS polyps in experimental mice and human were characterized by intense immune cell infiltration and increased levels of inflammatory cytokines (interleukin 6) and enhanced STAT3 signaling, providing new potential therapeutic targets for PJS.</p></div></div><div id="pjs.Genetic_Counseling"><h2 id="_pjs_Genetic_Counseling_">Genetic Counseling</h2><p>
<i>Genetic counseling is the process of providing individuals and families with
information on the nature, mode(s) of inheritance, and implications of genetic disorders to help them
make informed medical and personal decisions. The following section deals with genetic
risk assessment and the use of family history and genetic testing to clarify genetic
status for family members; it is not meant to address all personal, cultural, or
ethical issues that may arise or to substitute for consultation with a genetics
professional</i>. &#x02014;ED.</p><div id="pjs.Mode_of_Inheritance"><h3>Mode of Inheritance</h3><p>Peutz-Jeghers syndrome (PJS) is inherited in an <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/autosomal-dominant/">autosomal dominant</a> manner.</p></div><div id="pjs.Risk_to_Family_Members"><h3>Risk to Family Members</h3><p>
<b>Parents of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a></b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>The majority of individuals diagnosed with PJS have an affected parent. In large series, 60%-78% of individuals with PJS had affected relatives [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.van_lier.2010a.1258">van Lier et al 2010a</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.resta.2013.606">Resta et al 2013</a>].</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Many individuals diagnosed with PJS represent apparently <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/simplex/">simplex</a> cases (i.e., a single affected family member).</div><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>In the series noted above, 17%-40% of probands represent apparently <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/simplex/">simplex</a> cases. In a separate study, between 30% and 45% of probands represent simplex cases [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.daniell.2018.421">Daniell et al 2018</a>]. In the absence of sufficient parental clinical and molecular data, the proportion of these individuals who have PJS as the result of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/de-novo/"><i>de novo</i></a> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> cannot be determined.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>In a study of 38 individuals with PJS, 11 of 26 probands had the disorder as the result of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/de-novo/"><i>de novo</i></a> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.wu.2020.9159315">Wu et al 2020</a>].</div></li></ul></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>If the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> appears to be the only affected family member, recommendations for the evaluation of parents of a proband include:</div><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Molecular genetic testing if an <i>STK11</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> has been identified in the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a>;</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Examination of the buccal mucosa and skin of the digits and genital area for hyperpigmented macules; upper and lower gastrointestinal endoscopy; mammography; bimanual pelvic examination and ovarian ultrasound examination (females); and testicular examination (males).</div></li></ul></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>If the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> identified in the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> is not identified in either parent, neither parent has clinical findings consistent with PJS, and parental identity testing has confirmed biological maternity and paternity, the following possibilities should be considered:</div><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>The <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> has a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/de-novo/"><i>de novo</i></a> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a>.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>The <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> inherited a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> from a parent with <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/germline/">germline</a> (or somatic and germline) <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/mosaicism/">mosaicism</a>. Note: Testing of parental leukocyte DNA may not detect all instances of <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/somatic-mosaicism/">somatic mosaicism</a> and will not detect a pathogenic variant that is present in the germ cells only.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Parental <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/mosaicism/">mosaicism</a> has been reported in PJS. Somatic mosaicism can have variable effects on severity of the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a> depending on which tissues have the <i>STK11</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.jelsig.2021.55">Jelsig et al 2021</a>]; a parent with somatic and <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/germline-mosaicism/">germline mosaicism</a> for a <i>STK11</i> pathogenic variant may be mildly/minimally affected [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.butelsimoes.2019.109">Butel-Simoes et al 2019</a>].</div></li></ul></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>The family history of some individuals diagnosed with PJS may appear to be negative because of failure to recognize the disorder in family members or early death of the parent (and other relatives) before the onset of symptoms. Therefore, an apparently negative family history cannot be confirmed unless appropriate clinical evaluation has been performed on the parents of the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> and/or <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/molecular-genetic-testing/">molecular genetic testing</a> has demonstrated that neither parent is <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a> for the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> identified in the proband.</div></li></ul><p><b>Sibs of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a>.</b> The risk to the sibs of the proband depends on the clinical/genetic status of the proband&#x02019;s parents.</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>If a parent of the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> is affected and/or is known to have the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> identified in the proband, the risk to the sibs of inheriting the pathogenic variant is 50%. PJS is marked by a wide range of phenotypic expression in family members with the same <i>STK11</i> pathogenic variant. Epigenetic modifications, the effects of modifying genes, environmental, and other factors are known to influence the development of PJS-related manifestations in <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a> individuals [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.daniell.2018.421">Daniell et al 2018</a>].</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>If the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> has a known <i>STK11</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> that cannot be detected in the leukocyte DNA of either parent and both parents are clinically unaffected, the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/recurrence-risk/">recurrence risk</a> to sibs is slightly greater than that of the general population because of the possibility of parental <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/mosaicism/">mosaicism</a> [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.butelsimoes.2019.109">Butel-Simoes et al 2019</a>].</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>If the genetic status of the parents is unknown but they are clinically unaffected, the risk to the sibs of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> appears to be low. However, sibs of a proband with clinically unaffected parents are still presumed to be at increased risk for PJS because of the possibility of parental <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/mosaicism/">mosaicism</a>.</div></li></ul><p>
<b>Offspring of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a></b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Each child of an individual with PJS and an identified <i>STK11</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> has a 50% chance of inheriting the <i>STK11</i> pathogenic variant. (The offspring of individuals who are mosaic for an <i>STK11</i> pathogenic variant are also at risk of inheriting an <i>STK11</i> pathogenic variant.)</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>The risk to the offspring of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> with a clinical diagnosis of PJS, a negative family history, and no identified <i>STK11</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> is unknown.</div></li></ul><p><b>Other family members.</b> The risk to other family members depends on the status of the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a>'s first-degree relatives: if a relative is affected and/or is <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a> for an <i>STK11</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a>, his or her family members are at risk.</p></div><div id="pjs.Related_Genetic_Counseling_Issues"><h3>Related Genetic Counseling Issues</h3><p>See Management, <a href="#pjs.Evaluation_of_Relatives_at_Risk">Evaluation of Relatives at Risk</a> for information on evaluating at-risk relatives for the purpose of early diagnosis and treatment.</p><p><b>Predictive testing</b> for at-risk asymptomatic family members requires prior identification of the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/germline/">germline</a> <i>STK11</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> in the family. Because early detection of at-risk individuals who have an <i>STK11</i> pathogenic variant affects medical management &#x02013; particularly surveillance (see <a href="/books/NBK1266/table/pjs.T.recommended_evaluations_following/?report=objectonly" target="object" rid-ob="figobpjsTrecommendedevaluationsfollowing">Table 4</a>) &#x02013; testing of at-risk individuals (with informed parental assent) during childhood is considered beneficial [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF37">NCCN 2021</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.wagner.2021.473">Wagner et al 2021</a>].</p><p>Parents often want to know the genetic status of their children prior to initiating screening in order to avoid unnecessary procedures in a child who has not inherited the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a>. Special consideration should be given to education of the children and their parents prior to genetic testing. A plan should be established for the manner in which results are to be given to the parents and their children.</p><p><b>Genetic cancer risk assessment and counseling.</b> For a comprehensive description of the medical, psychosocial, and ethical ramifications of identifying at-risk individuals through cancer risk assessment with or without <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/molecular-genetic-testing/">molecular genetic testing</a>, see <a href="https://www.cancer.gov/about-cancer/causes-prevention/genetics/risk-assessment-pdq" ref="pagearea=body&amp;targetsite=external&amp;targetcat=link&amp;targettype=uri">Cancer Genetics Risk Assessment and Counseling &#x02013; Health Professional Version</a> (part of PDQ<sup>&#x000ae;</sup>, National Cancer Institute). Limited data on the effects of a PJS diagnosis on family planning and quality of life are available [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.van_lier.2010b.219">van Lier et al 2010b</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.van_lier.2012.236">van Lier et al 2012</a>]</p><p><b>Other issues to consider.</b> It is recommended that physicians ordering PJS <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/molecular-genetic-testing/">molecular genetic testing</a> and individuals considering undergoing testing understand the risks, benefits, and limitations of the testing prior to sending a sample to a laboratory. Referral to a genetic counselor and/or a center in which testing is routinely offered is recommended.</p><p><b>Genetic heterogeneity.</b> Individuals with a clinical diagnosis of PJS who do not have a detectable <i>STK11</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> may have a pathogenic variant(s) in a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gene/">gene</a> other than <i>STK11</i> and, consequently, have a disorder that is inherited in a different manner from <i>STK11</i>-related PJS (see <a href="#pjs.Differential_Diagnosis">Differential Diagnosis</a>). Of 25 individuals who had PJS but did not have a detectable <i>STK11</i> pathogenic variant, one had a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a> pathogenic variant in <i>MUTYH</i> [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.alhopuro.2008.5513">Alhopuro et al 2008</a>]. Of note, pathogenic variants in <i>MUTYH</i> ordinarily cause an <a href="/books/n/gene/maps/">autosomal recessive form of adenomatous polyposis coli</a>.</p><p>
<b>Family planning</b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>The optimal time for determination of genetic risk and discussion of the availability of prenatal/<a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/preimplantation-genetic-testing/">preimplantation genetic testing</a> is before pregnancy. Similarly, decisions about testing to determine the genetic status of at-risk asymptomatic family members are best made before pregnancy.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>It is appropriate to offer <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/genetic-counseling/">genetic counseling</a> (including discussion of potential risks to offspring and reproductive options) to young adults who are affected or at risk.</div></li></ul><p><b>DNA banking.</b> Because it is likely that testing methodology and our understanding of genes, allelic variants, and diseases will improve in the future, consideration should be given to banking DNA from probands in whom a molecular diagnosis has not been confirmed (i.e., the causative genetic alteration/s are unknown).</p></div><div id="pjs.Prenatal_Testing_and_Preimplantation"><h3>Prenatal Testing and Preimplantation Genetic Testing</h3><p>Once the <i>STK11</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> has been identified in an affected family member, <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/prenatal-testing/">prenatal testing</a> and <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/preimplantation-genetic-testing/">preimplantation genetic testing</a> for PJS are possible.</p><p>Differences in perspective may exist among medical professionals and within families regarding the use of <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/prenatal-testing/">prenatal testing</a>. While most centers would consider use of prenatal testing to be a personal decision, discussion of these issues may be helpful.</p></div></div><div id="pjs.Resources"><h2 id="_pjs_Resources_">Resources</h2><p>

<a href="/omim/602216" ref="pagearea=body&amp;targetsite=entrez&amp;targetcat=term&amp;targettype=omim">602216</a></td><td rowspan="1" colspan="1" style="text-align:left;vertical-align:top;">SERINE/THREONINE PROTEIN KINASE 11; STK11</td></tr></tbody></table></div></div><div id="pjs.Molecular_Pathogenesis"><h3>Molecular Pathogenesis</h3><p><i>STK11</i> encodes a serine/threonine-protein kinase (STK11), a multi-tasking tumor suppressor that has a role in apoptosis, cell cycle arrest, cell proliferation, cell polarity, and energy metabolism.</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>STK11 activates <i>TSC2</i> through an AMP-dependent protein kinase [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.corradetti.2006.6347">Corradetti &#x00026; Guan 2006</a>] leading to accumulation of mTOR, which is critical for protein translation. Loss of STK11 increased tumor cell metabolism via mTOR [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.faubert.2014.2554">Faubert et al 2014</a>].</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>STK11 expression was shown to cause apoptosis in epithelial cells [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.marignani.2005.15">Marignani 2005</a>].</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>STK11 was reported to phosphorylate AMPK and several other members of the AMPK-related subfamily of kinases including the microtubule affinity-regulating kinases (MARKs) to regulate cell polarity [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.tanwar.2012.4394">Tanwar et al 2012</a>].</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Through activation of AMPK by phosphorylation, STK11 plays a role in energy metabolism [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.udd.2011.425">Udd &#x00026; M&#x000e4;kel&#x000e4; 2011</a>].</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Loss of STK11 increased tumor-promoting cytokines with reduced expression of PD-1 ligand in mouse and human tumors [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.koyama.2016.999">Koyama et al 2016</a>].</div></li></ul><p>More than 300 <i>STK11</i> pathogenic variants have been reported in persons with Peutz-Jeghers syndrome. All types of variants have been reported, from <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/missense/">missense</a> variants to whole-<a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gene/">gene</a> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/deletion/">deletion</a>.</p><p><b>Mechanism of disease causation.</b> Loss of function</p></div><div id="pjs.Cancer_and_Benign_Tumors"><h3>Cancer and Benign Tumors</h3><p><b>Non-small cell lung cancer.</b> Sporadic pathogenic variants in <i>STK11</i> are observed in one third of non-small cell lung cancers; <i>STK11</i> is the third most commonly mutated <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gene/">gene</a>, following <i>p53</i> and <i>KRAS</i>. Demographically, <i>STK11</i> variants in lung tumors appear to be frequent in male smokers of northern European background and are associated with poorly differentiated tumors. In addition, <i>STK11</i> pathogenic variants are more frequently observed in association with concomitant activating <i>KRAS</i> pathogenic variants. Such <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/digenic/">digenic</a> variants have been associated with a poorer prognosis compared to a <i>KRAS</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> alone [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.p_cuchet.2017.23831">P&#x000e9;cuchet et al 2017</a>]. The high prevalence of <i>STK11</i> pathogenic variants in non-small cell lung cancers suggests that cigarette smoking could be particularly harmful for individuals with pathogenic variants in <i>STK11</i>. The presence of <i>STK11</i> pathogenic variants in these types of cancers may indicate a more aggressive tumor that has a poorer prognosis and is seen more often in smokers [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.p_cuchet.2017.23831">P&#x000e9;cuchet et al 2017</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.wang.2020.1060574">Wang et al 2020</a>].</p><p><b>Cervical cancer.</b> Sporadic pathogenic variants in <i>STK11</i> are observed in at least 20% of cervical cancers [<a class="bk_pop" href="#pjs.REF.wingo.2009.e5137">Wingo et al 2009</a>]. Among the variants observed in tumors, half are single-nucleotide variants or small indels and the other half are large-fragment deletions in either one <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/allele/">allele</a> or both alleles. <i>STK11</i> inactivation in primary cervical tumors has been associated with accelerated disease progression. These results suggest that <i>STK11</i> is a major cervical tumor suppressor for <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/sporadic/">sporadic</a> cancers.</p></div></div><div id="pjs.Chapter_Notes"><h2 id="_pjs_Chapter_Notes_">Chapter Notes</h2><div id="pjs.Author_Notes"><h3>Author Notes</h3><p>Dr Amos is a research scholar in the Cancer Prevention Research Institute of Texas supported by RR170048.
