Major depressive disorder (MDD) is a debilitating neuropsychiatric condition 
characterized by complex and multifactorial pathobiology. Emerging evidence 
increasingly implicates oligodendrocyte (OL) dysfunction as a key 
pathophysiological mechanism in MDD, extending beyond their classical role in 
myelination to include critical contributions to neural plasticity, metabolic 
support, and circuit regulation. Such dysfunctions manifest primarily as myelin 
abnormalities and are closely associated with depression-related alterations in 
brain connectivity and emotional processing. Collectively, these findings 
provide a novel framework for understanding the cellular and molecular 
underpinnings of depression. This review systematically summarizes current 
evidence on the involvement of OLs in the pathogenesis of MDD, with particular 
emphasis on their interactions with neurons, astrocytes, and microglia within 
the central nervous system. In addition, we discuss emerging OL-targeted 
therapeutic strategies, including pharmacological, genetic, and environmental 
interventions that may promote remyelination or restore glial function. Finally, 
we highlight key knowledge gaps and propose future research directions aimed at 
clarifying the contribution of OL biology to depressive disorders and improving 
treatment outcomes.
