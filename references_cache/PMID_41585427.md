PURPOSE: This study aimed to compare the outcomes of proton radiotherapy alone
versus its combination with immuno-oncology agents (Proton-IO) or tyrosine
kinase inhibitors (Proton-TKI) in patients with intermediate- to advanced-stage
hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC).
METHODS: We retrospectively reviewed the medical records of 137 patients with
Barcelona Clinic Liver Cancer (BCLC) stage B or C HCC treated with proton
radiotherapy at Linkou Chang Gung Memorial Hospital between 2020 and 2023.
Patients were stratified into three groups: proton radiotherapy alone (n = 64),
Proton-IO (n = 44), and Proton-TKI (n = 29). The most frequently used
immuno-oncology agents were atezolizumab-bevacizumab (n = 33) and pembrolizumab
(n = 5). Tyrosine kinase inhibitors (TKIs) included lenvatinib (n = 16) and
sorafenib (n = 13).
RESULTS: With a median follow-up of 30 months, patients in the Proton-IO group
were significantly associated with higher 2-year overall survival (OS) rates
compared with those receiving Proton-TKI or proton radiotherapy alone (77.0% vs.
47.2% vs. 52.7%; p = 0.002). Proton-IO was also associated with significantly
longer time to progression (TTP) and distant metastasis-free survival (DMFS)
(2-year TTP: 50.5% vs. 28.1% vs. 24.2%, p = 0.003; 2-year DMFS: 83.4% vs. 61.1%
vs. 67.2%, p = 0.027). No significant differences in 2-year local control rates
were observed among the treatment groups (97.7% vs. 92.9% vs. 86.8%; p = 0.230).
Multivariate analysis identified Proton-IO as an independent predictor of
improved OS (p < 0.001), TTP (p < 0.001), and DMFS (p = 0.004). Grade 3-4 upper
gastrointestinal (UGI) bleeding was observed in 2 (1.5%) patients (proton
monotherapy, n = 1; Proton-IO, n = 1). There were no significant differences
among the groups in the incidence of grade ≥3 UGI bleeding, liver toxicity,
colitis, rib fractures, or hematologic adverse events.
CONCLUSION: In BCLC stage B/C HCC, proton radiotherapy combined with
immunotherapy was significantly associated with higher OS, TTP, and DMFS without
an increase in grade ≥3 toxicity compared with proton radiotherapy alone or
Proton-TKIs.

Plain Language Summary: Liver cancer is often diagnosed at a stage when surgery
is no longer an option, and patients rely on drug treatments such as targeted
medications or immunotherapy. Proton radiotherapy is a newer form of radiation
treatment that can precisely target tumors while reducing damage to nearby
healthy tissues. This study looked at how effective proton radiotherapy is when
used alone or in combination with either immunotherapy or targeted drugs in
patients with more advanced liver cancer. We reviewed the records of 137
patients with intermediate to advanced liver cancer who were treated with proton
radiotherapy. Some received only proton therapy, while others received it along
with immunotherapy or targeted medications. We followed these patients for a
median of 30 months. The results showed that patients who received proton
radiotherapy combined with immunotherapy lived longer and were less likely to
have the cancer spread to other parts of the body, compared to those who
received proton therapy alone or with targeted drugs. The combination with
immunotherapy also prevented the cancer from growing or spreading. Importantly,
this treatment was safe and did not lead to more serious side effects such as
bleeding, liver failure, or blood-related issues. In conclusion, combining
proton radiotherapy with immunotherapy appears to be a promising and safe
treatment option for patients with more advanced liver cancer, offering better
outcomes than using proton therapy alone or with targeted drugs.
