itemprop="description"><h2 id="_ptdss1-lmhd_Summary_">Summary</h2><div><h4 class="inline">Clinical characteristics.</h4><p><i>PTDSS1</i>-related Lenz-Majewski hyperostotic dysplasia (LMHD) is characterized by cutis laxa and progressive bone sclerosis, primarily affecting the skull and long bones. Individuals with classic <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD typically presents in infancy with cutis laxa, prominent cutaneous veins, characteristic craniofacial features (disproportionately large head, broad forehead, delayed closure of the fontanelles, hypertelorism, large floppy ears, nasal obstruction&#x000a0;/ choanal atresia, macrostomia, thin vermilion of the lips, dental enamel hypoplasia, and prognathism or retrognathia), brachydactyly and syndactyly of the digits, early-onset osteosclerosis (involving the skull, spine, diaphyses of the long bones, clavicles, and ribs), severe growth deficiency, and significant developmental delays. Additional features can include genitourinary anomalies in males, inguinal hernia, ophthalmologic manifestations, hearing loss, and hydrocephalus. Attenuated <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD is characterized by minimal or mild cutis laxa, slower progression of hyperostosis, preserved or mildly affected development, and normal stature.</p></div><div><h4 class="inline">Diagnosis/testing.</h4><p>The diagnosis of <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD is established in a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> with characteristic clinical and imaging findings and a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a> pathogenic <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gain-of-function/">gain-of-function</a> variant in <i>PTDSS1</i> identified by <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/molecular-genetic-testing/">molecular genetic testing</a>.</p></div><div><h4 class="inline">Management.</h4><p><i>Treatment of manifestations:</i> Treatment of skeletal manifestations per orthopedist; consider physical therapy, occupational therapy, and assisted devices for mobility; decompression of cervical spine stenosis as needed; treatment of hydrocephalus as needed per neurosurgeon; treatment of respiratory difficulty and obstructive sleep apnea per otolaryngologist and/or pulmonologist; careful airway evaluation prior to surgical procedures; supportive therapies for those with developmental delays; individualized education plan for learning disorders and school performance issues; treatment of dental enamel hypoplasia per dentist; standard treatments for genitourinary anomalies, delayed puberty, inguinal hernia, vision issues, and hearing loss; consider dermatology referral for cosmetic concerns due to cutis laxa.</p><p><i>Surveillance:</i> Growth assessment and orthopedic evaluation annually or as determined by the orthopedist to monitor joint and skeletal manifestations; brain and spine MRI as needed; assess for manifestations of sleep apnea at each visit; polysomnography as needed; dental evaluation with frequency per dentist; ophthalmology evaluation with frequency per ophthalmologist; audiology evaluation as needed; monitor developmental progress, educational needs, and family needs at each visit.</p><p><i>Agents/circumstances to avoid:</i> Activities/procedures that involve extreme neck extension and flexion in individuals with craniovertebral junction stenosis.</p></div><div><h4 class="inline">Genetic counseling.</h4><p><i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD is an <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/autosomal-dominant/">autosomal dominant</a> disorder. All probands reported to date with <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD whose parents have undergone <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/molecular-genetic-testing/">molecular genetic testing</a> have had the disorder as the result of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/de-novo/"><i>de novo</i></a>
<i>PTDSS1</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a>. Risk to the parents of the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> of having another affected pregnancy is presumed to be low as the proband most likely has a <i>de novo PTDSS1</i> pathogenic variant. There is, however, a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/recurrence-risk/">recurrence risk</a> (~1%) to sibs based on the possibility of parental <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gonadal-mosaicism/">gonadal mosaicism</a>. Given this risk, prenatal and <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/preimplantation-genetic-testing/">preimplantation genetic testing</a> may be considered.</p></div></div><div id="ptdss1-lmhd.GeneReview_Scope"><h2 id="_ptdss1-lmhd_GeneReview_Scope_"><i>GeneReview</i> Scope</h2><div id="ptdss1-lmhd.Td" class="table"><p class="large-table-link" style="display:none"><span class="right"><a href="/books/NBK621299/table/ptdss1-lmhd.Td/?report=objectonly" target="object">View in own window</a></span></p><div class="large_tbl" id="__ptdss1-lmhd.Td_lrgtbl__"><table><thead><tr><th id="hd_h_ptdss1-lmhd.Td_1_1_1_1" scope="col" rowspan="1" colspan="1" style="text-align:left;vertical-align:middle;"><i>PTDSS1</i>-Related Lenz-Majewski Hyperostotic Dysplasia (LMHD): Phenotypic Spectrum</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td headers="hd_h_ptdss1-lmhd.Td_1_1_1_1" rowspan="1" colspan="1" style="text-align:left;vertical-align:middle;"><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Classic <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Attenuated <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD</div></li></ul>

Reference to "pathogenic variants" in this <i>GeneReview</i> is understood to include likely pathogenic variants. (2) Identification of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a> <i>PTDSS1</i> variant of <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/uncertain-significance/">uncertain significance</a> does not establish or rule out the diagnosis.</p><p>Molecular genetic testing approaches can include a combination of <b><a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gene/">gene</a>-targeted testing</b> (<a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/multigene-panel/">multigene panel</a>) and <b>comprehensive</b>
<b><a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/genomic/">genomic</a> testing</b> (<a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/exome-sequencing/">exome sequencing</a>, <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/genome-sequencing/">genome sequencing</a>). Gene-targeted testing requires that the clinician determine which gene(s) are likely involved (see <a href="#ptdss1-lmhd.Option_1">Option 1</a>), whereas comprehensive genomic testing does not (see <a href="#ptdss1-lmhd.Option_2">Option 2</a>). Note: Single-gene testing (<a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/sequence-analysis/">sequence analysis</a> of <i>PTDSS1</i>) would NOT generally be recommended, as the phenotypic features of <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD are broad and can appear over time.</p><div id="ptdss1-lmhd.Option_1"><h4>Option 1</h4><p><b>A <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/multigene-panel/">multigene panel</a></b> that includes <i>PTDSS1</i> and other genes of interest (see <a href="#ptdss1-lmhd.Differential_Diagnosis">Differential Diagnosis</a>) is most likely to identify the genetic cause of the condition while limiting identification of pathogenic variants and variants of <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/uncertain-significance/">uncertain significance</a> in genes that do not explain the underlying <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a>. Note: (1) The genes included in the panel and the diagnostic <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/sensitivity/">sensitivity</a> of the testing used for each <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gene/">gene</a> vary by laboratory and are likely to change over time. (2) Some multigene panels may include genes not associated with the condition discussed in this <i>GeneReview</i>. (3) In some laboratories, panel options may include a custom laboratory-designed panel and/or custom phenotype-focused <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/exome/">exome</a> analysis that includes genes specified by the clinician. (4) Methods used in a panel may include <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/sequence-analysis/">sequence analysis</a>, <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/deletion-duplication-analysis/">deletion/duplication analysis</a>, and/or other non-sequencing-based tests.</p><p>For an introduction to multigene panels click <a href="/books/n/gene/app5/#app5.Multigene_Panels">here</a>. More detailed information for clinicians ordering genetic tests can be found <a href="/books/n/gene/app5/#app5.Multigene_Panels_FAQs">here</a>.</p></div><div id="ptdss1-lmhd.Option_2"><h4>Option 2</h4><p>When the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a> is indistinguishable from many other skeletal dysplasias, <b>comprehensive</b>

Increasing recognition of phenotypic variability in <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD has led to the proposed classification of classic and attenuated phenotypes. Individuals with classic <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD typically present in infancy with cutis laxa, characteristic craniofacial features, early-onset osteosclerosis, severe growth failure, and significant developmental delays. Attenuated <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD is characterized by slower progression of bone changes, preserved or mildly affected development, and normal stature. style="text-align:left;vertical-align:middle;">0/1</td></tr></tbody></table></div></div><div id="ptdss1-lmhd.Classic_PTDSS1Related_LMHD_1"><h4>Classic <i>PTDSS1-</i>Related LMHD</h4><p><b>Integument.</b> Individuals with classic <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD typically present with loose, atrophic skin and prominent cutaneous veins, including scalp veins. Individuals had an almost complete absence of dermal elastic tissue in a punch biopsy taken from the forearm [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.kaye.1974.115">Kaye et al 1974</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.sousa.2014.70">Sousa et al 2014</a>]. The atrophic skin along with sparse subcutaneous fat has led to classic <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD being compared to progeria [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.majewski.2000.335">Majewski 2000</a>]. Lipodystrophy has been reported of the buttocks [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.piard.2018.668">Piard et al 2018</a>] and can occur in areas with cutis laxa [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.afifi.2019.2039">Afifi et al 2019</a>]. Cutis laxa seems to improve with age [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.tamhankar.2015.392">Tamhankar et al 2015</a>].</p><p>Individuals with classic <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD often have sparse scalp hair.</p><p><b>Craniofacial features.</b> Postnatal head circumference for age tends to remain below the third centile. However, in individuals with marked short stature, the head is relatively macrocephalic compared to height [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.gorlin.1983.129">Gorlin &#x00026; Whitley 1983</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.nishimura.1997.87">Nishimura et al 1997</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.tamhankar.2015.392">Tamhankar et al 2015</a>]. Anterior fontanelle closure is delayed and often remains open until age four years, as observed in several individuals [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.robinow.1977.417">Robinow et al 1977</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.majewski.2000.335">Majewski 2000</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.saraiva.2000.198">Saraiva 2000</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.wattanasirichaigoon.2004.137">Wattanasirichaigoon et al 2004</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.sousa.2014.70">Sousa et al 2014</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.tamhankar.2015.392">Tamhankar et al 2015</a>]. In one individual, closure was delayed until age nine years [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.piard.2018.668">Piard et al 2018</a>].</p><p>Individuals with classic <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD have characteristic facial features that include a broad or prominent forehead. Hypertelorism is found in all individuals. One affected child, evaluated at age four years, had an inner canthal distance of 3.5 cm (more than two standard deviations [SD] above the mean) and an outer canthal distance of 9 cm (more than one SD above the mean) [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.saraiva.2000.198">Saraiva 2000</a>]. An adult with classic <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD was also described as having large, prominent eyes [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.majewski.2000.335">Majewski 2000</a>]. Large, floppy ears are found in many individuals and have been described as poorly lobulated and posteriorly angulated [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.chrzanowska.1989.470">Chrzanowska et al 1989</a>].</p><p>Nasal obstruction is commonly observed in infancy, often beginning immediately after birth. Affected infants have been reported to experience nasal stuffiness with mouth breathing [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.saraiva.2000.198">Saraiva 2000</a>]. Reported causes include nasal congestion that improves with antihistamines [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.whyte.2015.606">Whyte et al 2015</a>], and unilateral or bilateral choanal stenosis or atresia requiring surgical correction [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.braham.1969.20">Braham 1969</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.robinow.1977.417">Robinow et al 1977</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.gorlin.1983.129">Gorlin &#x00026; Whitley 1983</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.chrzanowska.1989.470">Chrzanowska et al 1989</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.majewski.2000.335">Majewski 2000</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.saraiva.2000.198">Saraiva 2000</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.sousa.2014.70">Sousa et al 2014</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.mizuguchi.2015.1567">Mizuguchi et al 2015</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.tamhankar.2015.392">Tamhankar et al 2015</a>].</p><p>Nasal obstruction has been reported to contribute to feeding difficulties in some individuals. Several individuals required enteral feeding support, including one extremely preterm infant who was never successfully weaned from a feeding tube for growth support [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.nishimura.1997.87">Nishimura et al 1997</a>]. In another reported individual, nasal obstruction led to decreasing oral intake and significant weight loss [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.whyte.2015.606">Whyte et al 2015</a>].</p><p>Macrostomia has been observed, including an intercommissural distance of 4.5 cm (more than 2 SD above the mean) in a girl age four years [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.saraiva.2000.198">Saraiva 2000</a>], and may be accompanied by downturned oral commissures. The vermilion of the upper and lower lips are thin, with the upper lip sometimes appearing almost inverted [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.bayram.2025.1423">Bayram et al 2025</a>]. However, longitudinal follow up of one individual showed that the vermilion of the upper and lower lips and tongue got thicker with age [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.majewski.2000.335">Majewski 2000</a>].</p><p>Prognathism or retrognathia has been reported in 17 individuals, including two with retrognathia. One individual initially had retrognathia in infancy but later developed prognathism in adulthood as a result of progressive sclerosis of the mandible [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.piard.2018.668">Piard et al 2018</a>].</p><p><b>Dental manifestations.</b> Dental enamel hypoplasia/dysplasia is common. Individuals with classic <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD may experience a range of dental issues, including hypodontia, oligodontia, dental crowding, malocclusion, and other dental anomalies, which can impact oral health and function [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.whyte.2015.606">Whyte et al 2015</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.piard.2018.668">Piard et al 2018</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.afifi.2019.2039">Afifi et al 2019</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.maden_bedel.2024.104910">Maden Bedel et al 2024</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.kobari.2025.e70112">Kobari et al 2025</a>].</p><p><b>Growth deficiency.</b> Individuals with classic <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD have severe growth deficiency. A history of intrauterine growth restriction is common. At birth, infants are symmetrically small for gestational age [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.robinow.1977.417">Robinow et al 1977</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.wattanasirichaigoon.2004.137">Wattanasirichaigoon et al 2004</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.sousa.2014.70">Sousa et al 2014</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.piard.2018.668">Piard et al 2018</a>]. Height ranges from approximately 2.3 to 10.4 SD below the mean, and weight ranges from 2.0 to 8.7 SD below the mean. Adults with classic <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD typically reach a stature of approximately 120-135&#x0202f;cm [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.chrzanowska.1989.470">Chrzanowska et al 1989</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.majewski.2000.335">Majewski 2000</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.saraiva.2000.198">Saraiva 2000</a>]. To date, growth hormone deficiency has not been reported in individuals with classic <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD.</p><p><b>Hand and digit findings.</b> Radiographs show shortened or absent middle phalanges, particularly affecting the fourth and fifth fingers, while often sparing the thumbs. This feature is accompanied by proximal symphalangism, frequently with cutaneous syndactyly of second to fifth digits. Nails are often large and/or hyperconvex [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.afifi.2019.2039">Afifi et al 2019</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.maden_bedel.2024.104910">Maden Bedel et al 2024</a>], and the fingertips can be bulbous. Some individuals have had a single transverse palmar crease [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.bayram.2025.1423">Bayram et al 2025</a>].</p><p><b>Other skeletal features.</b> A striking pattern of bone sclerosis, particularly affecting the cranium, vertebral column, and diaphysis of tubular bones, with relative epimetaphyseal osteopenia, has been noted in classic <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD. Bone sclerosis varies considerably between individuals but is progressive. Facial nerve palsy was observed in one individual and hypothesized to result from cranial nerve compression due to sclerosis [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.wattanasirichaigoon.2004.137">Wattanasirichaigoon et al 2004</a>]. Other complications include craniovertebral junction stenosis with myelopathy of the upper cervical cord [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.mizuguchi.2015.1567">Mizuguchi et al 2015</a>]. Joint-related problems are consistently reported to affect mobility, particularly through impaired hand grip strength, limited elbow extension or flexure contracture, and restricted movement of the shoulders and knees [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.nishimura.1997.87">Nishimura et al 1997</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.sousa.2014.70">Sousa et al 2014</a>]. However, despite joint issues, one individual was able to walk quickly at age 30 years [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.majewski.2000.335">Majewski 2000</a>].</p><p>Serum calcium, vitamin&#x0202f;D, and alkaline phosphatase concentrations are typically within normal limits, although there have been several reports of mildly elevated alkaline phosphatase, suggesting that the hyperostotic <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a> arises from intrinsic bone dysplasia rather than systemic metabolic disturbances.</p><p>No bone fractures have been reported, indicating that the increased bone density does not correspond to an elevated risk of fractures [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.whyte.2015.606">Whyte et al 2015</a>].</p><p><b>Intellectual disability (ID).</b> To date, all reported individuals with classic <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD have some degree of ID, ranging from mild to severe. Approximately half (11/23) are known to have severe ID and are dependent on a caregiver. The distribution of ID severity among the 23 reported individuals is as follows: borderline (1/23), mild (3/23), mild-to-moderate (1/23), moderate (3/23), moderate-to-severe (1/23), severe (11/23), and unspecified (3/23).</p><p><b>Developmental delay.</b> Up to 93% of individuals with <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD exhibit speech impairment, ranging from absent expressive speech to some use of sentences [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.sousa.2014.70">Sousa et al 2014</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.piard.2018.668">Piard et al 2018</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.maden_bedel.2024.104910">Maden Bedel et al 2024</a>]. Only one reported individual with classic <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD demonstrated relatively preserved speech capabilities [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.chrzanowska.1989.470">Chrzanowska et al 1989</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.sousa.2014.70">Sousa et al 2014</a>]. Longitudinal follow up suggests that speech may improve gradually with time, but verbal ability varies considerably between individuals. One child began using a single word at age 2.5 years and was using multiple words by age 9.5 years [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.majewski.2000.335">Majewski 2000</a>].</p><p>Although details on gross motor, fine motor, and social development are limited, existing reports highlight a pattern of developmental delay that parallels the speech delay [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.wattanasirichaigoon.2004.137">Wattanasirichaigoon et al 2004</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.tamhankar.2015.392">Tamhankar et al 2015</a>]. At age eight months, one individual could only lift his head; the Gesell Developmental Scale showed that he had an adaptive development quotient 16 (1.3 months), gross motor development quotient 43 (2.5 months), fine motor quotient 53 (3 months), language quotient 71 (4 months), and personal-social quotient 36 (2 months) [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.zhang.2025.1617541">Zhang et al 2025</a>].</p><p><b>Genitourinary anomalies in males.</b> Among 13 reported males, six had cryptorchidism. One male had penile hypospadias and chordee [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.robinow.1977.417">Robinow et al 1977</a>].</p><p><b>Hernia.</b> Six individuals had unilateral inguinal hernia. One individual had bilateral inguinal hernia [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.braham.1969.20">Braham 1969</a>].</p><p><b>Ophthalmologic problems.</b> A variety of eye problems are reported in individuals with classic <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD, including optic atrophy (2/23) [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.tamhankar.2015.392">Tamhankar et al 2015</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.whyte.2015.606">Whyte et al 2015</a>], hyperopia (1/23) [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.chrzanowska.1989.470">Chrzanowska et al 1989</a>], small iris (1/23) [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.saraiva.2000.198">Saraiva 2000</a>], and entropion (1/23) [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.wattanasirichaigoon.2004.137">Wattanasirichaigoon et al 2004</a>].</p><p><b>Hearing loss.</b> Five individuals have been reported to have hearing problems. Among them, one had bilateral low-frequency hearing loss [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.kobari.2025.e70112">Kobari et al 2025</a>], and another developed bilateral conductive hearing deficits (40-80 dB) at age ten years [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.chrzanowska.1989.470">Chrzanowska et al 1989</a>]; hearing deficits in the remaining individuals were not fully described. Hearing impairment is thought to be related, at least in some individuals, to progressive cranial deformity [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.piard.2018.668">Piard et al 2018</a>].</p><p><b>Brain Imaging.</b> To date, only 14 individuals with classic <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD have undergone brain imaging. The most common finding, observed in 50% (7/14), is diffuse cortical atrophy. Ventricular enlargement has also been noted in a few individuals. This may result from progressive osteopetrosis and craniovertebral junction stenosis causing communicating hydrocephalus with associated periventricular edema [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.mizuguchi.2015.1567">Mizuguchi et al 2015</a>]. One individual benefited from ventriculoperitoneal shunting followed by foramen magnum decompression [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.mizuguchi.2015.1567">Mizuguchi et al 2015</a>]. Additional neuroimaging findings include dysgenesis of the corpus callosum, pituitary hypoplasia, and focal cerebral atrophy with hemosiderin deposits in the cerebellum [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.saraiva.2000.198">Saraiva 2000</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.wattanasirichaigoon.2004.137">Wattanasirichaigoon et al 2004</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.sousa.2014.70">Sousa et al 2014</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.piard.2018.668">Piard et al 2018</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.maden_bedel.2024.104910">Maden Bedel et al 2024</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.zhang.2025.1617541">Zhang et al 2025</a>].</p><p>
<b>Other</b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Elevated total thyroxine and free thyroxine, low total triiodothyronine (TT3), and normal thyroid-stimulating hormone; no treatment was initiated (one individual) [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.zhang.2025.1617541">Zhang et al 2025</a>]</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Recurrent otitis media and low immunoglobulin levels without lymphopenia or neutropenia (one individual) [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.maden_bedel.2024.104910">Maden Bedel et al 2024</a>]</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Delayed puberty was observed in one female. Menarche started at age 19 years. She had normal breast development [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.majewski.2000.335">Majewski 2000</a>].</div></li></ul><p><b>Prognosis.</b> Based on current data, several adults have been reported. The oldest individual reported was age 36 years, demonstrating that survival into adulthood is possible [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.sousa.2014.70">Sousa et al 2014</a>]. Another reported individual was age 30 years at the time of the report. Despite neurodevelopmental delay and progressive sclerosis, she had not experienced any serious illness. Although she had limited mobility in her upper extremities due to proximal symphalangism of the hands, she remained able to walk quickly [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.majewski.2000.335">Majewski 2000</a>]. There is no evidence of psychomotor regression.</p><p>Since many adults with mild intellectual disability and/or mild features may not have undergone genetic testing, it is possible that adults with this condition are underrecognized and underreported.</p></div><div id="ptdss1-lmhd.Attenuated_PTDSS1Related_LMH_1"><h4>Attenuated <i>PTDSS1-</i>Related LMHD</h4><p><b>Integument.</b> Individuals with attenuated <i>PTDSS1-</i>related LMHD also have skin findings of cutis laxa with prominent cutaneous veins. However, it is minimal compared to individuals with classic <i>PTDSS1-</i>related LMHD. Individuals with attenuated <i>PTDSS1-</i>related LMHD also have sparse hair [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.kobari.2025.e70112">Kobari et al 2025</a>].</p><p><b>Dental manifestations.</b> Delayed eruption of the permanent teeth with taurodontism (vertically enlarged pulp chambers and apical root furcation) was reported in an individual with attenuated <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.bayram.2025.1423">Bayram et al 2025</a>].</p><p><b>Growth.</b> In those with attenuated <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD, growth is generally preserved. One individual with attenuated <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD reached a height of 164.5 cm at age 17 years [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.dateki.2007.686">Dateki et al 2007</a>].</p><p><b>Hand and digit findings.</b> Brachydactyly is less pronounced in those with attenuated <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD, and proximal symphalangism has not been reported [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.dateki.2007.686">Dateki et al 2007</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.bayram.2025.1423">Bayram et al 2025</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.kobari.2025.e70112">Kobari et al 2025</a>].</p><p><b>Other skeletal features.</b> Skeletal surveys typically do not show evidence of bone sclerosis in early life. However, by late childhood, radiographic imaging may reveal slowly progressive hyperostosis involving the calvaria, skull base, mandible, metaepiphyseal regions of the long bones, sacrum, and acetabulum. A key distinguishing feature is the absence of diaphyseal hyperostosis, which clearly distinguishes attenuated <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD from classic <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD. Notably, diaphyseal undermodeling of the long bones (reduced/absent diaphyseal narrowing) is observed in attenuated <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.dateki.2007.686">Dateki et al 2007</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.bayram.2025.1423">Bayram et al 2025</a>].</p><p>The progressive thickening of the skull base and calvaria could lead to increased intracranial pressure and cranial nerve entrapment affecting vision, hearing, and facial nerves, as seen in other disorders with progressive hyperostosis. One individual with attenuated <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD developed obstructive hydrocephalus and increased intracranial pressure [Kochakorn Buasri, unpublished data]. To date, this complication has not been reported in other individuals with attenuated <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD, although there are very few reported individuals.</p><p><b>Intellectual disability.</b> Among the three reported individuals with attenuated <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD, two individuals have mild intellectual disability, and the third individual has a moderate degree of cognitive impairment [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.dateki.2007.686">Dateki et al 2007</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.bayram.2025.1423">Bayram et al 2025</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.kobari.2025.e70112">Kobari et al 2025</a>]. One individual began walking independently at age 16 months and spoke her first words at age 20 months [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.kobari.2025.e70112">Kobari et al 2025</a>].</p><p><b>Behavioral concerns.</b> Attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder has been reported in one individual with attenuated <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.kobari.2025.e70112">Kobari et al 2025</a>]. No other neurobehavioral issues have been reported.</p></div></div><div id="ptdss1-lmhd.GenotypePhenotype_Correlatio"><h3>Genotype-Phenotype Correlations</h3><p>Individuals with the <i>PTDSS1</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> <a href="/books/NBK621299/table/ptdss1-lmhd.T.ptdss1_pathogenic_variants/?report=objectonly" target="object" rid-ob="figobptdss1lmhdTptdss1pathogenicvariants">c.284G&#x0003e;A (p.Arg95Gln)</a> are reported to have a milder <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a> characterized by slower progression of hyperostosis and normal stature consistent with attenuated <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.bayram.2025.1423">Bayram et al 2025</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.kobari.2025.e70112">Kobari et al 2025</a>]. However, due to the limited number of genetically confirmed individuals, establishing a robust <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/genotype/">genotype</a>-phenotype correlation remains challenging.</p><p>For other <i>PTDSS1</i> pathogenic variants, a spectrum of phenotypic presentations has been observed. Among four individuals with the same <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a>, <a href="/books/NBK621299/table/ptdss1-lmhd.T.ptdss1_pathogenic_variants/?report=objectonly" target="object" rid-ob="figobptdss1lmhdTptdss1pathogenicvariants">c.1058A&#x0003e;G (p.Gln353Arg)</a>, one had borderline ID and preserved speech, another had mild-to-moderate ID, and two others individuals exhibited severe ID and absence of speech [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.chrzanowska.1989.470">Chrzanowska et al 1989</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.saraiva.2000.198">Saraiva 2000</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.maden_bedel.2024.104910">Maden Bedel et al 2024</a>].</p></div><div id="ptdss1-lmhd.Penetrance"><h3>Penetrance</h3><p>Penetrance is predicted to be complete. All reported individuals with a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gain-of-function/">gain-of-function</a> <i>PTDSS1</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> have features of LMHD.</p></div><div id="ptdss1-lmhd.Prevalence"><h3>Prevalence</h3><p><i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD is a rare disorder. To date, 23 individuals with LMHD have been reported.</p></div></div><div id="ptdss1-lmhd.Genetically_Related_Allelic"><h2 id="_ptdss1-lmhd_Genetically_Related_Allelic_">Genetically Related (Allelic) Disorders</h2><p>A <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/missense/">missense</a> variant in <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/exon/">exon</a> 4 of <i>PTDSS1</i> (c.409C&#x0003e;T [p.Leu137Phe]) was reported in a female age three years with developmental delay and autism [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.gracie.2022.1739">Gracie et al 2022</a>]. Skeletal examination was unremarkable, apart from the presence of an extra right rib, and growth parameters were normal. Functional studies indicated that the variant resulted in a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/loss-of-function/">loss-of-function</a> effect, in contrast to the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gain-of-function/">gain-of-function</a> variants typically associated with <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD [<a class="bk_pop" In the US, early intervention is a federally funded program available in all states that provides in-home services to target individual therapy needs.</p><p><b>Ages 3-5 years.</b> In the US, developmental preschool through the local public school district is recommended. Before placement, an evaluation is made to determine needed services and therapies and an individualized education plan (IEP) is developed for those who qualify based on established motor, language, social, or cognitive delay. The early intervention program typically assists with this transition. Developmental preschool is center based; for children too medically unstable to attend, home-based services are provided.</p><p><b>All ages.</b> Consultation with a developmental pediatrician is recommended to ensure the involvement of appropriate community, state, and educational agencies (US) and to support parents in maximizing quality of life. Some issues to consider:</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>IEP services:</div><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>An IEP provides specially designed instruction and related services to children who qualify.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>IEP services will be reviewed annually to determine whether any changes are needed.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Special education law requires that children participating in an IEP be in the least restrictive environment feasible at school and included in general education as much as possible, when and where appropriate.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Vision and hearing consultants should be a part of the child's IEP team to support access to academic material.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>PT, OT, and speech services will be provided in the IEP to the extent that the need affects the child's access to academic material. Beyond that, private supportive therapies based on the affected individual's needs may be considered. Specific recommendations regarding type of therapy can be made by a developmental pediatrician.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>As a child enters the teen years, a transition plan should be discussed and incorporated in the IEP. For those receiving IEP services, the public school district is required to provide services until age 21.</div></li></ul></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>A 504 plan (Section 504: a US federal statute that prohibits discrimination based on disability) can be considered for those who require accommodations or modifications such as front-of-class seating, assistive technology devices, classroom scribes, extra time between classes, modified assignments, and enlarged text.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Developmental Disabilities Administration (DDA) enrollment is recommended. DDA is a US public agency that provides services and support to qualified individuals. Eligibility differs by state but is typically determined by diagnosis and/or associated cognitive/adaptive disabilities.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Families with limited income and resources may also qualify for supplemental security income (SSI) for their child with a disability.</div></li></ul></div><div id="ptdss1-lmhd.Motor_Dysfunction"><h4>Motor Dysfunction</h4><p>
<b>Gross motor dysfunction</b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Physical therapy is recommended to maximize mobility and to reduce the risk for later-onset orthopedic complications (e.g., contractures, scoliosis, hip dislocation).</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Consider use of durable medical equipment and positioning devices as needed (e.g., wheelchairs, walkers, bath chairs, orthotics, adaptive strollers).</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>For muscle tone abnormalities including hypertonia or dystonia, consider involving appropriate specialists to aid in management of baclofen, tizanidine, Botox<sup>&#x000ae;</sup>, anti-parkinsonian medications, or orthopedic procedures.</div></li></ul><p><b>Fine motor dysfunction.</b> Occupational therapy is recommended for difficulty with fine motor skills that affect adaptive function such as feeding, grooming, dressing, and writing.</p><p><b>Oral motor dysfunction</b> should be assessed at each visit and clinical feeding evaluations and/or radiographic swallowing studies should be obtained for choking/gagging during feeds, poor weight gain, frequent respiratory illnesses, or feeding refusal that is not otherwise explained. Assuming that the child is safe to eat by mouth, feeding therapy (typically from an occupational or speech therapist) is recommended to help improve coordination or sensory-related feeding issues. Feeds can be thickened or chilled for safety. When feeding dysfunction is severe, an NG-tube or G-tube may be necessary.</p><p><b>Speech, language, and communication issues.</b> Speech-language evaluation should be considered early in development for children who have delayed communication milestones or who are not yet talking. PSS1 catalyzes the conversion of phosphatidylcholine or phosphatidylethanolamine into phosphatidylserine. Two enzymes, PSS1 and PSS2, are responsible for PS synthesis, with PSS1 being the predominant isoform in many tissues [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.kim.2014.1">Kim et al 2014</a>].</p><p>Gain-of-function <i>PTDSS1</i> pathogenic variants lead to increased enzymatic activity of PSS1, causing an aberrant accumulation of PS in cell membranes. Normally, PSS1 activity is tightly regulated via feedback inhibition by PS itself. However, in <i>PTDSS1</i>-related Lenz-Majewski hyperostotic dysplasia (LMHD), this negative feedback mechanism is impaired, resulting in unchecked PS synthesis. Excess PS may influence mineralization processes in bone tissue due to its high affinity for calcium ions. PS acts as a nucleation site for hydroxyapatite crystal formation, which is the primary mineral component of bone. Consequently, elevated levels of PS can disrupt normal bone remodeling, leading to pathologic calcification and increased bone density as seen in individuals with <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.sousa.2014.70">Sousa et al 2014</a>]. One study showed that the elevated PS concentration impaired osteoclast formation and activity and changed the quantity and composition of some phospholipid classes [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ptdss1-lmhd.REF.sugahara.2023.100443">Sugahara et al 2023</a>].</p><p>Functional studies on fibroblasts from individuals with <i>PTDSS1</i>-related LMHD have demonstrated a more pronounced increase in PS synthesis compared to wild-type.
