The pathogenesis of alcohol-associated liver disease (ALD) is complex and 
multifactorial. Several intracellular, intrahepatic, and extrahepatic factors 
influence development of early fatty liver injury leading to inflammation and 
fibrosis. Alcohol metabolism, cellular stress, and gut-derived factors 
contribute to hepatocyte and immune cell injury leading to cytokine and 
chemokine production. The pathogenesis of alcohol-associated hepatitis (AH), an 
advanced form of acute-on-chronic liver failure due to excessive chronic intake 
in patients with underlying liver disease, is not well understood. While 
pathogenic mechanisms in early ALD are studied, the pathogenesis of AH requires 
further investigation to help design effective drugs for patients.
