</div><div id="ncl.Clinical_Characteristics_of_Neuronal"><h2 id="_ncl_Clinical_Characteristics_of_Neuronal_">1.  Clinical Characteristics of Neuronal Ceroid Lipofuscinoses</h2><p>Neuronal ceroid lipofuscinoses (NCLs) are inherited neurodegenerative disorders caused by lysosomal accumulation of cellular ceroid lipofuscin, a waste product of lipids and proteins often found in the neurons of the retina and brain. The NCLs (commonly known as Batten disease) are generally characterized by progressive brain involvement, progressive retinal dystrophy, and shortened life span. NCLs are inherited in an <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/autosomal-recessive/">autosomal recessive</a> manner except <i>DNAJC5</i>-related NCL (CLN4 disease), which is inherited in an <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/autosomal-dominant/">autosomal dominant</a> manner. Collectively, the NCLs are the most common inherited neurodegenerative disorders in children.</p><p>Except for <i>CTSD</i>-related NCL (CLN10 disease), NCLs are typically characterized by a normal neonatal course and early childhood development followed by slowing of development that progresses to loss of previously acquired skills. The onset of nervous system deterioration depends on the genetic cause of the NCL. Affected individuals have at least two, if not all, of the following clinical manifestations: vision loss, psychomotor regression, dementia, epilepsy, and movement disorders.</p><p>Although these manifestations are typically present in all individuals with an NCL, the age of onset, range of manifestations, and rate of disease progression vary by the associated <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gene/">gene</a> (see <a href="#ncl.Genetic_Causes_of_Neuronal_Ceroid_Li">Section 2</a>).</p><p>The possibility of an NCL should be considered in a child with developmental delay, especially speech-language delays, plus any of the following:</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Progressive vision loss</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Epilepsy</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Ataxia</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Developmental regression</div></li></ul><p>Additionally, an individual with epilepsy with developmental regression or ataxia should be evaluated for neurodegenerative conditions such as NCL. Although these findings might be considered possible adverse effects of anti-seizure medications (ASMs), they are not common medication side effects.</p></div><div id="ncl.Genetic_Causes_of_Neuronal_Ceroid_Li"><h2 id="_ncl_Genetic_Causes_of_Neuronal_Ceroid_Li_">2.  Genetic Causes of Neuronal Ceroid Lipofuscinoses</h2><div id="ncl.Genes"><h3>Genes</h3><p>To date, pathogenic variants in 12 genes are confirmed to be associated with NCLs (see <a href="/books/NBK1428/table/ncl.T.neuronal_ceroid_lipofuscinoses_gen/?report=objectonly" target="object" rid-ob="figobnclTneuronalceroidlipofuscinosesgen">Table 1</a>). Most of these genes are associated with both classic and atypical phenotypes. The more common pathogenic variants in each NCL-related <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gene/">gene</a> result in a typical or classic NCL <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a>, while rarer variants may be associated with atypical phenotypes with a different age of onset and variable severity and progression. Atypical phenotypes that are attenuated (e.g., later onset and/or slower disease progression) are referred to as "protracted"; this phenomenon is postulated to be due to less deleterious pathogenic variants resulting in residual protein function.</p><p>Current NCL nomenclature incorporates the associated <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gene/">gene</a> (e.g., CLN1 disease is associated with pathogenic variants in <i>PPT1</i> [formerly <i>CLN1</i>]) and age of onset (e.g., CLN1 disease, infantile). <b>Presenting findings</b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Classic infantile <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a></div><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Age of onset: six to 18 months</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Typical development in the first few months of life. Between ages six and 12 months, developmental progress slows, then acquisition of motor and cognitive skills declines along with sleep disturbances, irritability, hypotonia, acquired microcephaly, and stereotypic hand movements. Seizures begin between ages one and three years followed by progressive loss of vision.</div></li></ul></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Atypical (late infantile, juvenile, or adult) <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a></div><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Age of onset: 18 months to adulthood [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ncl.REF.augustine.2021.38">Augustine et al 2021</a>]</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Later onset and slower disease progression than the infantile <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a>; however, chronology of neurodegenerative manifestations is similar, with onset of vision loss later than other neurologic manifestations.</div></li></ul></li></ul><p>
<b>Natural history</b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Classic infantile <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a>. By ages three to five years rapid neurodegeneration progresses to loss of motor skills (ambulation and trunk control) and cognitive decline. Children are dependent on others for care and typically require gastrostomy tube placement for feeding. Severe neurologic impairment occurs by age five years [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ncl.REF.simonati.2018">Simonati et al 2018</a>]. Loss of vision and light perception is a later manifestation compared to other CLN diseases. Life expectancy is between ages three and 12 years (median age: 9.5 years) [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ncl.REF.augustine.2021.38">Augustine et al 2021</a>].</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Atypical phenotypes</div><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Children with the late infantile <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a> experience severe neurologic impairment from ages six to 12 years. Life span is into the second or third decade of life (median life span reported: age 16.6 years).</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Individuals with the juvenile <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a> may have severe neurologic impairment in the third decade; median life expectancy is age 27 years [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ncl.REF.simonati.2018">Simonati et al 2018</a>].</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Life expectancy for adult-onset CLN1 disease is unknown.</div></li></ul></li></ul><p><b>Disease-modifying treatments.</b> None approved to date; however, enzyme replacement therapy (ERT) and <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gene/">gene</a>-based therapies for CLN1 disease are in development (see BDSRA Foundation, <a href="https://bdsrafoundation.org/research/clinical-studies/" ref="pagearea=body&amp;targetsite=external&amp;targetcat=link&amp;targettype=uri">Clinical Trials &#x00026; Natural History Studies</a>).</p></div><div id="ncl.CLN2_Disease"><h4>CLN2 Disease</h4><p><b>Presenting findings</b> (in untreated individuals)</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Classic late infantile <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a></div><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Age of onset: two to four years</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Typical development until age two to four years, after which developmental progress slows with notable language delay and onset of seizures. From age four years, onset of ataxia and rapid decline in motor and language skills, cognition, and vision [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ncl.REF.specchio.2020.213">Specchio et al 2020</a>].</div></li></ul></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Atypical <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a></div><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Age of onset: preschool to adulthood</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Later onset of manifestations with slower progression, often first presenting with seizures or ataxia and cerebellar atrophy or vision loss [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ncl.REF.louren_o.2021.519">Louren&#x000e7;o et al 2021</a>]. There is clinical and molecular overlap with spinocerebellar ataxia <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/autosomal-recessive/">autosomal recessive</a> 7.</div></li></ul></li></ul><p>
<b>Natural history</b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Classic infantile <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a> (untreated). Rapid loss of developmental skills with loss of ambulation by ages four to five years due to myoclonus and ataxia; around age six years most gross motor function is lost, and seizures become difficult to control. Feeding and swallowing difficulties often require gastrostomy tube placement by age six years. Dependence on others markedly increases between ages seven and ten years, when complete loss of vision occurs. Life expectancy is typically between ages six and 12 years [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ncl.REF.williams.2017.102">Williams et al 2017</a>].</div></li></ul><p><b>Disease-modifying treatment.</b> Targeted therapy (ERT) with cerliponase alfa has demonstrated clinically significant slowing of the decline of motor and language function, and likely prolongs life span [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ncl.REF.schulz.2024.60">Schulz et al 2024</a>] (see <a href="#ncl.Targeted_Therapy">Targeted Therapy</a>).</p></div><div id="ncl.CLN3_Disease"><h4>CLN3 Disease</h4><p>
<b>Presenting findings</b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Classic <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a></div><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Age of onset: four to seven years</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Onset of vision loss due to cone-rod dystrophy around age four to seven years, with rapid progression to complete blindness by around age nine to 12 years [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ncl.REF.kuper.2021.397">Kuper et al 2021</a>], often followed by or coinciding with neurobehavioral/psychiatric manifestations, cognitive decline, and sleep disturbance.</div></li></ul></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Protracted <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a></div><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Age of onset: juvenile to adult</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Onset of vision loss at a similar age to the classic <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a> but with slower disease progression with a more gradual decline in cognitive and motor function, and longer expected life span compared to the classic phenotype [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ncl.REF.cameron.2023.1833">Cameron et al 2023</a>]</div></li></ul></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div><i>CLN3</i>-associated <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/isolated/">isolated</a> retinal degeneration</div><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Age of onset: early onset (age 7-17 years) or late onset (second to fourth decade of life)</div></li></ul></li></ul><p>
<b>Natural history</b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Classic <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a>. Initial presentation of vision loss due to a cone-rod dystrophy with bulls eye maculopathy leading to diagnosis of pigmentary retinopathy and referral for further evaluation [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ncl.REF.kuper.2021.397">Kuper et al 2021</a>]. Complete loss of vision typically occurs around age nine to 12 years. Many individuals have mild speech delays in early childhood recognized prior to diagnosis. School-age children have inattentiveness and issues with learning. Seizures develop around age ten years, whereas gait and motor impairment (which are due to both loss of function and involuntary movements) and cognitive decline progress over the following decade. By age 20 years most individuals are unable to ambulate and are dependent on others for care. Life expectancy is into the third decade [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ncl.REF.ostergaard.2016.73">Ostergaard 2016</a>]. Cardiac findings including symptomatic bradycardia and left ventricular hypertrophy [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ncl.REF.rietdorf.2020.165643">Rietdorf et al 2020</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ncl.REF.handrup.2022.846240">Handrup et al 2022</a>] are typically evident after age 20 years; in one study the earliest manifestations were at age 14 years.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Protracted <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a>. Delayed age of onset of vision loss and other manifestations with a slower progression</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div><i>CLN3</i>-associated <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/isolated/">isolated</a> retinal degeneration. No progression to other neurologic manifestations of CLN3 disease [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ncl.REF.ku.2017.749">Ku et al 2017</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ncl.REF.smirnov.2021.278">Smirnov et al 2021</a>]</div></li></ul><p><b>Disease-modifying treatments.</b> None approved to date; however, therapies under development include (1) adeno-associated virus (AAV)-mediated <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gene/">gene</a> replacement, (2) messenger RNA (<a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/mrna/">mRNA</a>)-targeted antisense oligonucleotides (ASO), and (3) small molecule therapies (see BDSRA Foundation, <a href="https://bdsrafoundation.org/research/clinical-studies/" ref="pagearea=body&amp;targetsite=external&amp;targetcat=link&amp;targettype=uri">Clinical Trials &#x00026; Natural History Studies</a>).</p></div><div id="ncl.CLN4_Disease"><h4>CLN4 Disease</h4><p>
<b>Presenting findings</b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Age of onset: adolescence to adulthood; mean age 30 years</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Typical presentation is adult-onset behavioral changes, as well seizures, myoclonus, progressive tremor, ataxia, and dementia [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ncl.REF.guo.2024.15">Guo et al 2024</a>]</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Note: Retinal degeneration is typically not present.</div></li></ul><p><b>Natural history.</b> Not well described; however, life expectancy is shortened.</p><p><b>Disease-modifying treatments.</b> None</p></div><div id="ncl.CLN5_Disease"><h4>CLN5 Disease</h4><p>
<b>Presenting findings</b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Age of onset: three to 6 years; however, actual age range has not yet been established due to the low number of affected individuals reported to date</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Psychomotor regression with clumsiness followed by progressive visual failure</div></li></ul><p>
<b>Natural history</b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Progressive vision loss, dementia, and seizures develop between ages four and seven years. Life span is between ages 14 and 36 years.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Note: While the natural history is similar to that of CLN2 disease, the onset of CLN5 disease is slightly later and life expectancy is longer [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ncl.REF.ge.2018.837">Ge et al 2018</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ncl.REF.zhang.2025.e70261">Zhang et al 2025</a>].</div></li></ul><p><b>Disease-modifying treatments.</b> None have been approved to date; however, an adeno-associated virus (AAV) vector therapy for CLN5 disease is in development (see BDSRA Foundation, <a href="https://bdsrafoundation.org/research/clinical-studies/" ref="pagearea=body&amp;targetsite=external&amp;targetcat=link&amp;targettype=uri">Clinical Trials &#x00026; Natural History Studies</a>).</p></div><div id="ncl.CLN6_Disease"><h4>CLN6 Disease</h4><p>
<b>Presenting findings</b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Late infantile to juvenile <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a></div><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Age of onset: early childhood through early juvenile period</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Developmental delay progressing to regression</div></li></ul></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Adult-onset phenotypes</div><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Age of onset: early adulthood</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Kufs Type A: progressive myoclonic epilepsy</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Kufs Type B: dementia and a range of movement disorders</div></li></ul></li></ul><p>
<b>Natural history</b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Late infantile <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a>. Manifestations vary but typically progress from developmental delays to regression followed by cognitive decline and ultimately seizures. Myoclonus, sleep disturbance, and vision loss are also observed. Life span typically is late childhood or early teens [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ncl.REF.rus.2022.179">Rus et al 2022</a>]. Note: Presentation of CLN6 disease is variable even for the classic phenotype.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Adult-onset phenotypes (also known as Kufs disease)</div><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Kufs type A. Manifestations in addition to progressive epilepsy include cognitive decline or dementia and motor decline resulting in inability to walk and ataxia [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ncl.REF.berkovic.2019.59">Berkovic et al 2019</a>].</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Kufs type B. Manifestations include epilepsy, myoclonus, ataxia, and progressive cognitive decline, but not vision loss [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ncl.REF.arsov.2011.566">Arsov et al 2011</a>].</div></li></ul></li></ul><p><b>Disease-modifying treatments.</b> None approved to date; however, a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gene-therapy/">gene therapy</a> for CLN6 disease is in development (see BDSRA Foundation, <a href="https://bdsrafoundation.org/research/clinical-studies/" ref="pagearea=body&amp;targetsite=external&amp;targetcat=link&amp;targettype=uri">Clinical Trials &#x00026; Natural History Studies</a>).</p></div><div id="ncl.CLN7_Disease"><h4>CLN7 Disease</h4><p>
<b>Presenting findings</b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Age of onset: two to three years</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>In one study, first concerns were gait changes (ages 2.5-4.5 years); however, often issues with speech were noted at age two years before gait changes. Seizures developed around age 3.5-4.5 years [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ncl.REF.kayani.2024.468">Kayani et al 2024</a>].</div></li></ul><p><b>Natural history.</b> Manifestations are similar to those of CLN2 disease. After onset of seizures, children experience a loss of skills including speech and ambulation. Life expectancy is late childhood to teenage years.</p><p><b>Disease-modifying treatments.</b> None approved to date; however, a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gene-therapy/">gene therapy</a> for CLN7 disease is in development (see BDSRA Foundation, <a href="https://bdsrafoundation.org/research/clinical-studies/" ref="pagearea=body&amp;targetsite=external&amp;targetcat=link&amp;targettype=uri">Clinical Trials &#x00026; Natural History Studies</a>).</p></div><div id="ncl.CLN8_Disease"><h4>CLN8 Disease</h4><p>CLN8 disease was classically defined as two distinct phenotypes; however, recent case reports describe a phenotypic spectrum [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ncl.REF.baltar.2024.1379254">Baltar et al 2024</a>]. The following are the two classic phenotypes.</p><p>
<b>Presenting findings</b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Progressive epilepsy with mental retardation (EPMR), also called Northern epilepsy</div><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Age of onset: five to 10 years</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Epilepsy (generalized, tonic-clonic seizures) followed by cognitive decline</div></li></ul></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Late infantile <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a></div><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Age of onset: two years</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Ataxia, speech delay, vision loss, seizures, and developmental regression. Children have a rapidly progressing course.</div></li></ul></li></ul><p><b>Disease-modifying treatments.</b> None</p></div><div id="ncl.CLN10_Disease"><h4>CLN10 Disease</h4><p>
<b>Presenting findings</b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Congenital <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a></div><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Age of onset: neonatal period (i.e., birth to age 28 days)</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Seizures may occur prenatally; microcephaly and apnea may be present at birth.</div></li></ul></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Other phenotypes</div><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Age of onset: variable, from early childhood to teenage years, with late infantile and juvenile presentations reported</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>May include ataxia, spasticity, speech impairment, and visual disturbance</div></li></ul></li></ul><p>
<b>Natural history</b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Congenital <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a>. Seizures are refractory to anti-seizure medications (ASMs); apnea&#x000a0;/ respiratory insufficiency may occur. Life expectancy is often within a few weeks of birth.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Other phenotypes</div><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Late infantile <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a>. Movement disorders, seizures, and vision loss</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Juvenile <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a>. Loss of motor function, ataxia, speech difficulties, cognitive decline, and vision loss</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Life span is shortened even in children with later-onset CLN10 disease.</div></li></ul></li></ul><p><b>Disease-modifying treatments.</b> None</p></div><div id="ncl.CLN11_Disease"><h4>CLN11 Disease</h4><p>
<b>Presenting findings</b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Age of onset: mid-childhood to second or third decade</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Visual loss, seizures, and ataxia.</div></li></ul><p><b>Natural history.</b> Progressive visual loss with retinal dystrophy and cataracts. Seizures, myoclonus, cerebellar ataxia, and cognitive decline. Both rapidly progressive disease courses as well as slower progression compared to other NCLs can be observed. Visual hallucinations and pyramidal signs (e.g., spasticity, hyperreflexia) have been reported.</p><p><b>Disease-modifying treatments.</b> None</p><p><b>Other.</b> A frontotemporal dementia (FTD)-like <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a> has been seen in heterozygotes (<a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK1371/" ref="pagearea=body&amp;targetsite=external&amp;targetcat=link&amp;targettype=uri"><i>GRN</i> frontotemporal dementia</a>) and an intermediate FTD&#x000a0;/ CLN11 disease spectrum has been considered in individuals with <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/biallelic/">biallelic</a> <i>GRN</i> variants [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ncl.REF.n_brega.2025.101291">N&#x000f3;brega et al 2025</a>].</p></div><div id="ncl.CLN13_Disease"><h4>CLN13 Disease</h4><p>
<b>Presenting findings</b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Age of onset: age 20-30 years</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Cognitive and psychomotor decline</div></li></ul><p><b>Natural history.</b> Seizures in some. Dementia requires dependence on others for care. Life expectancy is shortened.</p><p><b>Disease-modifying treatments</b>. None</p></div><div id="ncl.CLN14_Disease"><h4>CLN14 Disease</h4><p>
<b>Presenting findings</b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Age of onset: early to late infancy (younger than age two years)</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Developmental regression with progressive epilepsy. Visual loss can occur with variable rates of disease progression.</div></li></ul><p><b>Natural history.</b> This rare disorder, previously described as progressive myoclonic epilepsy (PME), is considered by some to be an NCL following identification of several individuals with intracellular accumulation of autofluorescent lipopigment storage material consistent with an NCL (see <a href="/books/NBK1428/table/ncl.T.neuronal_ceroid_lipofuscinoses_ult/?report=objectonly" target="object" rid-ob="figobnclTneuronalceroidlipofuscinosesult">Table 2</a>). PME and CLN14 disease can be distinguished by the earlier onset, more rapid progression, vision loss, and shortened life span seen in CLN14 disease. More data are necessary to elucidate the distinction between PME and CLN14 disease [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ncl.REF.zeineddin.2024.367">Zeineddin et al 2024</a>]. See also <a href="/books/n/gene/kctd7-epilepsy/"><i>KCTD7</i>-Related Progressive Myoclonic Epilepsy</a>.</p><p><b>Disease-modifying treatments.</b> None</p></div></div><div id="ncl.Nomenclature"><h3>Nomenclature</h3><p>Collectively, the NCLs are now commonly referred to as Batten disease. While this name was originally used in reference to CLN3 disease, juvenile onset, use of the term has broadened in recent decades to encompass all subtypes. This single term serves as a concise, practical name for families, advocacy groups, and the scientific community to unify the NCLs. Batten disease should be considered synonymous with neuronal ceroid lipofuscinosis.</p><p>Prior to discovery of the causative genes, NCLs were clinically classified by the age of onset of the first manifestations: i.e., infantile NCL (onset 6-24 months), late infantile NCL (onset 2-4 years), juvenile NCL (onset 4-15 years), and adult NCL (onset 15-50 years) [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ncl.REF.zhang.2025.e70261">Zhang et al 2025</a>]. As NCL-related genes have been identified and understanding of the underlying molecular pathogenesis has increased, <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gene/">gene</a>-related terminology (e.g., CLN3 disease) has largely replaced clinically defined designations, although they are occasionally still in clinical use. Kufs disease, for example, is an eponym for adult-onset NCL.</p></div></div><div id="ncl.Evaluation_Strategies_to_Identify_th"><h2 id="_ncl_Evaluation_Strategies_to_Identify_th_">3.  Evaluation Strategies to Identify the Genetic Cause of a Neuronal Ceroid Lipofuscinosis in a Proband</h2><p>Establishing a specific genetic cause of a neuronal ceroid lipofuscinosis (NCL):</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Usually involves a medical history, physical examination, family history, and <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/genomic/">genomic</a>/genetic testing;</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Can identify individuals with CLN2 disease, for which <a href="#ncl.Targeted_Therapy">targeted therapy</a> is available;</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Can support the affected individual and their family through clarification of natural history and expected changes in function, medical needs, and life span;</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Can aid in <a href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/genetic-counseling/" ref="pagearea=body&amp;targetsite=external&amp;targetcat=link&amp;targettype=uri">genetic counseling</a> (see <a href="#ncl.Genetic_Counseling">Section 5</a>).</div></li></ul><p><b>Family history.</b> A three-generation family history should be taken, with attention to relatives with early dementia, vision loss, epilepsy, and early death and documentation of relevant findings through direct examination or review of medical records, including results of <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/molecular-genetic-testing/">molecular genetic testing</a>. Absence of a known family history does not preclude the diagnosis.</p><div id="ncl.GenomicGenetic_Testing"><h3>Genomic/Genetic Testing</h3><p>Molecular genetic testing approaches can include a combination of <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gene/">gene</a>-targeted testing (<a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/multigene-panel/">multigene panel</a>) and comprehensive <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/genomic/">genomic</a> testing (<a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/chromosomal-microarray/">chromosomal microarray</a> analysis, <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/exome-sequencing/">exome sequencing</a>, <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/genome-sequencing/">genome sequencing</a>). Gene-targeted testing requires the clinician to hypothesize which gene(s) are likely involved, whereas genomic testing can be deployed earlier and for a less specific <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/phenotype/">phenotype</a>.</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div class="half_rhythm"><b>Multigene disease association panels</b> are often first line and can be more accessible than broader whole-<a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/exome/">exome</a> or whole-genome testing; they often include some or all the genes listed in <a href="/books/NBK1428/table/ncl.T.neuronal_ceroid_lipofuscinoses_gen/?report=objectonly" target="object" rid-ob="figobnclTneuronalceroidlipofuscinosesgen">Table 1</a>. (4) Methods used in a panel may include <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/sequence-analysis/">sequence analysis</a>, <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/deletion-duplication-analysis/">deletion/duplication analysis</a>, and/or other non-sequencing-based tests.</div><div class="half_rhythm">For an introduction to multigene panels click <a href="/books/n/gene/app5/#app5.Multigene_Panels">here</a>. <b><a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/genomic/">genomic</a> testing</b> does not require the clinician to determine which <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gene/">gene</a> is likely involved. <b>Exome sequencing</b> is most commonly used; <b><a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/genome-sequencing/">genome sequencing</a></b> is also possible. ACMG recommends <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/exome/">exome</a> and genome sequencing as first- or second-tier diagnostic testing for children with intellectual disability and/or multiple <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/congenital/">congenital</a> anomalies [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ncl.REF.manickam.2021.2029">Manickam et al 2021</a>].</div><div class="half_rhythm">For an introduction to comprehensive <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/genomic/">genomic</a> testing click <a href="/books/n/gene/app5/#app5.Comprehensive_Genomic_Testing">here</a>. </p></div></dd></dl></div></div></div></div><div id="ncl.Supportive_Care"><h4>Supportive Care</h4><p><b>Epilepsy management.</b> Multiple seizure types, including focal, atonic, absence, tonic, and generalized tonic-clonic seizures, have been observed in NCLs.</p><p>Broad-spectrum anti-seizure medications (ASMs) including valproate, benzodiazepines (clobazam/clonazepam), lamotrigine, zonisamide,e and levetiracetam or brivaracetamare are typically preferred; however, no data support the use any one ASM.</p><p>As the NCL progresses, complete freedom from seizures is often unrealistic, but it may be achievable in earlier stages.</p><p>Sodium channel blockers (phenytoin, fosphenytoin, carbamazepine, lamotrigine, oxcarbazepine) should be used with caution as they may aggravate myoclonic and/or generalized seizures. Ketogenic diet and neuromodulation devices may be helpful, similar to responses observed in other individuals with refractory epilepsy.</p><p><b>Movement disorders.</b> Spasticity, dystonia, and myoclonus can cause significant discomfort and pain, particularly in later disease stages. </p></div></dd></dl></div></div></div></div></div><div id="ncl.Genetic_Counseling"><h2 id="_ncl_Genetic_Counseling_">5.  Genetic Counseling</h2><p>
<i>Genetic counseling is the process of providing individuals and families with
information on the nature, mode(s) of inheritance, and implications of genetic disorders to help them
make informed medical and personal decisions. The following section deals with genetic
risk assessment and the use of family history and genetic testing to clarify genetic
status for family members; it is not meant to address all personal, cultural, or
ethical issues that may arise or to substitute for consultation with a genetics
professional</i>. &#x02014;ED.</p><div id="ncl.Mode_of_Inheritance"><h3>Mode of Inheritance</h3><p>Neuronal ceroid lipofuscinoses (NCLs) caused by pathogenic variants in <i>PPT1</i> (CLN1 disease), <i>TPP1</i> (CLN2 disease), <i>CLN3</i> (CLN3 disease), <i>CLN5</i> (CLN5 disease), <i>CLN6</i> (CLN6 disease), <i>MFSD8</i> (CLN7 disease), <i>CLN8</i> (CLN8 disease), <i>CTSD</i> (CLN10 disease), <i>GRN</i> (CLN11 disease), <i>CTSF</i> (CLN13 disease), and <i>KCTD7</i> (CLN14 disease) are inherited in an <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/autosomal-recessive/">autosomal recessive</a> manner.</p><p><i>DNAJC5-</i>related NCL (CLN4 disease) is inherited in an <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/autosomal-dominant/">autosomal dominant</a> manner.</p></div><div id="ncl.Autosomal_Recessive_Inheritance__Ris"><h3>Autosomal Recessive Inheritance &#x02013; Risk to Family Members</h3><p>
<b>Parents of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a></b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>The parents of an affected individual are presumed to be <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a> for the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/autosomal-recessive/">autosomal recessive</a> NCL-related <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a>.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>If a molecular diagnosis has been established in the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a>, <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/molecular-genetic-testing/">molecular genetic testing</a> is recommended for the parents of the proband to confirm that both parents are <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a> for the NCL-related <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> and to allow reliable <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/recurrence-risk/">recurrence risk</a> assessment.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>If a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> is detected in only one parent and parental identity testing has confirmed biological maternity and paternity, it is possible that one of the pathogenic variants identified in the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> occurred as a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/de-novo/"><i>de novo</i></a> event in the proband or as a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/postzygotic/">postzygotic</a> <i>de novo</i> event in a mosaic parent [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ncl.REF.j_nsson.2017.519">J&#x000f3;nsson et al 2017</a>]. If the proband appears to have <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/homozygous/">homozygous</a> pathogenic variants (i.e., the same two pathogenic variants), additional possibilities to consider include:</div><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>A single- or multiexon <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/deletion/">deletion</a> in the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> that was not detected by <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/sequence-analysis/">sequence analysis</a> and that resulted in the artifactual appearance of homozygosity;</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Uniparental isodisomy for the parental <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/chromosome/">chromosome</a> with the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> that resulted in homozygosity for the pathogenic variant in the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a>.</div></li></ul></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Heterozygotes (carriers) are asymptomatic and are not at risk of developing an NCL.</div></li></ul><p>
<b>Sibs of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a></b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>If both parents are known to be <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a> for an <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/autosomal-recessive/">autosomal recessive</a> NCL-related <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a>, each sib of an affected individual has at conception a 25% chance of being affected, a 50% chance of being an asymptomatic <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/carrier/">carrier</a>, and a 25% chance of being unaffected and not a carrier.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Heterozygotes (carriers) are asymptomatic and are not at risk of developing an NCL.</div></li></ul><p><b>Offspring of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a>.</b> Unless an affected individual's reproductive partner also has the NCL or is a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/carrier/">carrier</a>, offspring will be obligate heterozygotes (carriers) for a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> in the NCL-related <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gene/">gene</a>.</p><p><b>Other family members.</b> Each sib of the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a>'s parents is at a 50% risk of being a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/carrier/">carrier</a> of the NCL-related <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a>.</p><p><b>Carrier detection.</b> Carrier testing for at-risk relatives requires prior identification of the NCL-related pathogenic variants in the family.</p></div><div id="ncl.Autosomal_Dominant_Inheritance__Risk"><h3>Autosomal Dominant Inheritance &#x02013; Risk to Family Members</h3><p>
<b>Parents of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a></b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Most individuals diagnosed with CLN4 disease have an affected parent.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Rarely, an individual diagnosed with CLN4 disease has the disorder as the result of a <i>de novo DNAJC5</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a>. <i>De novo</i>
<i>DNAJC5</i> pathogenic variants have been reported; however, the proportion of individuals with CLN4 disease caused by a <i>de novo DNAJC5</i> pathogenic variant is not clear due to limited parental testing data [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ncl.REF.velinov.2012.e29729">Velinov et al 2012</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ncl.REF.naseri.2021.111">Naseri et al 2021</a>, <a class="bk_pop" href="#ncl.REF.huang.2022.829573">Huang et al 2022</a>].</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>If the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> appears to be the only affected family member (i.e., a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/simplex/">simplex</a> case), <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/molecular-genetic-testing/">molecular genetic testing</a> is recommended for the parents of the proband to evaluate their genetic status and inform <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/recurrence-risk/">recurrence risk</a> assessment. Note: A proband may appear to be the only affected family member because of failure to recognize the disorder in family members, reduced <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/penetrance/">penetrance</a>, early death of a parent before the onset of symptoms, or late onset of the disease in an affected parent. Therefore, <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/de-novo/"><i>de novo</i></a> occurrence of a <i>DNAJC5</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> cannot be confirmed unless molecular genetic testing has demonstrated that neither parent is <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a> for the <i>DNAJC5</i> pathogenic variant.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>If the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> identified in the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> is not identified in either parent and parental identity testing has confirmed biological maternity and paternity, the following possibilities should be considered:</div><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>The <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> has a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/de-novo/"><i>de novo</i></a> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a>.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>The <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> inherited a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> from a parent with gonadal (or somatic and gonadal) <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/mosaicism/">mosaicism</a>. Note: Testing of parental leukocyte DNA may not detect all instances of <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/somatic-mosaicism/">somatic mosaicism</a> and will not detect a pathogenic variant that is present in the germ (gonadal) cells only.</div></li></ul></li></ul><p><b>Sibs of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a>.</b> The risk to the sibs of the proband depends on the clinical/genetic status of the proband's parents:</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>If a parent of the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> is affected and/or is known to have the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> identified in the proband, the risk to the sibs of inheriting the pathogenic variant is 50%. Penetrance is high in CLN4 disease. Reduced <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/penetrance/">penetrance</a> has not been widely reported in the literature for <i>DNAJC5</i> pathogenic variants.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>If the <i>DNAJC5</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a> identified in the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> cannot be detected in the leukocyte DNA of either parent, the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/recurrence-risk/">recurrence risk</a> to sibs is estimated to be 1% because of the possibility of parental <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gonadal-mosaicism/">gonadal mosaicism</a> [<a class="bk_pop" href="#ncl.REF.rahbari.2016.126">Rahbari et al 2016</a>].</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>If the parents are clinically unaffected but their genetic status is known, the risk to the sibs of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a> appears to be low. However, sibs of a proband with clinically unaffected parents are still presumed to be at increased risk for CLN4 disease because of the possibility of reduced <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/penetrance/">penetrance</a> in a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/heterozygous/">heterozygous</a> parent and the possibility of parental <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/gonadal-mosaicism/">gonadal mosaicism</a>.</div></li></ul><p><b>Offspring of a <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a>.</b> Each child of an individual with CLN4 disease has a 50% chance of inheriting the <i>DNAJC5</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a>.</p><p><b>Other family members.</b> The risk to other family members depends on the status of the <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/proband/">proband</a>'s parents: if a parent has the <i>DNAJC5</i> <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a>, the parent's family members may be at risk.</p></div><div id="ncl.Related_Genetic_Counseling_Issues"><h3>Related Genetic Counseling Issues</h3><p>
<b>Family planning</b>
</p><ul><li class="half_rhythm"><div>The optimal time for determination of genetic risk and discussion of the availability of prenatal/<a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/preimplantation-genetic-testing/">preimplantation genetic testing</a> is before pregnancy.</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>It is appropriate to offer <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/genetic-counseling/">genetic counseling</a> (including discussion of potential risks to offspring and reproductive options) to young adults who are at risk of having an NCL-related <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a>(s).</div></li><li class="half_rhythm"><div>Carrier testing should be considered for the reproductive partners of individuals known to be carriers of an <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/autosomal-recessive/">autosomal recessive</a> NCL-related <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a>, particularly if both partners are of the same ancestry. Founder variants in <i>CLN3</i>, <i>CLN5</i>, <i>CLN8,</i> and <i>PPT1</i> have been identified in the <a href="/books/n/gene/founder_finnish/">Finnish population</a>; in <i>CLN6</i> in the <a href="/books/n/gene/founder_costa_rican/">Costa Rican</a> population; in <i>CLN6</i> and <i>TPP1</i> in the <a href="/books/n/gene/founder_newfl/">Newfoundland and/or Labrador</a> population; and in <i>MFSD8</i> in the <a href="/books/n/gene/founder_romani/">Romani</a> population.</div></li></ul></div><div id="ncl.Prenatal_Testing_and_Preimplantation"><h3>Prenatal Testing and Preimplantation Genetic Testing</h3><p>Once the NCL-related <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/pathogenic-variant/">pathogenic variant</a>(s) have been identified in an affected family member, prenatal and <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/preimplantation-genetic-testing/">preimplantation genetic testing</a> are possible.</p><p>Differences in perspective may exist among medical professionals and within families regarding the use of prenatal and <a class="def" href="/books/n/gene/glossary/def-item/preimplantation-genetic-testing/">preimplantation genetic testing</a>. While most health care professionals would consider use of prenatal and preimplantation genetic testing to be a personal decision, discussion of these issues may be helpful.</p></div></div><div id="ncl.Resources"><h2 id="_ncl_Resources_">Resources</h2><p>
