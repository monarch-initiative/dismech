Celiac disease is one of the most common life-long disorders worldwide, with a 
prevalence mostly ranging between 0.7% and 2.9% in the general population and a 
higher frequency in females and well-defined at-risk groups, such as relatives 
of affected individuals and patients with autoimmune comorbidities. Increasing 
clinical detection is facilitated by improving awareness, implementation of a 
case-finding approach, and serology availability for screening at-risk patients, 
among other factors. Nevertheless, due to huge clinical variability, many celiac 
disease cases still escape diagnosis in most countries, unless actively searched 
by proactive policies. The burden of celiac disease is increasing, as is the 
need for better longitudinal care. Pediatric screening of the general population 
could represent the road ahead for an efficient intervention of secondary 
prevention aimed to reduce the social and health burden of celiac disease. This 
review analyses the epidemiology of celiac disease continent by continent, 
discusses current strategies to improve the detection of celiac disease, and 
highlights challenges related to the burden of celiac disease globally.
