Schizophrenia is a heritable brain illness with unknown pathogenic mechanisms.
Schizophrenia's strongest genetic association at a population level involves
variation in the major histocompatibility complex (MHC) locus, but the genes and
molecular mechanisms accounting for this have been challenging to identify. Here
we show that this association arises in part from many structurally diverse
alleles of the complement component 4 (C4) genes. We found that these alleles
generated widely varying levels of C4A and C4B expression in the brain, with
each common C4 allele associating with schizophrenia in proportion to its
tendency to generate greater expression of C4A. Human C4 protein localized to
neuronal synapses, dendrites, axons, and cell bodies. In mice, C4 mediated
synapse elimination during postnatal development. These results implicate
excessive complement activity in the development of schizophrenia and may help
explain the reduced numbers of synapses in the brains of individuals with
schizophrenia.
