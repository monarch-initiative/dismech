RNA polymerases (RNAPs) accomplish the first step of gene expression in all 
living organisms. However, the sequence divergence between bacterial and human 
RNAPs makes the bacterial RNAP a promising target for antibiotic development. 
The most clinically important and extensively studied class of antibiotics known 
to inhibit bacterial RNAP are the rifamycins. For example, rifamycins are a 
vital element of the current combination therapy for treatment of tuberculosis. 
Here, we provide an overview of the history of the discovery of rifamycins, 
their mechanisms of action, the mechanisms of bacterial resistance against them, 
and progress in their further development.
