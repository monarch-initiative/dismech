| Precise causal step from global SMN deficiency to selective alpha motor neuron vulnerability | Core selectivity problem: SMN is ubiquitous, but motor neurons degenerate disproportionately | Reviews/syntheses support SMN roles in snRNP assembly, translation, axonal/NMJ biology, but direct human causal linkage from each SMN-dependent defect to selective motor neuron death remains incomplete (pqac-00000001, pqac-00000005, pqac-00000007) | Human iPSC tri-culture system (motor neurons + astrocytes + myotubes) from SMA patients stratified by SMN2 copy number; CRISPR SMN rescue in one compartment at a time; single-cell RNA-seq, splicing, ribosome profiling, axonal transport imaging, survival/NMJ endpoints | Canonical model: neuron-intrinsic SMN rescue should largely normalize survival and major transcriptomic/splicing defects; alternative non-cell-autonomous model: glial or muscle rescue is required for full protection |
| Whether motor neuron degeneration is primary driver or whether NMJ and muscle pathology are equally early causal lesions | Determines whether seed model is sufficient or needs explicit NMJ/muscle branches | Current evidence shows early NMJ defects, muscle-intrinsic abnormalities, and multisystem involvement, but treated cohorts still largely interpret benefit through motor-neuron rescue (pqac-00000000, pqac-00000004, pqac-00000007) | Longitudinal presymptomatic infant cohort detected by newborn screening; serial motor unit number estimation, CMAP, muscle MRI, plasma/CSF biomarkers, and if feasible muscle transcriptomics before and after therapy | Canonical model: neuronal/electrophysiologic denervation markers should precede major muscle-intrinsic signatures; alternative parallel-pathology model: muscle/NMJ abnormalities emerge concurrently or before denervation |
| Extent to which residual disability after SMN restoration reflects irreversible developmental loss vs ongoing SMN-independent pathology | Critical for interpreting why approved therapies validate but do not fully explain all treated phenotypes | Human muscle study found restored SMN mRNA/protein with persistent mitochondrial and stress abnormalities; real-world gene-therapy cohorts still show scoliosis/bracing, ventilation, feeding needs in subsets (pqac-00000000, pqac-00000004, pqac-00000008, pqac-00000013) | Early-vs-late treatment matched cohort with deep phenotyping; compare presymptomatic, mildly symptomatic, and late-treated patients using motor scales, scoliosis progression, mitochondrial assays in blood/muscle, and omics | Canonical developmental-window model: earliest-treated patients show near-complete normalization, whereas late-treated patients retain fixed deficits; alternative ongoing-parallel-pathology model: persistent mitochondrial/stress signatures remain even in earliest-treated patients |
| Therapeutic timing window: how early SMN restoration must occur to prevent downstream pathology | Limits generalizability of the canonical mechanism across stages and subtypes | Registry data show better outcomes with newborn screening and earlier treatment; reviews emphasize fetal/developmental onset in severe SMA (pqac-00000008, pqac-00000009, pqac-00000003) | Registry-based causal inference study using newborn-screened infants treated at <2 weeks, 2–6 weeks, >6 weeks; endpoints: independent sitting/walking, event-free survival, electrophysiology, scoliosis, bulbar outcomes | Canonical timing-sensitive model: monotonic benefit gradient with earliest treatment giving strongest normalization; alternative stage-independent model: smaller timing effect after adjusting for baseline severity |
| How much non-neuronal tissues contribute after CNS-targeted or partial-peripheral rescue | Determines whether SMA should be modeled as motor neuron disease only or systemic SMN-deficiency disorder | Nusinersen-treated muscle can show SMN restoration yet persistent deficits; reviews emphasize systemic disease; risdiplam and onasemnogene have broader distribution but residual pathology persists (pqac-00000000, pqac-00000001, pqac-00000004) | Comparative cohort of nusinersen vs risdiplam vs onasemnogene started at similar stage; endpoints: CNS motor scales, peripheral biomarkers, muscle mitochondrial readouts, liver/metabolic markers, fatigue/quality of life | Canonical CNS-dominant model: similar long-term outcomes once spinal motor neurons are rescued; systemic model: broader-distribution therapies show superior peripheral/metabolic/fatigue outcomes despite similar CNS motor scores |
| Source of heterogeneity in response beyond SMN2 copy number | Important for subtype restrictions and for avoiding over-strong canonical claims | SMN2 copy number is established as major modifier, but adult transcriptomic data and real-world cohorts show variable response and incomplete biomarker standardization (pqac-00000002, pqac-00000022, pqac-00000023) | Multi-center genotype-phenotype-response study integrating SMN2 copies, SMN2/SMN1 expression ratio, modifier variants, treatment timing, baseline denervation; endpoint: motor trajectory and bulbar/respiratory response | Canonical model: SMN2 copy number and baseline SMN-related measures explain most variance; alternative modifier-rich model: substantial residual variance explained by inflammatory, muscle, or glial biomarkers/modifier genes |
| Whether biomarker changes truly report causal rescue vs downstream correlation | Needed to distinguish mechanism-proximal from epiphenomenal biomarkers | NFL, tau, GFAP, SMN2/SMN1 ratio, myostatin, CMAP/MUNE, miRNAs show associations, but many are not yet validated as causal or stage-general markers (pqac-00000003, pqac-00000019, pqac-00000022, pqac-00000023) | Prospective biomarker qualification cohort across infant, pediatric, and adult SMA; repeated sampling pre/post therapy; mediation analysis linking biomarker change to later motor benefit | Canonical model: proximal SMN-linked biomarkers should change early and mediate later functional benefit; alternative model: many biomarkers track disease burden only and fail mediation/forecasting |
| Relative contribution of inflammatory/glial pathways to ongoing degeneration | Could indicate a competing or complementary non-cell-autonomous mechanism | Adult nusinersen transcriptomics implicate miR-146a, NF-kB, Toll-like receptor and NOTCH pathways; reviews highlight glial activation, but direct perturbation evidence in human disease is sparse (pqac-00000022, pqac-00000023, pqac-00000007) | Patient-derived astrocyte–motor neuron co-culture with selective SMN rescue vs anti-inflammatory/miR-146a inhibition; endpoints: motor neuron survival, synaptic activity, target-gene normalization | Canonical model: SMN restoration dominates rescue and anti-inflammatory manipulation adds little; alternative glial/inflammatory model: blocking astrocyte/miR-146a pathways improves survival even with partial SMN rescue |
| Whether persistent muscle mitochondrial deficits are downstream scars or active therapeutic targets | Directly affects whether combination therapy should be added to KB as parallel mechanism | Treated type II muscle retained reduced mitochondrial copy number/oxidative phosphorylation signatures despite SMN restoration (pqac-00000004) | Randomized add-on study in treated later-onset SMA using mitochondrial-targeted intervention; endpoints: fatigue, endurance, muscle transcriptomics, mitochondrial DNA copy number, motor scales | Canonical scar model: add-on has limited benefit once SMN is restored; alternative active-muscle-pathology model: mitochondrial-targeted add-on improves endurance/fatigue and muscle molecular signatures |
| Lack of direct head-to-head mechanistic comparison across approved SMN-restoring therapies | Important because all three validate the axis, but tissue distribution and response patterns differ | Current evidence includes separate real-world cohorts for nusinersen, onasemnogene, and risdiplam, but cross-therapy mechanistic comparisons are limited and confounded (pqac-00000008, pqac-00000018, pqac-00000024) | Pragmatic harmonized registry with common endpoints and biospecimens across therapy classes, stratified by age, SMN2 copies, and prior treatment | Canonical model: all therapies converge on similar mechanism-proximal biomarker and functional rescue when exposure is adequate; alternative distribution-specific model: each therapy leaves distinct residual pathology signatures |
| Absence of strong direct human evidence separating fetal developmental arrest from postnatal neurodegeneration in severe SMA | Affects whether seed hypothesis should explicitly include developmental pathology, not only degeneration | Reviews and early-treatment data suggest pathology begins prenatally and severe SMA may be both developmental and degenerative, but direct longitudinal human fetal-to-neonatal data are limited (pqac-00000003, pqac-00000008, pqac-00000009) | Natural-history-plus-screening study using prenatal/fetal imaging where available, neonatal electrophysiology, and immediate postnatal treatment outcomes in genetically diagnosed fetuses/newborns | Canonical degeneration-focused model: postnatal rescue should largely prevent pathology if given quickly; developmental-arrest model: measurable deficits are already present at birth despite immediate postnatal treatment |
