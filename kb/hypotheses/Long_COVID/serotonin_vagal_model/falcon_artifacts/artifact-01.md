| Hypothesis edge/step | What is known (with brief evidence) | What is missing/uncertain | Why it matters | Proposed discriminating experiment or dataset |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Peripheral serotonin is truly reduced in PASC | Wong et al. reported serotonin as the metabolite most associated with PASC across several cohorts, with reduced tryptophan and links to platelet biology; however one cohort (UNCOVR) did not reproduce serotonin reduction (pqac-00000045, pqac-00000046) | Whether published human plasma 5-HT values are analytically valid; whether differences reflect true serotonin depletion vs sample-prep artifact or platelet loss | This is the foundation of the whole model; if the human serotonin signal is not robust, the downstream vagus/hippocampus chain weakens substantially | Independent multi-center replication using standardized serotonin assays in whole blood, platelet-rich plasma, platelet-poor plasma, and serum, with contemporaneous platelet counts and per-platelet normalization; pre-register centrifugation/processing SOPs and include blinded assay cross-validation (pqac-00000043, pqac-00000044) |
| Platelet normalization and storage defects explain “low serotonin” | The seed paper proposes thrombocytopenia and platelet hyperactivation reduce systemic serotonin storage/carrying capacity; animal work supports platelet involvement (pqac-00000001, pqac-00000047, pqac-00000048) | No definitive human PASC study has shown whether low circulating 5-HT remains after normalization to platelet number, platelet volume, or platelet serotonin content | Distinguishes true serotonergic depletion from hematologic confounding and clarifies whether platelet dysfunction is upstream, parallel, or merely a measurement issue | Human longitudinal cohort measuring platelet count, mean platelet volume, platelet activation markers, platelet serotonin content, plasma serotonin, and symptom trajectories before and after exertion or inflammatory flares |
| Type I IFN causally drives serotonin depletion in humans | In mice/organoids, TLR3/IFNAR/STAT1 signaling and viral inflammation reduce serotonin via tryptophan uptake, platelet changes, and MAO-mediated turnover; human PASC data are consistent but mainly associative (pqac-00000000, pqac-00000002, pqac-00000066, pqac-00000071) | No direct human perturbation or longitudinal mediation study shows IFN elevation precedes and causes serotonin decline in PASC | Establishes whether IFN is the actionable upstream driver rather than a correlate of viral persistence or generalized inflammation | Serial human sampling from acute infection through 12 months with plasma IFN-α/β, IFN-stimulated gene signatures, serotonin, tryptophan, platelet metrics, and mediation analysis; ideally nested in cohorts receiving antiviral or IFN-modulating treatment |
| Reduced intestinal tryptophan uptake is a main source of serotonin loss | Mouse/organoid work shows reduced Ace2/Slc6a19 and impaired tryptophan absorption after viral/TLR3 signaling; PASC patients also show lower tryptophan (pqac-00000011, pqac-00000019, pqac-00000033, pqac-00000066) | In humans with PASC, there is no direct demonstration that impaired intestinal transporter activity meaningfully lowers systemic serotonin over time | Needed to know whether gut epithelial dysfunction is a key intervention point vs secondary metabolic noise | Endoscopy/biopsy subcohort with duodenal ACE2/SLC6A19 expression, tracer-based oral tryptophan absorption testing, stool virome/microbiome, plasma tryptophan-serotonin panels, and matched symptom phenotyping |
| MAO-mediated turnover contributes materially in human PASC | In animal models, ileal Maoa and urinary 5-HIAA increase during viral inflammation and MAO inhibition rescues platelet serotonin (pqac-00000001, pqac-00000011, pqac-00000048) | No clear human PASC evidence quantifies MAO activity, tissue source, or whether elevated 5-HIAA is present and symptom-linked | Determines whether turnover, rather than synthesis/storage, is the dominant druggable mechanism | Human urine/plasma 5-HIAA plus platelet/intestinal MAO-A measures, PET MAO imaging where feasible, and ex vivo platelet serotonin uptake/turnover assays across PASC subtypes |
| Low peripheral serotonin directly impairs human vagal signaling | Mouse work shows reduced NTS activation, serotonin-responsive vagal neurons, and rescue with capsaicin, 5-HT3 agonism, or chemogenetic vagal activation (pqac-00000000, pqac-00000024, pqac-00000025, pqac-00000047) | No direct human evidence links lower serotonin to reduced vagal afferent signaling in PASC; current human vagal data are mostly indirect via HRV or stimulation trials | This is the key bridge from peripheral biochemistry to neurocognitive/autonomic symptoms | Combine autonomic testing (HRV, baroreflex sensitivity, tilt, respiratory sinus arrhythmia), plasma/platelet serotonin, and pharmacologic challenge (e.g., acute 5-HTP or peripheral 5-HT3 agonist where safe) with symptom and physiologic readouts |
| Vagal impairment causes altered hippocampal responses in PASC | In mice, reduced peripheral serotonin blunts NTS and dentate gyrus cFos and impairs novel object recognition; rescue restores hippocampal activation and behavior (pqac-00000011, pqac-00000015, pqac-00000024) | No human PASC study has directly shown that individuals with low serotonin/low vagal tone have abnormal hippocampal activation specifically attributable to this pathway | Necessary to validate the “vagus-to-hippocampus” limb rather than assuming animal circuitry translates directly | fMRI task studies focused on hippocampal novelty/memory encoding in PASC, stratified by serotonin and autonomic status, with concurrent inflammatory markers and follow-up after vagal or serotonergic intervention |
| Hippocampal dysfunction explains brain fog/memory symptoms better than alternatives | The model is strongest for memory-related cognitive symptoms; animal novelty-recognition deficits align with hippocampal involvement (pqac-00000000, pqac-00000047) | Human cognitive phenotyping in Long COVID is heterogeneous; it is unclear whether hippocampal-type deficits, executive dysfunction, fatigue-related slowing, or mood symptoms dominate in serotonin-low patients | Helps define the symptom domain and prevent overextension of the model to all neurocognitive complaints | Deep neuropsychological batteries separating episodic memory, executive function, attention, processing speed, and depression/anxiety, linked to serotonin/vagal/IFN biomarkers |
| The model applies to specific Long COVID subtypes rather than all PASC | Wong et al. used an n=1,540 PASC cohort with symptom clustering and noted serotonin reduction was not confined to a single subset, but heterogeneity across cohorts was substantial (pqac-00000018, pqac-00000022, pqac-00000045) | No validated subtype restriction identifies which patients are best explained by the serotonin-vagus model (e.g., brain fog, dysautonomia, pain, GI) | Mechanistic enrichment is essential for trial design and KB curation; a true subtype model may be obscured in pooled cohorts | Clustered, multi-omic PASC cohorts with predefined endotypes (neurocognitive, dysautonomic, GI, pain, ME/CFS-like) and interaction analyses for serotonin-pathway biomarkers |
| Microbiome/tryptophan changes are upstream of serotonin rather than parallel mechanisms | Human 2024 data link microbial tryptophan metabolism and indole deficits to inflammatory mediators in acute COVID and long COVID; microbial indoles modulate STING/TLR3/TLR4 responses in vitro (pqac-00000065, pqac-00000067, pqac-00000070) | It is unresolved whether microbiome-mediated tryptophan dysregulation primarily acts through serotonin depletion, independent immune signaling, or both | Important because microbiome-targeted therapies may help even if serotonin is not the critical mediator | Integrated stool metagenomics, microbial tryptophan pathway capacity, host tryptophan/serotonin panels, cytokines, and causal modeling; intervention arms with synbiotics vs serotonergic agents to test mediation |
| SSRI benefits, if real, test the hypothesis rather than confound it | Small RCT and large observational data suggest some protection against post-COVID fatigue/Long COVID with SSRIs, but mechanisms are uncertain; SSRIs can also alter platelet serotonin uptake (pqac-00000049, pqac-00000058, pqac-00000059) | Whether SSRI effects operate through peripheral serotonin, sigma-1 receptor signaling, mood effects, anti-inflammatory effects, or platelet modulation is unresolved | Treatment response cannot be used as strong mechanistic support without understanding what SSRIs are actually changing | Biomarker-embedded randomized trials of SSRI vs non-SSRI comparator with serial platelet serotonin, plasma serotonin, IFN signatures, autonomic metrics, cognition, and depression severity |
| SSRI exposure may itself distort platelet-based serotonin measurements | Commentary notes SSRIs are expected to lower platelet serotonin via transporter inhibition and therefore could confound circulating serotonin interpretation (pqac-00000043, pqac-00000044) | Many studies do not clearly stratify or exclude SSRI/SRI users when interpreting serotonin assays | Without this, both biomarker studies and intervention studies may misclassify the biology | Reanalyze existing cohorts by SSRI/SRI exposure; in new studies, prespecify exclusion or stratification and measure platelet serotonin content directly |
| Viral persistence biomarkers are upstream drivers of the pathway | Gut/tissue viral persistence is documented in some PASC studies and Wong et al. position persistent viral material as a trigger for sustained IFN and serotonin changes (pqac-00000003, pqac-00000011, pqac-00000045) | No study yet demonstrates a full human chain from viral persistence biomarker → IFN activation → serotonin depletion → vagal/hippocampal dysfunction → symptoms | This missing source-to-target chain is central to deciding whether antivirals, immune modulation, or neuromodulation should be prioritized | Tissue-informed cohort with stool/tissue viral RNA or antigen, circulating spike/RNA, IFN signatures, serotonin measures, autonomic tests, hippocampal imaging, and longitudinal symptom outcomes before/after antiviral therapy |
| Competing models may explain the same symptoms more parsimoniously | Microclot/coagulation, endothelial/fibrin inflammation, autoimmunity, and dysautonomia models also explain fatigue, pain, and brain fog; some have direct human evidence independent of serotonin (pqac-00000037, pqac-00000039) | No head-to-head study shows serotonin-pathway biomarkers outperform fibrin/endothelial/autoimmune markers for predicting cognition or autonomic symptoms | Required to know whether this hypothesis is primary, secondary, or one of several parallel mechanisms | Multi-arm mechanistic cohort measuring serotonin/tryptophan/IFN/vagal metrics alongside fibrinogen, D-dimer, endothelial markers, autoantibodies, and viral persistence markers, with model-comparison analyses for symptom prediction |
