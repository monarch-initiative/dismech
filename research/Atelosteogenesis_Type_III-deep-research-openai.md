# Atelosteogenesis Type III: Mechanistic Pathophysiology and Clinical Overview

## Definition and Background  
**Atelosteogenesis Type III (AO3)** is a rare and severe skeletal dysplasia characterized by profound abnormalities in bone development. Fewer than 25 cases have been reported worldwide ([medlineplus.gov](https://medlineplus.gov/genetics/condition/atelosteogenesis-type-3#:~:text=About%20two%20dozen%20affected%20individuals,have%20been%20identified)), reflecting an extremely low prevalence (on the order of <1 in 1,000,000 ([www.orpha.net](https://www.orpha.net/en/disease/detail/56305#:~:text=Epidemiology))). Clinically, AO3 falls on the severe end of the **filamin B (FLNB)**–related skeletal disorder spectrum, which also includes Larsen syndrome (milder) and Atelosteogenesis Type I (more severe, typically perinatal lethal) ([www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK2534/#:~:text=syndrome%20%28FLNB,the%20previously%20described%20entities%20Piepkorn)) ([www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/medgen/777149#:~:text=From%20OMIM%20Atelosteogenesis%20type%20III,Infants%20with%20AO3%20often%20have)). Affected infants present with **disproportionate short-limb dwarfism, multiple joint dislocations, and distinctive craniofacial anomalies** ([www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/medgen/777149#:~:text=From%20OMIM%20Atelosteogenesis%20type%20III,Infants%20with%20AO3%20often%20have)). Most cases are diagnosed at birth or prenatally by ultrasound due to the striking skeletal abnormalities, and **neonatal mortality is high** because of chest wall hypoplasia and airway malformations leading to respiratory failure ([medlineplus.gov](https://medlineplus.gov/genetics/condition/atelosteogenesis-type-3#:~:text=Individuals%20with%20atelosteogenesis%20type%203,are%20probably%20caused%20by%20low)). Rarely, with intensive medical support, infants have survived beyond the newborn period ([medlineplus.gov](https://medlineplus.gov/genetics/condition/atelosteogenesis-type-3#:~:text=Individuals%20with%20atelosteogenesis%20type%203,are%20probably%20caused%20by%20low)). 

## Genetic Cause and Inheritance  
AO3 is **caused by mutations in the FLNB gene**, which encodes the cytoskeletal protein **filamin B** ([medlineplus.gov](https://medlineplus.gov/genetics/condition/atelosteogenesis-type-3#:~:text=Mutations%20in%20the%20FLNB%20,links%20actin%20to%20many%20other)). Filamin B is an actin-binding protein that organizes the intracellular cytoskeleton and links it to the cell membrane, serving as a scaffold for signaling pathways ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/14991055/#:~:text=The%20filamins%20are%20cytoplasmic%20proteins,and%20the%20perinatal%20lethal)). It is highly expressed in developing cartilage (growth plate chondrocytes) and bone structures during embryogenesis ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/14991055/#:~:text=dominant%20Larsen%20syndrome%20,this%20ubiquitously%20expressed%20cytoskeletal%20protein)). **Heterozygous** missense mutations or small in-frame deletions in *FLNB* underlie AO3 ([www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK2534/#:~:text=match%20at%20L632%20%2A%20AO3,Rock%20et%20al%202006)). These mutations produce an abnormal full-length filamin B protein that has a **gain-of-function (neomorphic)** effect, rather than a simple loss of function ([www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/gtr/conditions/C3668942/#:~:text=by%20loss%20of%20function%20or,For%20the%20purposes)) ([medlineplus.gov](https://medlineplus.gov/genetics/condition/atelosteogenesis-type-3#:~:text=FLNB%20gene%20mutations%20that%20cause,symptoms%20of%20atelosteogenesis%20type%203)). Mechanistically, the mutant filamin B interferes with normal chondrocyte proliferation and differentiation, disrupting endochondral ossification (the process by which cartilage is replaced by bone) ([medlineplus.gov](https://medlineplus.gov/genetics/condition/atelosteogenesis-type-3#:~:text=FLNB%20gene%20mutations%20that%20cause,symptoms%20of%20atelosteogenesis%20type%203)). This dominant-negative effect on cartilage development leads to **impaired skeletal segmentation, joint formation, and bone mineralization** ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/14991055/#:~:text=found%20that%20filamin%20B%20is,this%20ubiquitously%20expressed%20cytoskeletal%20protein)). In a landmark 2004 study, researchers identified *FLNB* mutations in AO3 and related disorders, establishing that filamin B is crucial for vertebral segmentation and limb skeletogenesis ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/14991055/#:~:text=dominant%20Larsen%20syndrome%20,this%20ubiquitously%20expressed%20cytoskeletal%20protein)). Recent experimental research (2024) in a mouse model further showed that an *FLNB* mutation can alter the number of ossification centers, implicating disrupted embryonic patterning (e.g. **HOX gene**–regulated segmentation) in AO3 pathogenesis ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10924170/#:~:text=Filamin%20B%20,A)).  

**Inheritance Pattern:** Atelosteogenesis III is inherited in an **autosomal dominant** manner ([medlineplus.gov](https://medlineplus.gov/genetics/condition/atelosteogenesis-type-3#:~:text=Inheritance)). In virtually all reported cases, the pathogenic *FLNB* variant arose **de novo** (a new mutation in the affected child), since individuals with AO3 rarely survive to reproduce ([medlineplus.gov](https://medlineplus.gov/genetics/condition/atelosteogenesis-type-3#:~:text=Inheritance)). Parents of affected infants typically have no family history of the disorder. However, there are rare instances of **germline mosaicism** – a mildly affected parent (often with a phenotype resembling a milder FLNB-related condition like Larsen syndrome) can carry the mutation in some cells and transmit a fully penetrant mutation to their child ([www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK2534/#:~:text=match%20at%20L570%20Infants%20with,pathogenic%20variant%20present%20a%20severe)) ([www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK2534/#:~:text=Infants%20with%20FLNB,pathogenic%20variant%20present%20a%20severe)). Such cases demonstrate the variable expressivity of FLNB mutations within families. Genetic testing confirms *FLNB* mutations in AO3 patients, which is important for definitive diagnosis and prenatal counseling ([www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK2534/#:~:text=match%20at%20L91%20FLNB,Some%20individuals%20diagnosed%20with)) ([www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK2534/#:~:text=match%20at%20L624%20FLNB,B%20protein)). 

## Pathophysiology and Mechanistic Insights  
At the cellular level, mutant filamin B disrupts the architecture and signaling of chondrocytes (cartilage cells). Normally, filamin B cross-links actin filaments into networks and connects them to membrane proteins, helping chondrocytes maintain shape and respond to mechanical signals ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/14991055/#:~:text=The%20filamins%20are%20cytoplasmic%20proteins,and%20the%20perinatal%20lethal)). It also acts as a scaffold for proteins that regulate chondrocyte maturation and division ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/14991055/#:~:text=The%20filamins%20are%20cytoplasmic%20proteins,and%20the%20perinatal%20lethal)). In AO3, the **gain-of-function FLNB mutation** produces an aberrant filamin B protein that is still present (not truncated) but functionally misbehaves ([medlineplus.gov](https://medlineplus.gov/genetics/condition/atelosteogenesis-type-3#:~:text=FLNB%20gene%20mutations%20that%20cause,symptoms%20of%20atelosteogenesis%20type%203)). This abnormal protein can oligomerize and incorporate into the cytoskeleton, where it likely sequesters or mislocalizes binding partners, ultimately **interfering with normal cartilage development** ([medlineplus.gov](https://medlineplus.gov/genetics/condition/atelosteogenesis-type-3#:~:text=FLNB%20gene%20mutations%20that%20cause,symptoms%20of%20atelosteogenesis%20type%203)). Chondrocytes with mutant filamin B show reduced proliferation and abnormal differentiation, which means the growth plates cannot properly form bone tissue ([medlineplus.gov](https://medlineplus.gov/genetics/condition/atelosteogenesis-type-3#:~:text=FLNB%20gene%20mutations%20that%20cause,symptoms%20of%20atelosteogenesis%20type%203)). As a result, endochondral ossification is profoundly impaired – many skeletal elements remain partially cartilaginous or undersized in the fetus ([www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/medgen/777149#:~:text=2004%29,and%20respiratory%20complications%20and%20cervical)). The developmental timing of ossification is also disrupted (“disharmonious skeletal maturation”), so some bones ossify late or irregularly ([www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/medgen/777149#:~:text=From%20OMIM%20Atelosteogenesis%20type%20III,Infants%20with%20AO3%20often%20have)). Moreover, filamin B’s role in joint and spine development is deranged: the formation of normal joint capsules and segmented vertebrae requires filamin-mediated signaling, so mutations lead to **joint malformations (e.g. failed separations, causing dislocations) and vertebral fusions** ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/14991055/#:~:text=found%20that%20filamin%20B%20is,this%20ubiquitously%20expressed%20cytoskeletal%20protein)). In summary, the *FLNB* mutation creates a dominant pathogenic protein that acts in a **dominant-negative fashion** within cartilage and bone cells, causing multi-system skeletal malformations characteristic of AO3 ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/14991055/#:~:text=dominant%20Larsen%20syndrome%20,this%20ubiquitously%20expressed%20cytoskeletal%20protein)). No other major genes are known to cause AO3 — it is specifically a filamin B–related disorder, distinct from Atelosteogenesis type II (which is caused by a different gene and inherited recessively) ([www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/medgen/777149#:~:text=2004%29,org%2Fentry%2F108721)) . Ongoing research continues to probe how mutant filamin B perturbs developmental pathways; for instance, recent studies suggest it may affect genetic regulators of skeletal patterning (such as HOX genes), further explaining the **abnormal skeletal segmentation** observed in these patients ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10924170/#:~:text=Filamin%20B%20,A)). 

## Hallmark Skeletal Phenotypes  
AO3 has distinctive and severe skeletal abnormalities apparent at birth. Key **hallmark phenotypes** include: 

- **Severely shortened limbs:** Affected infants have **micromelic dwarfism**, with extremely short arms and legs. Many limb bones are **underdeveloped or even absent** in severe cases, resulting in shortened upper and lower extremities ([medlineplus.gov](https://medlineplus.gov/genetics/condition/atelosteogenesis-type-3#:~:text=turning%20feet%20,About)). The long bones that are present often appear **poorly modeled (dysplastic)** on X-rays, with flared metaphyses and tapered, thin diaphyses ([www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/medgen/777149#:~:text=From%20OMIM%20Atelosteogenesis%20type%20III,Infants%20with%20AO3%20often%20have)).  

- **Joint dislocations:** There are multiple **congenital joint dislocations**, notably of the **large joints**. The **hips, knees, and elbows** are frequently dislocated or subluxed at birth ([medlineplus.gov](https://medlineplus.gov/genetics/condition/atelosteogenesis-type-3#:~:text=Atelosteogenesis%20type%203%20is%20a,be%20underdeveloped%20or%20in%20some)). This reflects the abnormal joint formation due to the FLNB mutation disrupting normal connective tissue and joint capsule development ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/14991055/#:~:text=found%20that%20filamin%20B%20is,this%20ubiquitously%20expressed%20cytoskeletal%20protein)). Recurrent or fixed dislocations are a defining clinical feature, often requiring orthopedic management if the child survives ([www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK2534/#:~:text=FLNB,cleft%20palate%20and%20hearing%20loss)).  

- **Clubfeet (foot deformities):** Virtually all reported cases have **bilateral clubfoot**, where the feet are fixed in an inward- and upward-turning position (talipes equinovarus or similar deformity) ([medlineplus.gov](https://medlineplus.gov/genetics/condition/atelosteogenesis-type-3#:~:text=Atelosteogenesis%20type%203%20is%20a,be%20underdeveloped%20or%20in%20some)). This foot malposition is a common finding in filamin B disorders and is present at birth as part of the AO3 phenotype ([www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK2534/#:~:text=FLNB,cleft%20palate%20and%20hearing%20loss)). 

- **Hand and finger anomalies:** The **hands and feet are unusually broad** with short, thick fingers and toes ([medlineplus.gov](https://medlineplus.gov/genetics/condition/atelosteogenesis-type-3#:~:text=cases%20absent,include%20a%20%203%20broad)). Often the digits exhibit permanent flexion contractures (**camptodactyly**), and some may be partially fused (**syndactyly**) ([medlineplus.gov](https://medlineplus.gov/genetics/condition/atelosteogenesis-type-3#:~:text=cases%20absent,About)). In severe cases (overlapping with AOI), there can be **polysyndactyly** or “flipper-like” appendages where digits are completely fused, although AO3 typically has distinct but broad digits ([medlineplus.gov](https://medlineplus.gov/genetics/condition/atelosteogenesis-type-3#:~:text=cases%20absent,About)) ([www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK2534/#:~:text=FLNB,cleft%20palate%20and%20hearing%20loss)). These digital anomalies further reflect aberrant ossification in the hands and feet. 

- **Spine and chest deformities:** AO3 involves significant **axial skeletal malformations**. Many patients have **vertebral anomalies** – the spine may show segmentation defects (fused vertebrae or hemivertebrae) and **abnormal curvature** (scoliosis or lordosis) ([www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/medgen/777149#:~:text=From%20OMIM%20Atelosteogenesis%20type%20III,Infants%20with%20AO3%20often%20have)). The **rib cage is hypoplastic** (underdeveloped) and narrow ([medlineplus.gov](https://medlineplus.gov/genetics/condition/atelosteogenesis-type-3#:~:text=Individuals%20with%20atelosteogenesis%20type%203,individuals%20survive%20longer%2C%20usually%20with)), due to shortened ribs and abnormal costal cartilage development. This small thoracic cage severely compromises lung development and expansion, leading to respiratory insufficiency at birth ([medlineplus.gov](https://medlineplus.gov/genetics/condition/atelosteogenesis-type-3#:~:text=Individuals%20with%20atelosteogenesis%20type%203,are%20probably%20caused%20by%20low)). The pelvis is also undersized and malformed, and some pelvic bones can be incompletely ossified or fused ([medlineplus.gov](https://medlineplus.gov/genetics/condition/atelosteogenesis-type-3#:~:text=turning%20feet%20,About)). These axial skeletal defects contribute to the lethal respiratory complications and limited mobility associated with AO3. 

- **Craniofacial features:** Although primarily a bone disorder, AO3 has recognizable **facial dysmorphism** related to the underlying skeletal maldevelopment. Infants have a **prominent, broad forehead** and **widely spaced eyes** (**hypertelorism**) ([medlineplus.gov](https://medlineplus.gov/genetics/condition/atelosteogenesis-type-3#:~:text=broad%20fingers%20and%20toes%20that,a%20cleft%20palate)). The midface is flattened with a **depressed nasal bridge** and small nose, due to midfacial hypoplasia ([medlineplus.gov](https://medlineplus.gov/genetics/condition/atelosteogenesis-type-3#:~:text=broad%20fingers%20and%20toes%20that,a%20cleft%20palate)). Approximately half of patients have a **cleft palate** (an opening in the roof of the mouth) ([medlineplus.gov](https://medlineplus.gov/genetics/condition/atelosteogenesis-type-3#:~:text=broad%20fingers%20and%20toes%20that,a%20cleft%20palate)), indicating incomplete fusion of the palatal bones. These craniofacial findings, while not life-threatening, are often noted in conjunction with the skeletal abnormalities and can aid in diagnosis.  

**Radiographic hallmarks** of AO3 correlate with these phenotypes. X-rays typically show **“punctate” or delayed ossification** in many bones, **distally tapering humeri**, and **vertebral fusion or coronal clefting**, alongside the limb shortening and flared metaphyses ([www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK2534/#:~:text=FLNB,of%20significant%20laryngotracheobronchomalacia%2C%20the%20major)) ([www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/medgen/777149#:~:text=From%20OMIM%20Atelosteogenesis%20type%20III,Infants%20with%20AO3%20often%20have)). The combination of rhizomelic limb shortening, joint dislocations, and a narrow thorax on prenatal ultrasound or postnatal radiographs is highly indicative of AO3 ([www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK2534/#:~:text=%23%20FLNB,AO3)) ([medlineplus.gov](https://medlineplus.gov/genetics/condition/atelosteogenesis-type-3#:~:text=Individuals%20with%20atelosteogenesis%20type%203,are%20probably%20caused%20by%20low)). Genetic testing for *FLNB* mutations confirms the diagnosis and distinguishes AO3 from other skeletal dysplasias with overlapping features (such as Atelosteogenesis type II, which has a different genetic cause) ([www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK2534/#:~:text=match%20at%20L632%20%2A%20AO3,Rock%20et%20al%202006)) . 

## Prognosis and Current Insights  
**Prognosis for AO3 is poor**, given the critical respiratory issues from thoracic insufficiency. Most affected fetuses are either **stillborn or die in the neonatal period** due to respiratory failure despite intensive intervention ([medlineplus.gov](https://medlineplus.gov/genetics/condition/atelosteogenesis-type-3#:~:text=Individuals%20with%20atelosteogenesis%20type%203,are%20probably%20caused%20by%20low)). Cervical spine instability (from vertebral anomalies) can further complicate early life and contribute to mortality ([www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/medgen/777149#:~:text=with%20AO3%20have%20less%20delay,org%2Fentry%2F108721)). A small number of patients have survived beyond infancy with tracheostomies, ventilatory support, and multiple orthopedic surgeries; in these cases, **developmental milestones are often delayed**, partly due to prolonged hospitalization and limited mobility ([medlineplus.gov](https://medlineplus.gov/genetics/condition/atelosteogenesis-type-3#:~:text=intensive%20medical%20support,such%20as%20standing%20and%20walking)). Long-term survivors may show mild neurodevelopmental impairment, likely secondary to chronic hypoxia in infancy rather than a direct effect of the mutation ([medlineplus.gov](https://medlineplus.gov/genetics/condition/atelosteogenesis-type-3#:~:text=closing%2C%20short%20pauses%20in%20breathing,such%20as%20standing%20and%20walking)). There is no curative treatment for AO3 – management is supportive and symptomatic. Orthopedic interventions (casting or surgical correction of clubfoot, stabilization of dislocated joints, spinal decompression) have been reported on a case-by-case basis ([www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK2534/#:~:text=FLNB,cleft%20palate%20and%20hearing%20loss)), but the severity of the skeletal malformations limits the effectiveness of such measures. 

On the research front, **Atelosteogenesis III continues to provide insights into skeletal development**. The discovery of *FLNB* as the causative gene (first reported in 2004 ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/14991055/#:~:text=dominant%20Larsen%20syndrome%20,this%20ubiquitously%20expressed%20cytoskeletal%20protein))) revealed an unexpected role for the cytoskeleton in bone formation and joint morphogenesis. Ongoing studies are using animal models and cellular assays to unravel how mutant filamin B perturbs signaling pathways in chondrocytes ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10924170/#:~:text=Filamin%20B%20,A)). This not only deepens the understanding of AO3 pathophysiology but also sheds light on fundamental processes like vertebral segmentation and endochondral ossification that are relevant to many skeletal conditions. While AO3 itself is exceedingly rare, these mechanistic insights may inform future therapies for more common orthopedic disorders and improve genetic counseling for skeletal dysplasias. Continued documentation of AO3 cases in medical literature (with only a few dozen known, each case report is valuable) will further refine knowledge of its clinical spectrum and management outcomes ([medlineplus.gov](https://medlineplus.gov/genetics/condition/atelosteogenesis-type-3#:~:text=About%20two%20dozen%20affected%20individuals,have%20been%20identified)) ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/1442028/#:~:text=Atelosteogenesis%20type%203%3A%20the%20first,Affiliation)). Experts emphasize the importance of early diagnosis – often via prenatal imaging and molecular testing – to guide perinatal care and family planning in this devastating but illuminating genetic bone disorder ([www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK2534/#:~:text=match%20at%20L91%20FLNB,Some%20individuals%20diagnosed%20with)) ([medlineplus.gov](https://medlineplus.gov/genetics/condition/atelosteogenesis-type-3#:~:text=Inheritance)).
